Growth, Profit, Disruption, and Deeper Thinking - research notes

Source set: Growth Engineering; Remarkable Business Growth; Make Phenomenal Profits; Dare to Disrupt; Think Deeper.

Use these notes for business-facing research outputs, commercial white papers, business plans, growth strategy reports, knowledge products, and proposal-style recommendations.

1. Growth as a system

Growth should be analysed as a system, not a channel list. The system connects target customer, problem, positioning, offer, acquisition, activation, retention, referral, revenue, service delivery, data, and management cadence.

For research outputs, ask:
- What is the growth engine?
- Which behaviour or metric proves customer value?
- Which constraint blocks growth: demand, conversion, retention, price, delivery, trust, data, or execution?
- Who owns each growth lever?
- Which dashboard shows whether the system is improving?

2. Growth engineering for digital products

For SaaS, platforms, AI products, marketplaces, and apps, growth is partly an engineering discipline. Research should inspect event data, cohort behaviour, experiments, activation, conversion, retention, product-qualified leads, and revenue expansion.

Useful output elements:
- event taxonomy;
- activation definition;
- retention cohort view;
- experiment backlog;
- KPI tree;
- growth loop diagram;
- product analytics gaps.

3. Remarkable business growth

Remarkable businesses systemise before they optimise. Research should connect strategy, marketing, sales, delivery, service, systems, people, and metrics. Specialist positioning is stronger than broad "we serve everyone" positioning.

Business research should evaluate:
- positioning clarity;
- sales process repeatability;
- lead generation system;
- service and support as retention levers;
- dashboard coverage for revenue, marketing, sales, service, and customer success.

4. Profit improvement

Profit improves through multiple compounding levers: price, packaging, offer mix, conversion, retention, purchase frequency, margin, cost-to-serve, and customer experience. A 15 percent improvement across several levers is often more credible than one dramatic breakthrough.

Research outputs should separate:
- revenue growth from profit growth;
- gross margin from contribution margin;
- acquisition economics from retention economics;
- price tests from price opinions;
- cost reduction from value improvement.

5. Disruption and transformation

Disruption changes the basis of competition. It may change access, delivery, pricing, customer journey, service model, ownership, speed, convenience, or operating model. Research should distinguish true transformation from incremental improvement.

Transformation assessment areas:
- leadership readiness;
- culture and incentives;
- talent and execution capability;
- systems and data;
- customer experience;
- technology;
- milestone discipline;
- metrics and feedback loops.

6. Deeper thinking and creativity

Creative business research should not stop at the first plausible answer. It should challenge assumptions and force role reversal.

Use prompts such as:
- What assumption would make this recommendation fail?
- What would a competitor do if they read this?
- What would a customer reject as inconvenient or risky?
- What would a regulator, supplier, investor, or employee notice?
- What would we recommend with half the budget?
- What would make the current model obsolete?

7. Research productization

When turning research into sellable knowledge products, package the smallest useful decision aid first. Strong candidates include:
- growth engine diagnostic;
- revenue leak audit;
- product-led growth scorecard;
- pricing and packaging experiment plan;
- disruption readiness assessment;
- customer retention dashboard;
- growth experiment backlog.

Each product should define audience, pain, decision supported, data required, output format, refresh cadence, and follow-on offer.
