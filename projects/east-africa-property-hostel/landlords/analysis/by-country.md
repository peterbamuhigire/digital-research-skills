# Landlord-side: country-by-country breakdown

A maturity gradient runs across the region — Kenya most institutionalised, Burundi / South Sudan least. The dominant pain shifts as the market formalises.

## Kenya — most mature, eviction-clogged

**Market shape:** Largest formal sector. Real platforms (BuyRent, Property24, Hass index, Cytonn quarterly). Functioning Rent Tribunal. CRBs available. Mortgage penetration 3.6% — still low globally, highest regionally.

**Top pain:** **7–11-month eviction delays** in magistrate court. Default-recovery is the single most-cited landlord complaint.

**Defining laws:**
- Rent Restriction Act 1982 (90-day notice, 1×/year cap)
- Landlord-Tenant Bill 2021 — still pending as of 2026
- Distress for Rent Act — operative; auctioneer model with 20–30% fees
- Rental Income Tax 7.5% (KSh 280K–15m / year); monthly iTax filing
- Nairobi land rates 0.115% property + 1% unimproved site value

**Recent precedent:**
- *Tribunal Case E007 of 2024* — 60-day deposit refund mandate
- *Muhanda v LP Holdings (2025)* — Small Claims Court has deposit-refund jurisdiction

**Currency exposure:** KSh -26.8% vs USD 2022–23 → +30–50% material costs.

**Opportunity:** PropTech still nascent (<5% CRB adoption); Tribunal improving but backlogged; Small Claims Court is now a fast deposit-refund lever.

## Uganda — rapidly formalising

**Market shape:** Real Muloodi pioneers zero-commission listings; AREA Uganda MLS expanding. Mobile-money penetration high. Mortgage penetration <3.6%.

**Top pain:** Refugee / migrant tenant discrimination is endemic; informal economy still dominates.

**Defining laws:**
- **Landlord & Tenant Act 2022** — written agreements >UGX 500k; 30-day non-payment notice; **distress for rent abolished**; 3 months' rent compensation for unlawful eviction
- No tribunal — Magistrate Courts only; backlog 4–8 months; High Court 12–18 months
- KCCA Property Rates 6% rateable value
- URA Rental Income Tax + Local Hotel Tax + VAT (above UGX 150m)

**Recent precedent:**
- *Komakech & 7 Ors v Ayaa & Anor (2018)* — eviction requires court order; forcible removal unlawful even after lease expiry; statutory tenancy protects holdover

**Opportunity:** L&T Act provides framework that compliance products can sit on; mobile money makes rent-collection automation easy.

## Tanzania — unregulated, dalali-dominant

**Market shape:** *Dalalis* (informal brokers) dominate. Government Nov 2021 ban on the 1-month-rent broker fee paid by tenants is **not enforced**. PropTech blue ocean.

**Top pain:** Unregulated broker layer. Currency depreciation. Arbitrary rent escalation with no statutory cap.

**Defining laws:**
- Rent Restriction Act 1984 — *repealed in practice*
- Land Act 1999 — abolished distress; requires summary judgment
- "Private matter" doctrine; courts 8–12 months

**Recent move:** Government regulatory body announced March 2025 — direction of travel towards regulation but framework not yet in force.

**Opportunity:** Greenfield for any platform that can establish standards before formal regulation arrives.

## Rwanda — small, fast, formal

**Market shape:** Emerging formal sector; expat-driven demand around Kacyiru, Nyarutarama, Kimihurura.

**Top pain:** Limited mortgage financing; expat preference bias against locals.

**Defining laws:** Written leases mandated; **fastest courts in EA at 3–5 months**.

**Opportunity:** PropTech underserved; government housing support active; the 3–5 month court window is a genuine competitive advantage versus EA peers.

## Burundi — crisis-driven

**Market shape:** Rent inflation has doubled rents in <12 months in some Bujumbura segments. Tenant affordability collapse.

**Top pain:** Affordability collapse; no legal framework.

**Recent move:** Regulatory development announced 2025.

**Opportunity:** Limited near-term, but post-stabilisation a regulatory anchor + currency-stable rent platform could lead.

## South Sudan (Juba) — conflict-driven, bifurcated

**Market shape:** Luxury (international staff) versus low-income, with land rights precarious in both segments.

**Top pain:** Land disputes, infrastructure scarcity, currency volatility.

**Opportunity:** Long-tail post-conflict reconstruction. Institutional investor interest possible if security stabilises.

## Cross-country pattern

The same five pains rank top-three in every country with sufficient data:

1. Eviction delays (severity scales inversely with court speed: RW < KE < UG < TZ)
2. Tenant-screening blindness (universal)
3. Vacancy & turnover cost (universal)
4. Currency / cost inflation (universal — even Rwanda's stable franc faces import costs)
5. Regulatory uncertainty / change

The countries diverge on:

- **Regulatory maturity:** UG (most articulated) > KE (court infrastructure) > RW (formal but small) > TZ > BI / SS
- **PropTech maturity:** KE > UG > RW > TZ > BI / SS
- **Court speed:** RW (3–5m) > UG magistrate (4–8m) > KE (7–11m) > TZ (8–12m) > UG High Court (12–18m)
- **Mobile-money rent share:** KE highest > UG > RW ≈ TZ > BI > SS

**Strategic implication for any cross-border product:** Start with **Kenya + Uganda**. They have the deepest pain documentation, highest mobile-money penetration, and most enforceable legal frameworks to build on.
