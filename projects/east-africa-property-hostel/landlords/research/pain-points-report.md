# East African Residential Rental Landlords — Pain Points Report

Compiled 2026-04-25 from 60+ sources spanning news media, industry reports (Hass Consult, Cytonn, Knight Frank, CAHF), legal databases (Kenyalaw, ULII), regulatory bodies (KRA, NEMA, KCCA), PropTech platforms, and academic repositories.

Scope: owners and managers of **general residential rentals** — bungalows, apartments, flats, "self-contained" units, single rooms / muzigos, small unit blocks, mid-tier complexes, commercial-residential mixes. **Student hostels are covered separately in `owners/`.**

## Executive summary

East African residential landlords operate in a fragmented, cash-dominant, lightly-institutionalised market. Five things hurt the most:

1. **Eviction delays** — 6–11 months Kenya/Uganda; 8–12 months Tanzania
2. **Tenant-screening blindness** — <5% CRB adoption; informal references dominate
3. **Vacancy & turnover** — 8–14 weeks vacancy in mid-income Nairobi; KSh 30–80k turnover cost per unit
4. **Maintenance grind** — pests, utilities, ageing infrastructure drain 5–10% of income
5. **Regulatory complexity** — Uganda L&T Act 2022 reshaped landlord obligations; Kenya Bill 2021 still pending; tax stack growing

The market is most mature in Kenya, rapidly formalising in Uganda, unregulated-ish in Tanzania, fast but small in Rwanda, and crisis-driven in Burundi / South Sudan.

## Part 1 — Finding tenants

### Time-on-market (typical)

| Market | Time-to-let |
|---|---|
| Kenya prime (Westlands, Kilimani) | 4–6 weeks |
| Mid-income Nairobi | 8–14 weeks |
| Mombasa | 12–16 weeks (yields 6–8%) |
| Kampala secondary | 10–20 weeks (softened H2 2024) |

### Brokers

- **Kenya:** 1 month rent commission (paid by tenant or landlord, varies)
- **Uganda:** Real Muloodi pioneers zero-commission; AREA Uganda MLS expanding
- **Tanzania:** Unregulated *dalalis* dominant; Nov 2021 government ban on the 1-month-rent broker fee paid by tenants is **not enforced**

### Online platforms — fragmented and friction-laden

| Platform | Coverage | Model | Friction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Property24 KE | 5,000+ listings | Freemium | Stale listings not removed |
| BuyRentKenya | 7,500+ | Free | Limited filters |
| Real Muloodi (UG) | 2,000–3,000 | Free for 30 days | Low cross-border reach |
| Lamudi | 800,000+ regional | Freemium | Cross-country dilution |
| Nyumba Zetu | n/a | SaaS + M-Pesa rent | ~2,000–5,000 users only |

No single platform has dominant share. Word-of-mouth, signage, Facebook Marketplace, and WhatsApp groups still drive most informal-segment lettings.

## Part 2 — Tenant screening

### ID & document verification

National-ID counterfeiting persists in Kenya and Uganda. There is no centralised real-time validation service for landlords.

### Credit Reference Bureaux (Kenya)

| Bureau | Cost / report | Landlord adoption |
|---|---|---|
| Metropol CRB (April 2011) | KSh 500–2,000 | <5% |
| TransUnion Kenya (Feb 2010) | KSh 1,000–3,000 | <3% |
| Creditinfo Kenya | KSh 800–2,500 | <2% |

**Why so low:** cost-prohibitive for small landlords; informal references preferred; CRB scope is loan repayment history, not rental.

### Reference culture

- Employer letters (forged easily)
- Prior-landlord references (unreliable; outgoing landlord may fabricate good reviews to push out problem tenants)
- Guarantor letters (informal; rarely enforceable)

### "Bad-tenant" networks

- Kenya: landlord WhatsApp / Telegram groups share unverified blacklists. Effective but discriminatory and legally exposed under Data Protection Act 2019.
- Uganda: similar but less organised
- Tanzania / Rwanda: minimal documented activity

### Discrimination patterns

| Country | Documented bias against |
|---|---|
| Kenya (UTAK reports) | Tribe (Somali, Nubian); nationality (Nigerian, Congolese); religion (Muslim); occupation (sex workers); marital status |
| Uganda | Refugees & migrants systematically underserved; religious minorities filtered |
| Tanzania | Nationality barriers for migrants |
| Rwanda | Expat preference; locals face subtle barriers |

Constitutional protections exist (Kenya Art. 27, Uganda Arts. 21 & 42) but enforcement reaches under 10% of cases.

## Part 3 — Rent collection & defaults

### Payment-method mix (Kenya, urban)

- M-Pesa / mobile money: 60–70%
- Cash: 25–30% (informal settlements)
- PesaLink: <15%
- Cheques: <5%

### PropTech rent platforms

|---|---|---|---|

| Lipa Na M-Pesa Bills | KE | Active | Moderate corporate |

### Default rates (anecdotal — no published statistics)

- Kenya: eviction applications surged 2024–25; 10–25% annual default in informal zones
- Uganda: rising post-2022 Act; specifics unreported
- Tanzania / Rwanda: 15–20% urban estimate

### Eviction timelines — the central operational pain

| Country | Filing → order | Execution | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kenya magistrate | 6–9 months | 2–4 weeks | 7–11 months |
| Uganda magistrate | 4–8 months | — | — |
| Uganda High Court | 12–18 months | — | — |
| Tanzania | 8–12 months typical | — | — |
| Rwanda | 3–5 months (fastest in EA) | — | — |

### Rent-escalation disputes

- **Kenya:** Rent Restriction Act caps once-per-year; 90-day notice. ~30% of disputes go to Rent Tribunal (4–6 month backlog).
- **Uganda L&T Act 2022:** 90-day written notice required.
- **Tanzania:** No regulation; arbitrary increases displace tenants.

## Part 4 — Property maintenance

### Pest infestation — the single most-cited operational nuisance

| Pest / region | Detail | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Bedbugs (Mombasa) | Endemic; warm climate | KSh 5,000–25,000 / unit fumigation; 2–4 ×/year |
| Cockroaches & rats (Kampala / Nairobi) | Density-driven | Continuous spend |
| Termites | Structural damage | KSh 200,000–2m repair |

Pest control (Arrow, Jopestkil, PestPro) typically eats **2–5% of rental income**.

### Utility outages

- KPLC: 2–4 outages / month, 2–8 hours
- UMEME: similar frequency
- TANESCO: more frequent, 6–12 hours
- Water: Nairobi 5–7 day disruptions; landlords cannot recover rent reductions tenants demand

### Caretaker / askari management

- Wages: KSh 5,000–12,000/month Nairobi; UGX 50,000–150,000 Kampala
- Turnover: 6–12 months average
- Theft: 15–25% of blocks affected per informal landlord surveys
- Weak legal recovery framework against caretaker abuse / theft

### Other maintenance reality

- Plumbing: KSh 3,000–15,000/call; 1–2 incidents per 5-unit block per year
- Electrical: KSh 300,000–1m rewiring on aging stock
- Roofing: KSh 200,000–800,000 per unit at the 8–15 year replacement cycle

### Insurance

<1% penetration for residential landlords. Uninsured losses 5–10% annually.

## Part 5 — Regulatory issues

### Kenya

- **Rent Restriction Act 1982** — 90-day notice, 1×/year increase
- **Landlord-Tenant Bill 2021** — pending enactment as of 2026; proposed 30-day non-payment notice, prohibition on self-help
- **Distress for Rent Act** — still operative; auctioneer can seize without court order
- **Rental Income Tax** — 7.5% on gross rent (KSh 280K–15M / year); monthly iTax filing
- **Land rates Nairobi** — 0.115% property value + 1% unimproved site value
- **Permits:** building KSh 50K–500K (4–12 weeks); change of use KSh 20K–150K (6–16 weeks); NEMA EIA for >3 storeys, KSh 500K–2m, 8–16 weeks

### Uganda

- **Landlord & Tenant Act 2022** — written agreements above UGX 500K; 30-day non-payment notice; **distress for rent abolished**; 3-months-rent compensation for unlawful eviction
- No tribunal — courts only; backlog 4–8 months
- Property tax — KCCA 6% rateable value (Kampala)

### Tanzania

- **Rent Restriction Act 1984 — repealed in practice.** No regulation.
- **Land Act 1999** — abolished distress; requires summary judgment
- "Private matter" doctrine; court delays 8–12 months

### Rwanda

- Written leases mandated; fast courts (3–5 months); formal rental framework

## Part 6 — Financing & investment pain

### Mortgage penetration

- **Kenya: 3.6%** (lowest in region)
- Uganda, Tanzania even lower

### Interest rates

- Kenya: 12–17% mortgage; +0.25–0.875% premium for investment property
- Uganda: 15–22% commercial
- Tanzania: limited products; FX risk high

### Build vs buy (Nairobi 2023–24)

- **Buy** 2-bed: KSh 8–15m → 6–8% gross yield
- **Build** 2-bed: land + construction KSh 10–20m, 12–18 months → 3–5% net after interest/taxes
- **Verdict:** buying preferred; building only viable for developers with scale

### Currency risk

- KSh -26.8% vs USD (2022–23)
- Construction material costs +30–50%
- Landlords cannot pass through all costs; margins compress
- UGX, TZS depreciation patterns similar

### Vacancy cash-flow impact

A 2-bed Nairobi at KSh 60,000/month with 8–14 week vacancy:
- Lost rent: KSh 120,000–210,000
- Turnover cost (repaint, clean, repairs): KSh 30,000–80,000
- **Total: 3–5% annual return erosion per turnover**

## Part 7 — Disputes & legal complexity

### Deposit-refund disputes (Kenya)

Landmark 2025 ruling (**Muhanda v LP Holdings**) — Small Claims Court now has jurisdiction over deposit refunds. Previously indefinite withholding was possible. Now: 60-day resolution target. Common cause of dispute: inflated damage claims; landlords lack photo documentation.

### Distress for Rent (Kenya)

Still operative. Seizure without court order via licensed auctioneer. Auctioneer fees (20–30%) often exceed recovered rent. Rarely used in practice — too complex.

### Holdover tenants

Formal eviction required (6+ months) across all jurisdictions. No self-help permitted.

### Tenant death / domestic violence

No clear legal framework. Police intervention required. Landlord liability uncertain.

## Part 8 — Problematic tenant categories landlords specifically name

- **Caretaker tenants (sub-letters)** — over-occupancy, unauthorised alterations, opaque rent collection, eviction complexity (multiple sub-tenants). Common in Nairobi informal zones.
- **Pastors / religious leaders converting flats to churches** — documented in Kampala and Nairobi. Noise, fire-safety violations, occupancy code violations. Constitutional conflict (religious freedom vs property rights). Cases stall.
- **Bar / lounge conversions** — Nairobi CBD, Mombasa. Late-night traffic, security risks, code violations. Eviction difficult (on-time payments + political connections). Council enforcement inconsistent.
- **Election-cycle politicised mass evictions / occupations** — particularly Nairobi informal settlements during election campaigns.

### Tanzania

- **Unregulated** ("private matter") with *dalali* dominance

### Rwanda

- **Emerging** formal sector; expat-driven demand
- **Top pain:** limited mortgage financing; expat-preference bias
- **Opportunity:** fastest dispute resolution in EA; underserved PropTech market

### Burundi

- **Crisis-driven** market; rent inflation doubling rents in <12 months
- **Top pain:** affordability collapse; no legal framework
- **Opportunity:** regulatory development announced 2025

### South Sudan (Juba)

- **Conflict-driven** with luxury / low-income bifurcation; precarious land rights
- **Top pain:** land disputes, infrastructure scarcity
- **Opportunity:** post-conflict reconstruction; institutional investor interest possible

## P2.1 Kenya court records — landlord-tenant precedent (kenyalaw.org)

Two named cases retrieved with full citations. These confirm BPRT (Business Premises Rent Tribunal) procedural tightening in 2025.

**Horientetertainment Limited v Maina** — Tribunal Case E1278 of 2024 [2025] KEBPRT 265 (KLR), 29 April 2025.
- Property: Lavington, Nairobi (Kuria Close)
- Rent escalated from KSh 65,000 (2018) → KSh 400,000 (2024)
- Eviction struck down for "no evidence of Notice of Termination"
- Tribunal ordered rent reconciliation with 60-day cure period before distress allowed
- Source: [kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/kebprt/2025/265](https://new.kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/kebprt/2025/265/eng@2025-04-29)

**Munyingi v Thuo** — Tribunal Case E105 of 2024 [2025] KEBPRT 425 (KLR), 9 October 2025.
- Business premises, Nyeri
- Landlord claimed KSh 1,010,480 arrears
- Tribunal denied eviction; required parties to reconcile ledgers in 30 days, then 60-day pay-up before distress
- Source: [kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/kebprt/2025/425](https://new.kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/kebprt/2025/425/eng@2025-10-09)

**Pattern.** Q1–Q4 2025 BPRT decisions consistently strike down invalid termination notices and require reconciliation processes before eviction or distress. **Kenya eviction timeline is therefore likely longer than the 7–11 month Wave-1 estimate** — add reconciliation + cure periods (typically +60–90 days).

## P2.2 Kenya eRITS — Electronic Rental Income Tax System (April 2025)

The single largest new compliance burden surfaced in Wave 2. Not in Wave 1.

- **Launched:** April 2025 by KRA
- **Compliance deadline:** 26 September 2025
- **Tax:** 7.5% Monthly Rental Income (MRI), final
- **Threshold:** KSh 288,000–15M annual rent
- **Filing:** Monthly, by the 20th of the following month
- **Registration:** Properties + tenant PINs
- **Penalties:** Fines, backdated claims, frozen bank accounts, court cases
- **Tax-gap target:** KSh 17bn collected vs KSh 100bn+ potential
- Sources: [EY Tax News](https://taxnews.ey.com/news/2025-1841-kenya-revenue-authority-kra-launches-erits-to-enhance-rental-income-tax-compliance), [erits.kra.go.ke](https://erits.kra.go.ke/)

**Implication for landlords:** Monthly filing, audit-trail PII registration, frozen-account risk for non-compliers. Likely accelerates PropTech adoption for compliance reasons.

## P2.3 Tanzania Use of Foreign Currency Regulations 2025

Major macro change with direct landlord impact, not Wave-1 documented.

- **GN No. 198, 28 March 2025**
- All goods and services must now be priced in TZS
- Existing foreign-currency contracts must be amended within 12 months or become void
- Sources: [Clyde & Co](https://www.clydeco.com/en/insights/2025/03/new-regulations-on-foreign-currency-use-tanzania), [PwC Tanzania](https://www.pwc.co.tz/press-room/regulations-on-foreign-currency.html)

**Implication for landlords:** USD-denominated leases (common in expat segments — Mikocheni, Masaki, Oyster Bay) face renegotiation or void risk. The Wave-1 search target of "dalali regulatory body announced March 2025" was not located — this Foreign Currency Regulation appears to have been the actual March 2025 development.

## P2.4 NEMA Uganda Lubigi Wetland demolitions (2024–25) — environmental enforcement as landlord risk

A new landlord-risk vector not in Wave 1.

- 2024–25: NEMA conducted sweeping Lubigi Wetland demolitions
- 37,000+ residents threatened in Katoogo Zone alone
- Some held valid land titles but faced demolition without DEMC (District Environment Management Committee) process or 3-month notice
- KCCA filed legal challenge alleging NEMA violated NEMA Act 2019 procedure
- Sources: [Daily Monitor](https://www.monitor.co.ug/uganda/news/national/city-authorities-accuse-nema-of-unlawful-evictions-4667710), [allAfrica](https://allafrica.com/stories/202406240119.html)

**Implication for landlords:** Environmental enforcement now sits alongside court eviction as a structural risk. Unlike court delays, NEMA demolitions can be summary (insufficient notice, no DEMC process). Holders of properties on or near wetlands face existential risk.

## P2.10 Uganda rental income tax detail (clarification)

- Individuals: **12% flat tax on rent above UGX 2,820,000 annual threshold**
- No expense deductions for individuals
- Corporate: up to 50% expense deduction allowed; 30% tax on net
- Source: [PwC Uganda Tax Summary](https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/uganda/individual/taxes-on-personal-income), [Real Muloodi rental-tax guide](https://realmuloodi.co.ug/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/RENTAL-INCOME-A-Simplified-Guide.pdf)
