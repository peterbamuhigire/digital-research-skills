# Product opportunities

Gaps the research surfaces, framed as hypotheses to test — not as a roadmap. Each idea names the pain it serves, who feels it, and the obvious failure modes.

## 1. Verified-listing marketplace with anti-scam guarantees

**Pain served:** A1 (fresher scams), A4 (no transparent listings), A3 (broker exploitation)

**Hypothesis:** A marketplace where every hostel is physically verified by a local agent, with photos, video tour, owner KYC, and student reviews — solving the "is this even real?" problem that drives the Sh2m doubler scams.

**Existing players:** Jiunge (Kenya), Katalyma (Ghana/EA), Hostelconnect. None has dominant share in Uganda; thin in Tanzania/Rwanda.

**Why it might fail:**
- Two-sided market cold start — landlords need students, students need landlords
- Brokers will fight it (their commissions are the moat)
- University students transact 1–2 times a year; LTV is low
- Listings get stale fast; verification is operationally expensive

**Test cheaply:** Manual WhatsApp concierge for 50 Makerere freshers next intake. Charge nothing. See if word of mouth spreads.

## 2. Deposit escrow + auto-refund

**Pain served:** B9 (deposit theft — 80% non-refund in Uganda)

**Hypothesis:** A neutral escrow holds the deposit. At checkout, a structured inspection (photo evidence) determines refund. Disputes go to a panel, not the landlord's pocket.

**Why it's promising:** The 80% statistic is the most striking single finding in the research. Students lose hundreds of thousands of shillings collectively every semester. The Landlord & Tenant Act 2022 already mandates 30-day itemized refunds — a product can productize the law.

**Why it might fail:**
- Landlords won't opt in voluntarily — needs aggregator pressure or regulator backing
- Mobile money escrow regulations vary by country
- Trust in the escrow operator must exceed trust in the landlord — high bar

**Test cheaply:** Partner with one Makerere-area hostel that wants to differentiate. Run for one cohort. Measure deposit-return rate vs. baseline.

## 3. Hostel rating & complaint hotline (the "Glassdoor for hostels")

**Pain served:** B1–B12 (all living-condition complaints), accountability gap

**Hypothesis:** Anonymous, verified-student review platform where complaints publicly aggregate. Bad management gets exposed; good management gets discovered.

**Why it might fail:**
- Defamation risk in jurisdictions with weak journalism protections
- Hostels can game with fake reviews
- Without enforcement teeth, ratings change nothing

**Adjacent existing model:** Campus Bee partly plays this role for Uganda but is editorial, not crowdsourced.

## 4. Group-buy / co-living for first-years

**Pain served:** A2 (capacity shortage), A1 (vulnerability of first-years), B7 (caretaker harassment of isolated students)

**Hypothesis:** Pre-form groups of 4–6 first-years (matched on study programme, gender preference, budget) and negotiate block leases on quality stock. Students arrive into pre-vetted housing with peers.

**Why it might fail:**
- Matching at scale is hard — Erasmus-style apps haven't cracked it in EA
- Universities won't endorse a third-party doing what they should be doing
- Block-lease landlords expect bigger discounts than groups can deliver

## 5. Female-only verified hostels with safety certification

**Pain served:** B7 (sexual harassment), B6 (security), A6 (off-campus risk)

**Hypothesis:** Certify a network of female-only hostels meeting a published safety standard (vetted caretakers with background checks, 24/7 staff, CCTV at entry, panic button, rapid-response partnership with university security).

**Why it's promising:** Mzumbe University already proposed a 1,200-bed female hostel — institutional appetite exists. Parents pay premium for verified safety.

**Why it might fail:**
- Background checks are weak in EA — KYC infrastructure isn't there
- "Certified safe" claim creates legal liability if anything happens
- Landlords resist standards that increase opex

## 6. Bedbug & sanitation inspection-as-a-service

**Pain served:** B1 (sanitation), B4 (bedbugs)

**Hypothesis:** A pest/sanitation specialist offers paid inspections + treatment contracts to hostels, with a published certification that students can check.

**Why it might fail:**
- Hostels won't pay unless students demand the certificate first — chicken-and-egg
- Treatment efficacy depends on neighbour buildings (bedbugs spread via people)

## 7. Pre-paid utility bundle (water, power tokens, WiFi)

**Pain served:** B2 (water), B3 (power), B11 (internet), B12 (hidden costs)

**Hypothesis:** Students prepay a fixed monthly utility bundle to a third party that delivers reliability — backup water tank, solar-backup, dedicated WiFi mesh — bypassing the hostel's broken infrastructure.

**Why it might fail:**
- Capex per building is high
- Landlords block third-party infrastructure
- Cheaper to switch hostel than fix utilities

## Strategic notes

**The cheapest first move** is research, not building. Most ideas above need 50–100 student interviews before any product decision. The student union at Makerere or UoN is the highest-leverage starting point.

**The hardest problem** is not finding pain — the report shows it everywhere. The hard problem is **monetization**: students transact infrequently, are price-sensitive, and the landlord side has weak willingness-to-pay for tooling. Any business model probably needs a B2B angle (universities, parents, government, NGOs, payment processors) rather than student-facing fees.

**The most defensible single idea** is probably **#2 (deposit escrow)** — it has a sharp legal hook (Landlord & Tenant Act 2022), a quantified pain (80% non-refund), and a clear willingness-to-pay (students already lose Sh hundreds of thousands).

**The most viral single idea** is probably **#3 (rating platform)** — students share complaints freely on TikTok and Twitter; channelling that into a structured product creates compounding content moat.

## Next step

Pick one of these and run a 2-week discovery sprint: 20 student interviews, 5 landlord interviews, 2 university administrator interviews. Decide go/no-go on evidence, not enthusiasm.
