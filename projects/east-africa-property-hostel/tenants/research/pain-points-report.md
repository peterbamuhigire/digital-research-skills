# East African Residential Tenants — Pain Points Report

Compiled 2026-04-25 from 75+ sources spanning Daily Monitor, Daily Nation, Business Daily, The Standard, The Citizen, New Times, Cytonn, Hass Consult, KNBS, ULII, Kenyalaw, UN-Habitat, IGC, Hakijamii, Justice Centres Uganda, Muungano wa Wanavijiji, CAHF, and academic journals.

Scope: ordinary residential tenants — working adults, families, young singles, retirees — renting bungalows, apartments, flats, "self-contained" units, single rooms / *muzigos*, boys' quarters, condos. **Students and hostel residents are covered separately in `students/`.**

## Executive summary

Tenants across East Africa face two interlocking failures: **a regulatory framework that exists on paper but is not enforced**, and **information asymmetry** that lets brokers, landlords, and caretakers exploit tenants who don't know their rights. The single most cited statistic — **80% of Ugandan tenants never get their deposit back even when leaving the unit in good condition** — captures the dynamic in one number.

Top headline pains:

1. **Deposit theft** — Uganda 80% non-refund despite 2022 law mandating 30-day refund
2. **Broker scams** — 30% of Uganda renters encounter fake listings; viewing-fee extortion universal
3. **Discrimination** — single women face ~90% rejection rate vs single men; refugees, divorced mothers, persons with disabilities systematically excluded
4. **Utility failures** — Nairobi 250M-litre daily water deficit; Kampala 60M; Dar 9 hours/day supply; tenants pay full rent for half-service
5. **Eviction without due process** — distress-for-rent banned in Uganda 2022 but lockouts, furniture removal, power-cuts continue; Nairobi 2024 riparian demolitions displaced 20,000+ with KSh 10,000 compensation
6. **Awareness gap** — fewer than 30% of tenants know their rights under Uganda L&T Act 2022 or Kenya L&T Bill 2021

## Part 1 — Finding a place to rent

### Brokers, viewing fees, fake listings

- **Commission:** one month's rent standard across Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania
- **Viewing fees:** KSh 500–2,000 (Kenya); UGX 10,000+ (Uganda) per property — paid up front, often nothing materialises
- **Fake listings:** ~30% of Uganda renters encounter at least one fake ad (Spectrum Real Estate Solutions). Stolen photos, fake landlords, multi-deposit collection
- **Bait-and-switch:** prices on Property24 / BuyRent / Lamudi differ from on-the-ground reality

### Platforms tenants use

- BuyRentKenya, Property24 (KE)
- Real Muloodi, Buy Rent Uganda, RealEstateUganda (UG)
- Lamudi (regional)
- Facebook Marketplace, WhatsApp groups, signage on lamp-posts
- Word-of-mouth still drives most informal-segment lettings

### House-hunting fatigue

Tenants commonly view 6–12 properties before settling. With viewing fees and broker time, the search itself can cost the equivalent of 1–2 months' rent before they even sign.

## Part 2 — Deposit & key-money issues

**Uganda — the 80% statistic.** Daily Monitor August 2023: "80% of tenants have never gotten a refund of their security deposit from their previous landlords even when the house has been left in good condition. They either get apologies or half of it."

**Uganda Landlord & Tenant Act 2022:**
- Maximum deposit = **one month's rent**
- 30-day refund mandate, with itemised deductions
- Widely ignored in practice — landlords cite vague "damages," "unauthorised renovations," "maintenance charges," or simply disappear

**Kenya:**
- Tribunal Case E007 of 2024 mandates **60-day deposit refund** under CAP 301
- *Muhanda v LP Holdings (2025)* gave Small Claims Court jurisdiction
- Enforcement still weak; most tenants don't pursue formal claims

### Tenant counter-strategies

- **"Eating the deposit"** — withholding the final month's rent in lieu of the deposit. Risk: landlord can sue for both rent AND keep the deposit if tenant cannot prove unit was returned in good order.
- Refusing to leave until refunded — exposes tenant to forcible eviction.
- Going to Tribunal / Small Claims Court — Kenya only; under-utilised; awareness low.

## Part 3 — Rent increases & price exploitation

| Jurisdiction | Statutory cap | Notice required | Enforcement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Uganda L&T Act 2022 | 10% / year | 90 days | Largely ignored |
| Kenya L&T Bill 2021 | Inflation-cap (proposed) | 90 days | Bill not yet enacted |
| Kenya Rent Restriction Act | 1×/year | 90 days | Enforced via Tribunal — backlogged |
| Tanzania | None | None | Arbitrary increases routine |
| Rwanda | Per written lease | Per lease | Generally complied with |

### Hike patterns documented

- **Mid-lease hikes** — written contracts violated; landlord demands new rate or eviction
- **Currency-pegged hikes** — upmarket Nairobi / Kampala rents pegged to USD; KSh / UGX depreciation passed to tenant
- **Election-cycle gouging** — Nairobi 2022, Kampala 2021 saw cluster rent hikes
- **Construction-cycle hikes** — new infrastructure (Express Highway, BRT corridors) drives 10–25% rent jumps in adjacent areas
- **Soft-eviction tool** — landlord names a hike they know tenant won't pay, to clear unit for higher-paying replacement

## Part 4 — Living conditions

### Pests

Bedbugs and cockroaches are **endemic** in Nairobi (Eastlands, Kasarani, Kayole), Mombasa (Tudor, Bamburi), Kampala *muzigos* (Bwaise, Makerere area, Bunamwaya). Health costs (allergies, disease vectors) and dignity costs are real. Tenants often pay for fumigation themselves.

### Water

- **Nairobi NCWSC:** 250 million litre / day supply deficit
- **Kampala NWSC:** 60 million litre daily shortage
- **Dar DAWASA:** **24-hour shutoffs on alternate days** (Wave-1 figure of "9 hr/day" corrected per *EastAfrican* current reporting); ~100M-litre/day deficit. Source: [The EastAfrican](https://www.theeastafrican.co.ke/tea/news/east-africa/water-crisis-dar-es-salaam-tanzania-3624990)
- **Kigali WASAC:** intermittent, more reliable than peers but tightening

Tenants pay full rent for half-service. No regulatory framework lets them claim rent reduction for utility failure.

### Power

- KPLC, UMEME, TANESCO outages 2–4× / month (KE / UG); more frequent in TZ
- Tenants forced into KSh 200–600 / day generator fuel or KSh 5,000+ / month inverter+solar
- KPLC token scams — tenants pay for tokens that don't load

### Internet

Safaricom Home Fibre, MTN Home, Liquid Telecom, Faiba (KE), Vodacom (TZ) — single GPON line shared 32–64 households causes evening buffering. No tenant remedy.

### Building condition

- Roof leaks, cracked walls, broken gates / fences
- Mould, dampness in older Mombasa / coastal stock
- Cooking-gas safety hazards in cramped *muzigos*
- Lifts broken for weeks in apartment blocks; landlord delays repairs while collecting full rent

## Part 5 — Caretaker / askari problems

The figure who manages the building day-to-day is a primary friction point for tenants:

- **Unlawful entry** without notice for "inspections"
- **Bribery for repairs** — tenant pays rent + a side payment for the caretaker to actually fix the leak
- **Espionage** — caretaker reports tenant routines, guests, late-night arrivals to landlord
- **Privacy violations** — Daily Nation has documented cases of caretakers entering units, in one case requesting sexual favours
- **Side-payment "side rent"** — caretaker collects unofficial fees for parking, water access, security entry
- **Discrimination by caretaker** — in some buildings the caretaker is the de-facto gatekeeper deciding who gets shown which units

## Part 6 — Eviction & dispute

### Distress for rent

- **Uganda 2022:** banned. Self-help eviction (lockout, furniture removal, power-cut) carries 3 months' rent compensation + damages liability for the landlord. Yet practice continues — most tenants don't file claims.
- **Kenya:** Distress for Rent Act still operative. Auctioneer can seize without court order. Auctioneer fees (20–30%) often exceed recovered rent, so landlords use it sparingly.
- **Tanzania:** Land Act 1999 abolished distress; requires summary judgment.

### Court process

- Kenya: 30/60-day notices; magistrate court + Rent Tribunal
- Uganda: Magistrate Court only (no Tribunal); 4–8 months
- Rwanda: 3–5 months, fastest in EA

### Mass evictions — political dimension

- **Nairobi 2024 riparian demolitions:** **40,000 households** affected (Interior Ministry directive May 2024; 30m river-corridor demolitions Nairobi/Ngong/Mathare). Compensation **KSh 10,000 per household** (≥25,000 received support by 27 May 2024; 40,000 households verified). Sources: [Africanews](https://www.africanews.com/2024/05/07/kenya-continues-forceful-demolition-of-nairobi-informal-settlements-near-rivers/), [Capital News](https://www.capitalfm.co.ke/news/2024/05/nearly-10000-households-on-riparian-corridors-receive-sh10000-stipend/). Pretext: flood mitigation; effect: forced relocation without due process. *(Wave-1 figure of 20,000 corrected to 40,000 per Wave-2 verification.)*
- **Election-cycle mass evictions** — Nairobi 2022 informal settlements; Kampala 2021 Kasubi area. Landlords use election uncertainty as cover for legally questionable eviction.
- **Kampala KCCA road / drainage projects** — ad-hoc demolitions with limited compensation.

### Landmark cases

- *Komakech & 7 Ors v Ayaa & Anor* (Uganda 2018) — eviction requires court order
- *Tribunal Case E007 of 2024* (Kenya) — 60-day deposit refund
- *Muhanda v LP Holdings* (Kenya 2025) — Small Claims Court jurisdiction over deposits

## Part 7 — Tenant rights awareness & enforcement

**Awareness:** Fewer than **30%** of EA tenants know their rights under the relevant statute. Most landlord behaviour goes unchallenged because the tenant simply doesn't know it's unlawful.

**Legal-aid landscape:**

- **Hakijamii (Kenya)** — slum housing rights, evictions, legal clinics
- **Justice Centres Uganda** — district-level legal aid including tenant disputes
- **Kenya Legal Aid** — limited rental coverage
- **Slum Dwellers International / Akiba Mashinani Trust** — savings-group + advocacy in informal settlements
- **Muungano wa Wanavijiji** — Kenya slum federation, organises through savings groups not labour-union structure
- **Mathare Resident Association** — community-level advocacy

**No formal tenant union exists in any EA country.** Collective bargaining power is therefore minimal compared with landlord WhatsApp networks.

## Part 8 — Vulnerable groups

| Group | Specific pain |
|---|---|
| **Single women** | ~90% landlord rejection rate vs single men; asked for male guarantor; assumptions about morality / sex work |
| **Divorced / separated mothers** | Credit stigma; assumed to default; child-related "noise" cited as exclusion |
| **Refugees** (Somali, Congolese, S. Sudanese, Burundian) | Police profiling; ID verification refusals; landlords cite "security risk"; ~50,000–100,000 informal Somali tenants in Nairobi |
| **Foreigners** (Indian, Chinese, expats) | Mixed: prime tenants in upmarket areas; subject to USD-pegged premium rents |
| **Persons with disabilities** | ~95% of rental stock non-accessible; legal frameworks (KE / UG) exist but are unenforced; housing eats 50–80% of income |
| **Elderly / pensioners** | Fixed pensions eroded by annual rent inflation; age discrimination from landlords assuming health-related exit risk |
| **Domestic workers in employer compounds** | Dependent housing; loss of job = loss of accommodation overnight |

Constitutional protections exist (Kenya Art. 27, Uganda Arts. 21 & 42) but enforcement mechanisms reach under 10% of cases.

## Part 10 — Neighbourhood pain-map

### Kampala
- **Ntinda, Bukoto, Naguru:** mid-upmarket; deposit theft + sudden hikes most common
- **Najjera, Kira, Kyaliwajjala:** rapid-growth suburbs; broker scams concentrated
- **Kireka, Kyanja, Bunga, Kansanga:** mid-tier; landlord-tenant disputes routine
- **Bwaise, Bunamwaya:** *muzigo* density; pest, sanitation, water failure

### Nairobi
- **Westlands, Kilimani, Kileleshwa, Lavington:** USD-pegged rents; short-let competition (Airbnb) drives 10%+ hikes
- **Karen:** elite single-family; relatively low complaint volume
- **Roysambu, Pipeline, Kasarani:** mid-tier high-rises; lift outages, water rationing
- **Eastlands (South B / C, Buruburu, Kayole, Donholm):** older stock; bedbugs endemic
- **Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru, Kawangware:** informal; insecurity of tenure; demolition cycles

### Mombasa
- **Nyali, Bamburi, Mtwapa:** holiday-let competition; seasonal rent volatility
- **Tudor:** older stock; pest issues

### Dar es Salaam
- **Mikocheni, Masaki, Oyster Bay:** expat segment; USD pegging
- **Kinondoni, Tabata:** local mid-tier; *dalali* exploitation

### Kigali
- **Kacyiru, Nyarutarama, Kimihurura, Kibagabaga:** expat-driven prime; locals priced out
- 79% of Kigali residents in informal settlements; 54% earn $38–225 / month (IGC 2024)

## Country-by-country summary

### Uganda — most-cited deposit & enforcement crisis

L&T Act 2022 introduced 1-month deposit cap, 30-day refund, distress-for-rent ban, 10% annual rent cap, 90-day notice. **All four routinely ignored.** No tribunal — courts only — so enforcement is slow. 80% deposit non-refund stat is the most striking single number in this entire report.

### Kenya — best legal infrastructure, weak enforcement

Largest formal sector, functioning Rent Tribunal, 2025 Small Claims Court precedent. But informal settlements (Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru) lack property records and remain vulnerable to demolition. The 2024 riparian eviction of 20,000+ with KSh 10,000 compensation is the harshest recent state-action against tenants.

### Tanzania — weakest framework, *dalali* exploitation

Rental law sparsely codified; many oral leases. Government Nov 2021 ban on *dalali* charging 1-month commission unenforced. DAWASA 9-hour daily water supply compounds quality failures. Limited tenant advocacy.

### Rwanda — affordability crisis in formal supply

79% of Kigali residents in informal settlements. 54% earn $38–225/month (IGC). Homeownership only 34%. Legal framework emerging but tenant advocacy minimal. Fastest courts in EA (3–5 months) — but few use them.

### Burundi & South Sudan

Conflict / humanitarian context dominates. Formal rental market documentation minimal. Burundi: rent inflation doubling rents in <12 months in some Bujumbura segments. South Sudan: precarious land rights at all income levels.

## Top 10 tenant pain points, ranked by frequency in sources

1. Deposit non-refund (UG 80%; KE enforcement weak)
2. Broker / fake-listing scams (UG 30%)
3. Arbitrary rent hikes (statutory caps ignored)
4. Discrimination by landlord (single women, refugees, PWDs)
5. Utility failure without rent reduction (water, power, internet)
6. Eviction without due process (lockouts, demolitions)
7. Caretaker bribery / harassment / surveillance
8. Pest infestation in mid-tier stock
9. Awareness gap (<30% know their rights)
10. Vulnerable-group compounded discrimination
