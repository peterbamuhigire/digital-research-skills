# Vulnerable groups — tenant cohort deep-dive

Six cohorts whose pain stacks compoundingly on top of the general-tenant baseline. Every one of them is a candidate sub-segment for targeted product, policy, or NGO work.

## 1. Single women

- Estimated **~90% rejection rate** vs single men of similar income (Medium / Daily Nation research synthesis — single source; needs independent verification)
- Common landlord rationales: assumptions about morality, sex work, "noise from visitors," male-guarantor demands
- Constitutional prohibition exists (Kenya Art. 27 — non-discrimination on sex / marital status) — enforcement reaches <10% of cases
- Compounding: divorced or separated women carry an additional credit-stigma layer ("she'll default")
- **Mitigation patterns:** women rely heavily on word-of-mouth networks, female-friendly buildings (rare), and family-controlled units

## 2. Refugees & migrants

**Documented populations:**
- Somali tenants in Nairobi (Eastleigh): 50,000–100,000 estimated informal
- Congolese, S. Sudanese, Burundian: spread across Kampala, Nairobi
- Indian / Chinese expat clusters: prime tenants in upmarket areas (different segment)

**Pain stack:**
- Police profiling and ID-document refusals
- Landlords cite "security risk" as exclusion ground
- Forced into informal settlements where rent is high relative to quality
- Limited access to Tribunal / Small Claims Court — fear of police interaction
- UN-Habitat and UNHCR documents recognise the rights gap; landlord practice doesn't reflect it

## 3. Persons with disabilities

- Estimated **~95% of EA rental stock is non-accessible**
- Legal frameworks (Kenya Persons with Disabilities Act; Uganda equivalent) exist but are unenforced
- Housing eats **50–80% of disposable income** for tenants with disabilities, vs ~30% benchmark for the general population
- The CNRSP Bujumbura 2024 expulsion of 26 disabled students (covered in `students/research/`) is illustrative of the broader pattern
- **Mitigation:** family-controlled housing dominates; institutional / market supply almost absent

## 4. Elderly / pensioners

- Fixed-pension income eroded by annual rent inflation that exceeds the inflation rate
- Age discrimination from landlords assuming health-related vacating risk
- Health-housing trade-off: elderly tenants downgrade unit quality to keep paying rent, accelerating health decline
- No specific EA statutory protection
- **Cohort size growing** as life expectancy rises and family-housing patterns shift toward urban nuclear households

## 5. Single mothers

- Layer A: single-woman discrimination (cohort 1)
- Layer B: child-related "noise" cited as exclusion
- Layer C: assumptions about default driven by sole breadwinner status
- Layer D: school-fee + rent simultaneous pressure forcing down-trading
- **Mitigation:** family compounds; church-affiliated housing; co-rent with sister / friend

## 6. Domestic workers in employer compounds

- Housing tied to employment — quit or be fired = lose accommodation overnight
- Below-market rent (often "free as part of package") but zero security
- No standalone tenancy rights
- High abuse exposure; difficult to report given housing dependency
- **Sector-specific advocacy:** Kenya KUDHEIHA (Kenya Union of Domestic, Hotels, Educational Institutions, Hospitals & Allied Workers); Uganda has nascent advocacy

## Compounded vulnerability — intersectionality

A **single mother who is a refugee with a disability** stacks four cohorts. EA housing markets do not have a recognised protection regime for this intersection. Discrimination compounds at each layer; no single legal-aid centre covers all four; product opportunities here are real but politically sensitive.

## What's not in scope

LGBTQ+ tenant safety has been excluded from this report at the user's instruction.

## Constitutional & statutory anchors

- **Kenya Constitution Article 27** — non-discrimination on race, sex, pregnancy, marital status, health status, ethnic / social origin, colour, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, dress, language, birth
- **Uganda Constitution Articles 21 & 42** — equality and freedom from discrimination
- **Persons with Disabilities Acts** (KE 2003, UG 2006) — accessibility obligations
- **Refugee Acts** (KE 2006, UG 2006) — right to non-discrimination including in housing

These anchors exist. Enforcement reaches <10% of cases. The single largest tenant-rights opportunity in EA is **enforcement infrastructure**, not new statutes.

## Product / policy implications

- **Targeted credit-builder products** for single women, single mothers, refugees — making rental-payment history visible to mainstream landlords as a counter-signal to discrimination
- **Disability-accessible building register** — opt-in landlord listing tier with accessibility certification
- **Refugee tenant legal aid** in partnership with UNHCR / UN-Habitat
- **Elderly housing tracker** — affordable-rent inventory specifically for pensioners
- **Domestic-worker housing rights** — bundled into employer-compound contracts via union pressure
