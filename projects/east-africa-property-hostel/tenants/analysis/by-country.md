# Tenant-side: country-by-country breakdown

## Uganda — strongest law on paper, weakest enforcement

**Defining statute:** Landlord & Tenant Act 2022.
- 1-month deposit cap
- 30-day refund mandate, itemised deductions
- Distress-for-rent ban
- 10% annual rent cap
- 90-day notice for any rent increase
- 3 months' rent + damages for unlawful eviction

**Reality:** all four caps and the distress ban routinely ignored. Headline stat: **80% of tenants never get their deposit back** (Daily Monitor 2023). Self-help evictions continue. Brokers extract viewing fees with limited consequence — Spectrum Real Estate Solutions estimates 30% of UG renters encounter at least one fake listing.

**Court / dispute infrastructure:** No tribunal. Magistrate Court only. 4–8 months. The 2018 *Komakech v Ayaa* precedent supports tenants but few have lawyers.

**Hot neighbourhoods:** Ntinda, Bukoto, Naguru (mid-upmarket); Najjera, Kira, Kyaliwajjala (rapid-growth, scam-dense); Bwaise, Bunamwaya (*muzigos* with pest, sanitation, water failure).

**Top tenant-priority interventions:**
1. Deposit-escrow product (the 80% stat is the sharpest hook in EA)
2. Landlord verification before viewing fee
3. Awareness campaign on the 2022 Act

## Kenya — best legal infrastructure, deepest informal-segment exposure

**Defining statutes:** Rent Restriction Act 1982 (operative); Distress for Rent Act (operative); Landlord & Tenant Bill 2021 (pending).

**Recent jurisprudence:**
- *Tribunal Case E007 of 2024* — Rent Tribunal hears deposit refunds within 60 days under CAP 301
- *Muhanda v LP Holdings (2025)* — Small Claims Court jurisdiction over deposits

**Reality:** Formal sector functions reasonably well in Westlands, Kilimani, Kileleshwa. Informal settlements (Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru, Kawangware) lack property records and remain vulnerable.

**The 2024 riparian demolition.** State action displaced 20,000+ residents from Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru, with **KSh 10,000 compensation per household**. Pretext: flood mitigation. The harshest recent state action against tenants in EA.

**Mathare Valley benchmark.** Huchzermeyer et al (2020): 87% earn below minimum wage; **80.9% of tenants are in rent arrears**. The structural mismatch between rent levels and income is starkest here.

**Hot neighbourhoods:** Westlands, Kilimani, Kileleshwa, Lavington (USD-pegged premium); Roysambu, Pipeline, Kasarani (mid-tier high-rise); Eastlands (older stock, bedbugs); Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru (informal, demolition cycles); Mombasa Tudor / Bamburi (coastal, holiday-let competition).

**Top tenant-priority interventions:**
1. Small Claims Court access for deposits (now jurisdictionally clear post-2025)
2. Tribunal awareness in mid-tier
3. Informal-settlement tenure documentation (NGO-led)

## Tanzania — weakest framework, *dalali* dominance

**Defining statutes:** Land Act 1999 (abolished distress, requires summary judgment); Rent Restriction Act 1984 (repealed in practice).

**Reality:** Many leases oral. No tribunal. Government Nov 2021 ban on *dalali* charging 1-month commission **unenforced**. Tenants face arbitrary rent escalation with no statutory cap.

**Utility crisis multiplier.** DAWASA delivers 9 hours/day water with 50% system loss. TANESCO outages frequent. No rent-abatement mechanism.

**Hot neighbourhoods:** Mikocheni, Masaki, Oyster Bay (expat / USD-pegged); Kinondoni, Tabata (local mid-tier); Dodoma (capital-relocation-driven demand).

**Top tenant-priority interventions:**
1. *Dalali* licensing & enforcement (announced March 2025 regulatory body)
2. Rental-law codification
3. Utility service-level regulation

## Rwanda — affordability crisis in formal supply, fastest courts

**Reality:** **79% of Kigali residents in informal settlements**; 54% earn $38–225/month (IGC 2024). Homeownership only 34%; 61% are tenants — but for many, "tenancy" is informal.

**Courts:** 3–5 months — fastest in EA — but few use them.

**Defining frame:** Written leases mandated for the formal sector; emerging tenant-rights framework but minimal advocacy infrastructure.

**Hot neighbourhoods:** Kacyiru, Nyarutarama, Kimihurura, Kibagabaga (expat-driven prime); informal settlements peripheral to these areas where locals are pushed out.

**Top tenant-priority interventions:**
1. Affordable formal-supply expansion
2. Informal-settlement tenure formalisation
3. Tenant-rights education (small population, achievable)

## Burundi — affordability collapse

Rent inflation has doubled rents in <12 months in some Bujumbura segments. No legal framework. Regulatory development announced 2025.

**Top tenant-priority interventions:** survival-level policy (rent freeze, currency stabilisation) more urgent than tenant-tech.

## South Sudan — conflict-driven, bifurcated

Luxury (international staff) vs low-income, with land rights precarious in both segments. Limited formal rental market. Land disputes the dominant concern.

**Top tenant-priority interventions:** tenure documentation; legal-aid expansion in Juba.

## Cross-country pattern

The same five tenant pains rank top-three in every country with sufficient data:

1. **Deposit / rent disputes** (severity scales inversely with court speed)
2. **Discrimination at letting**
3. **Utility failure with no rent remedy**
4. **Eviction without due process**
5. **Awareness gap on existing statutory protection**

Countries diverge on:

- **Legal infrastructure:** UG (most articulated) > KE (operational) > RW > TZ > BI / SS
- **Court speed:** RW (3–5m) > UG (4–8m) > KE (7–11m) > TZ (8–12m)
- **Affordability crisis intensity:** BI > RW (Kigali) > TZ > UG > KE
- **State-led mass-eviction risk:** KE (2024 riparian) ≥ UG > others
- **Tenant-advocacy infrastructure:** KE (Hakijamii, Muungano, MRA) > UG (Justice Centres) > others

**Strategic implication:** A two-sided rental-platform product should anchor in **Kenya** (best legal infrastructure for trust signalling) and **Uganda** (sharpest pain via the 80% deposit stat). Tanzania and Rwanda are second-wave; Burundi and South Sudan are aid / reconstruction territory.
