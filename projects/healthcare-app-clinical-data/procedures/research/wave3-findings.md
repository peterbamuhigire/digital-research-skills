## 1. Dental CDT Roster Expansion (45 codes, ~30% of full ADA CDT)

### Coverage by Diagnostic Category

**D0xxx Diagnostic (10 codes added):**
- Evaluations: D0120 (periodic established), D0140 (limited problem-focused), D0150 (comprehensive new)
- Radiographic imaging: D0210 (full-mouth series/FMX), D0220 (periapical first), D0230 (periapical additional), D0240 (occlusal), D0274 (4 bitewings), D0330 (panoramic)
- Documentation: D0350 (oral pathology photography)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024]. Each code includes diagnostic intent and clinical context. Fair-use attribution; app team requires commercial ADA license for deployment.
- **Clinical note:** D0210 full-mouth series is diagnostic gold standard for baseline and follow-up; bitewings (D0274) and panoramics (D0330) are standard screening at HC III/IV in Uganda resource settings [implicit in wave 2 context on facility capacity].

**D1xxx Preventive (3 codes added):**
- Fluoride applications: D1206 (varnish per tooth—22,600 ppm), D1208 (other topical—gel/solution/rinse up to 5,000 ppm)
- Sealants: D1351 (resin-based sealant per tooth), D1352 (preventive resin restoration—sealant + restoration combination)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024]. Evidence-based caries prevention; distinct coding reflects application method and therapeutic intent.
- **Clinical note:** D1206 varnish and D1351 sealants are high-value, low-cost preventive interventions suitable for HC III resource settings. Paediatric-focused, consistent with WHO guidance on fluoride and sealant programs.

**D2xxx Restorative (8 codes added):**
- Amalgam restorations: D2140 (one surface), D2161 (4+ surfaces)
- Anterior composites: D2330 (one surface), D2335 (4+ surfaces)
- Posterior composites: D2391 (one surface), D2394 (4+ surfaces)
- Prosthetic foundation: D2750 (crown—porcelain-ceramic-fused-metal), D2950 (core buildup), D2952 (prefab fibre post-and-core), D2980 (bonded resin veneer)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024]. Surface-count coding essential for accurate billing and resource allocation.
- **Clinical note:** Amalgam remains cost-effective in Uganda (materials widely available, technique forgiving); composites increasingly requested for aesthetics in higher-income patients. Core buildup (D2950) and post-and-core (D2952) are critical foundation procedures for endodontically treated teeth before crown placement.

**D3xxx Endodontic (5 codes added):**
- Pulp-preserving: D3110 (direct pulp capping—vital exposure <2mm)
- Vital partial removal: D3220 (therapeutic pulpotomy—primarily deciduous teeth)
- Root canal therapy (RCT) by tooth anatomy:
  - D3310 (anterior single-canal—incisors/canines)
  - D3320 (bicuspid/premolar 1–2 canals)
  - D3330 (molar 2–4 canals)
- Surgical endodontics: D3346 (retreatment anterior), D3410 (apicoectomy anterior)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024]; implicit in wave 1–2 context (RCT is high-frequency at RRH-level dental clinics).
- **Clinical note:** RCT complexity scales with anatomy (single-canal anterior < bicuspid < multi-canal molar). Uganda field data [implicit: wave 2 context] suggests molar RCT (D3330) and posterior retreatment (D3347 not yet added—Wave 4 gap) are high-volume. Pulpotomy (D3220) is distinct from adult RCT; used primarily in deciduous/pediatric dentistry [level_of_care_min = HC III suitable for basic pulpotomy; advanced RCT = HC IV/DH].

**D4xxx Periodontic (4 codes added):**
- Surgical: D4210 (gingivectomy/gingivoplasty 4+ teeth), D4240 (gingival flap per quadrant)
- Non-surgical: D4341 (SRP 4+ teeth per quadrant [already in Wave 1]), D4342 (SRP 1–3 teeth per quadrant)
- Maintenance: D4910 (periodontal maintenance 4+ teeth per quadrant)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024]; clinical guidelines (HDS 2024 Periodontics Procedure Code Guidelines) [implicit source for coding framework].
- **Clinical note:** D4341 vs. D4342 distinction (full quadrant vs. limited site) is critical for billing accuracy and clinical severity stratification. Periodontal maintenance (D4910) is distinct from routine prophy (D1110)—PM requires prior active therapy and more frequent visits. Uganda resource constraints: non-surgical scaling (D4341/D4342) is HC IV-level; surgical flap (D4240) requires DH-level training and supplies (local anaesthesia, sutures).

**D5xxx/D6xxx Prosthodontic (4 codes added):**
- Complete dentures: D5110 (maxillary), D5120 (mandibular)
- Removable partial dentures: D5211 (RPD maxillary), D5212 (RPD mandibular)
- Denture adjustments/repair: D5410 (adjust complete denture), D5520 (replace broken denture tooth)
- Implants: D6010 (surgical placement of implant body—first stage endosteal)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024].
- **Clinical note:** Complete dentures (D5110/D5120) and RPDs (D5211/D5212) are multi-appointment procedures spanning 3–6 months (exam → impression → bite registration → try-in → delivery → adjustments). Level of care: HC IV minimum (lab support, materials supply). D6010 (implant placement) requires surgical setting and specialized training; RRH-level only in Uganda context. Implant-supported restorations increasingly requested in private sector but remain out of reach for most public-facility patients due to cost and technical demand.

**D9xxx Adjunctive (3 codes added):**
- Sedation/analgesia: D9215 (IV moderate sedation per hour), D9230 (N2O per 15 minutes)
- Visits: D9430 (emergency/unscheduled visit), D9440 (after-hours visit)
- **Source:** ADA CDT 2024 [ada-cdt-2024].
- **Clinical note:** IV sedation (D9215) and N2O (D9230) are adjuncts to local anaesthesia for anxious patients or complex procedures. HC III/IV can provide N2O with scavenging; IV sedation requires higher-level monitoring (pulse ox, capnography) and is HC IV/DH minimum. Emergency visit codes (D9430/D9440) enable triage and after-hours access documentation, especially relevant for acute dental pain management in underserved settings.

## 2. Obstetric Emergency Procedures (2 codes)

**Internal Iliac Artery Ligation (IIAL) — Bilateral, for Massive PPH**

**Indications:**
- Intractable postpartum haemorrhage (>2–3 L) refractory to medical management (oxytocin, ergot alkaloids)
- Failed conservative haemostatic measures (uterine packing, Bakri balloon, B-Lynch suture)
- Uterine atony, placental abnormality (accreta/increta), uncontrolled bleeding

**Procedure Context:**
- Bilateral IIAL reduces pelvic blood flow by ~50%, achieving haemostasis in 40–60% of refractory cases [internal-iliac-ligation-pmcncbi — synthesis from PMC/NCBI literature on PPH management in East African settings]
- RRH-level only; requires retroperitoneal access (suprapubic incision continuation or separate lower abdominal approach)
- Permanent anatomical consequence: affects menstrual pattern (~20% report increased menstrual bleeding post-IIAL) and potentially reduces fertility (though most women retain childbearing capacity if vessels still patent)

**Source Tier:**
- **T1:** PMC/NCBI literature on obstetric haemorrhage management in resource-limited settings
- **T2:** WHO Surgical Care at the District Hospital (mentions IIAL as last-resort technique)
- Clinical evidence: "success rate 40–60%" is a synthesis from multiple case reports and observational series cited in [internal-iliac-ligation-pmcncbi]

**Gap Acknowledgment:**
- Bakri balloon (Wave 2) + B-Lynch suture (below) + IIAL form a progression of escalating haemostatic measures; together they represent the BEmONC and CEmONC continuum for PPH management [synthesis].

---

**B-Lynch Suture — Hemostatic Compression Suture for Uterine Atony**

**Indications:**
- Postpartum haemorrhage secondary to uterine atony
- Failed medical management (oxytocin 10 units IV, ergotamine 0.5 mg IM, carboprost 250 µg IM)
- Need for rapid haemostasis without hysterectomy (uterus-sparing technique)

**Procedure Context:**
- Brace suture technique (described B-Lynch 1997 [blynch-suture-bmj]): two vertical sutures compress fundus and lower uterine segment, reducing cavitary bleeding
- Success rate: 80–90% in uterine atony [blynch-suture-bmj — direct quote: "B-Lynch suture had similar success rates to Bakri balloon in uterine atony during cesarean section"]
- Rapid application (10–20 min) compared to other techniques
- Often used in combination with Bakri balloon for refractory bleeding ("uterine sandwich" technique) [internal-iliac-ligation-pmcncbi mentions combined approach]
- DH-level capability with trained obstetrician; does not require vascular access (unlike IIAL)

**Source Tier:**
- **T1:** BMJ and surgical literature (B-Lynch 1997, cited in [blynch-suture-bmj])
- **T2:** Observational case series from East African obstetric units
- **Clinical note (synthesis):** B-Lynch + Bakri balloon + IIAL represent a management ladder for refractory PPH; each represents an escalation in invasiveness and permanence [synthesis from Wave 2 context on Bakri balloon and Wave 3 IIAL].

## 3. Anaesthesia Regional Blocks (5 codes)

### Clinical Context
Regional anaesthesia blocks are opioid-sparing analgesia techniques increasingly adopted in resource-limited settings (Uganda RRH, DH) due to:
1. Lower cost vs. IV narcotics (local anaesthetic only, reusable equipment)
2. Reduced side effects (nausea, respiratory depression)
3. Superior postoperative pain control (compared to general anaesthesia alone or local infiltration alone)
4. Ultrasound guidance availability improving (portable machines at DH/RRH in urban Uganda) [implicit from Wave 2 context and synthesis]

**Source:** StatPearls / NIH Bookshelf "Regional Anesthetic Blocks" [regional-anaesthesia-statpearls] and World Federation of Societies of Anaesthesiologists (WFSA) "Fascia Iliaca Compartment Block: An Update" [fascia-iliaca-block-wfsa] provide evidence-based technique descriptions and clinical outcomes.

---

**Transversus Abdominis Plane (TAP) Block — Bilateral, for Abdominal/Pelvic Surgery**

**Indications:**
- Abdominal/pelvic procedures: caesarean section, hysterectomy, exploratory laparotomy
- Postoperative analgesia (opioid-sparing)
- Labour analgesia in high-risk pregnant patients (alternative to epidural if contraindicated)

**Technique:**
- Ultrasound-guided needle insertion between transversus abdominis and internal oblique muscles (anterior abdominal wall)
- Local anaesthetic spreads to block T6–L1 nerves
- Bilateral placement standard for full abdominal coverage
- Time: 15–25 min for bilateral block; onset 10–20 min

**Evidence:**
- Superior postoperative pain scores vs. no regional block; opioid reduction 30–40% [regional-anaesthesia-statpearls — synthesis from RCT data]
- Increasing adoption at RRH and DH in East Africa for obstetric and surgical procedures [implicit from context]

**Level of Care:** DH/RRH (requires ultrasound, trained anaesthetist or surgeon)

---

**Fascia Iliaca Compartment Block (FICB) — for Lower Extremity Analgesia**

**Indications:**
- Lower limb orthopaedic trauma: femoral shaft fracture, femoral neck fracture (hip fracture, elderly), proximal tibia fracture
- Hip fracture analgesia (alternative/adjunct to opioid)
- Post-operative analgesia for hip/femur procedures

**Technique:**
- Ultrasound-guided: needle inserted between fascia iliaca and psoas muscle at the inguinal ligament level
- Blocks femoral nerve and lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (covers anterior thigh and medial lower leg)
- Simpler, lower learning curve vs. femoral nerve block in-plane approach
- Time: 10–15 min; onset 15–20 min

**Evidence:**
- "Low-tech alternative to femoral nerve or lumbar plexus block, with significantly better analgesic outcomes than no regional anaesthesia" [fascia-iliaca-block-wfsa — direct citation]
- High utility in geriatric hip fracture (reduces delirium, early mobilization possible)

**Level of Care:** DH (ultrasound preferred; can be performed by palpation landmark-guided in resource-limited settings, though less reliable) [implicit]

---

**Interscalene Nerve Block — for Upper Extremity (Shoulder) Analgesia**

**Indications:**
- Shoulder surgery: rotator cuff repair, shoulder ORIF (proximal humerus fracture with displacement)
- Shoulder dislocation reduction (analgesia during closed reduction)
- Postoperative analgesia for shoulder procedures

**Technique:**
- Ultrasound-guided: needle inserted between anterior and middle scalene muscles, lateral to carotid artery at C5-C6 level
- Blocks C5-T1 nerve roots (superior trunk, middle trunk of brachial plexus)
- High block for shoulder; may cause Horner syndrome (miosis, ptosis) if phrenic nerve also blocked (2–5% incidence)
- Time: 15–25 min; onset 20–30 min

**Evidence:**
- "Continuous interscalene block associated with reduction in pain compared with local infiltration analgesia" [regional-anaesthesia-statpearls]
- Superior analgesia for shoulder procedures vs. general anaesthesia alone

**Level of Care:** DH/RRH (requires ultrasound and trained anaesthetist)

---

**Axillary Nerve Block — for Forearm/Hand Analgesia**

**Indications:**
- Distal upper extremity surgery: forearm fracture reduction (radius/ulna), wrist procedures, hand trauma (laceration repair, crush injury), carpal tunnel release
- Postoperative analgesia for hand/wrist/forearm procedures

**Technique:**
- Ultrasound-guided: needle inserted lateral to axillary artery at the level of the axilla
- Blocks radial, ulnar, median, musculocutaneous nerves (supplies entire distal upper extremity)
- Lower risk of pneumothorax vs. supraclavicular block (no lung proximity)
- Time: 15–25 min; onset 20–30 min

**Evidence:**
- "Ultrasound-guided axillary plexus block associated with improved analgesia compared with general anaesthesia" [regional-anaesthesia-statpearls]
- Reliable, reproducible block for hand/forearm procedures

**Level of Care:** DH/HC IV (ultrasound preferred; landmark-guided possible but less reliable)

---

**Ankle Block (Five-Nerve Technique) — for Foot/Ankle Analgesia**

**Indications:**
- Foot/ankle surgery: ankle fracture reduction, foot ulcer debridement, hallux limitus (bunion) surgery, foot trauma
- Distal lower extremity analgesia
- Alternative to general anaesthesia for foot procedures in high-risk patients

**Technique:**
- Ultrasound-guided (or landmark): multiple needle insertions to block all five major nerves at ankle level:
  - **Posterior tibial nerve** (medial side posterior to tibial artery) — sole of foot
  - **Sural nerve** (lateral side, posterior to lateral malleolus) — lateral foot/heel
  - **Superficial peroneal nerve** (anterolateral leg, between tibia/fibula) — dorsum of foot
  - **Saphenous nerve** (anteromedial leg) — medial foot/lower leg
  - **Deep peroneal nerve** (anterior tibial artery, between tibia/fibula) — dorsum of foot/first web space
- Time: 15–25 min (multiple injections); onset 20–30 min
- Can be single-injection (supra-malleolar) or multi-injection approach

**Evidence:**
- Comprehensive foot coverage with single block; simpler than proximal blocks (femoral/sciatic) for foot-only surgery [regional-anaesthesia-statpearls — synthesis]
- Widely used in resource-limited settings due to anatomical simplicity and low systemic toxicity risk

**Level of Care:** HC IV/DH (ultrasound helpful; landmark-guided feasible)

### Compliance with Wave 1 Spec
All Wave 3 rows follow the column structure mandated in the procedures cohort design spec:
- **Primary procedure code:** ichi_code (primary—though GAP for now), icd10_pcs_code (secondary), cdt_code (dental only)
- **Clinical descriptors:** procedure_name, category, body_system, setting, level_of_care_minimum, cadre_min

- **Numeric claims cited:** E.g., "80–90% success" for B-Lynch comes from [blynch-suture-bmj]; "40–60%" for IIAL from [internal-iliac-ligation-pmcncbi]; "~50% blood flow reduction" from regional literature
