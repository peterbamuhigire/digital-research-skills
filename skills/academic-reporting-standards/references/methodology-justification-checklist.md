# Reference — Methodology Justification Checklist

**Canonical source:** Brause, *Writing Your Doctoral Dissertation*, 1999, Ch 10. Tier 1.

Brause's rule: "Prepare to explain your rationale for selecting the materials and/or processes in light of the theory or phenomenon under investigation" (Ch 10).

A methodology chapter that **describes** without **justifying** is incomplete. Examiners read the methodology chapter looking for the rationale chain: design → theory → phenomenon. If any link in the chain is missing, the examiner asks at viva, and the candidate must supply it on the spot.

## The checklist

For every methodological choice, the chapter must answer:

### Design

- [ ] **What design** (RCT, cohort, case-control, cross-sectional, longitudinal, mixed-methods, ethnographic, grounded-theory, action-research, design-based, …)
- [ ] **Why this design** — link to the research question and theoretical frame. What can this design uniquely answer that an alternative could not?
- [ ] **What designs were considered and rejected** — name them; say why.
- [ ] **What threats to validity** are inherent to the chosen design — and how the design mitigates them.

### Sample / participants

- [ ] **What sample** — N, demographics, setting, recruitment source.
- [ ] **Why this sample** — match between sample and theoretical frame. Why is this sample the right one to answer the question?
- [ ] **Sample-size justification** — power calculation (quantitative); theoretical-saturation argument (qualitative); pragmatic limits acknowledged.
- [ ] **Inclusion and exclusion criteria** — explicit; rationale for each.
- [ ] **Recruitment and consent** — process described; ethics-board approval cited.

### Instruments / data sources

- [ ] **What instruments** — surveys, interview guides, observation schedules, archival sources, secondary datasets.
- [ ] **Why these instruments** — how they map to the constructs the theory implies.
- [ ] **Instrument validation** — published validation if a standard instrument; pilot-study evidence if a new instrument.
- [ ] **Construct validity** — match between concept, operationalisation, and indicator.
- [ ] **Reliability** — internal consistency, test-retest, inter-rater (whichever apply).

### Procedures

- [ ] **Data-collection procedure** — replicable, recipe-style; timing; setting.
- [ ] **Pilot study** — what was tested; what was learned; what changed.
- [ ] **Technology and back-up** — recording equipment, transcription software, data-management systems, redundancy.
- [ ] **Researcher role and reflexivity** (qualitative) — positionality stated; influence on participants acknowledged.

### Analysis

- [ ] **What analytic procedures** — statistical tests, coding scheme, thematic analysis, grounded-theory steps.
- [ ] **Why these procedures** — link to the design and the kind of inference the question requires.
- [ ] **Software** — named (SPSS, R, Stata, NVivo, Atlas.ti).
- [ ] **Coding reliability** (qualitative) — inter-rater agreement; how disagreements were resolved.
- [ ] **Sensitivity analyses** (quantitative) — robustness of findings to alternative model specifications.

### Ethics

- [ ] **IRB / ethics-board approval** — institution, approval number, date.
- [ ] **Informed consent** — process; documentation.
- [ ] **Confidentiality and data-protection** — storage, access, retention, destruction.
- [ ] **Risk-benefit assessment** — for participants; for researcher; mitigations.
- [ ] **Vulnerable populations** — special protections if applicable.

### Limitations

- [ ] **Design limitations** — what this design cannot do.
- [ ] **Sample limitations** — generalisability bounds.
- [ ] **Instrument limitations** — what the measures miss.
- [ ] **Analytic limitations** — assumptions that, if violated, would undermine the findings.
- [ ] **Researcher limitations** — bias, scope, time, resources.

Brause is emphatic on this: every study has limitations. **Honesty enhances credibility; concealment destroys it**. Examiners look for limitations; if absent, they assume the candidate has not thought rigorously about the work.

## The rationale-chain test

For each entry on the checklist, the candidate writes one paragraph in the methodology chapter that connects:

```
[CHOICE] → [DESIGN LOGIC] → [THEORETICAL FRAME] → [RESEARCH QUESTION]
```

If any arrow is weak, the choice is suspect.

Worked example:

> *We used semi-structured interviews of 30 participants drawn from
> three rural districts. Semi-structured (rather than fully structured)
> interviews allow the participant to surface categories the researcher
> may not have anticipated, which is appropriate to a grounded-theory
> design that aims to build, not test, a theory of [PHENOMENON].
> Thirty participants is the sample size at which Glaser & Strauss
> argue theoretical saturation is typically reached for a focused
> phenomenon (Glaser & Strauss, 1967, p. 61); this study confirmed
> saturation by the 26th interview, with the final four serving as
> confirmation rather than as new-category-generation. Three districts
> were chosen to span the variation in [VARIABLE] documented in
> [CITED PRIOR WORK], allowing the emerging theory to be tested
> against within-sample variation.*

Each sentence carries a rationale; nothing is described without justification.

## Anti-patterns

- **Description without justification.** "We used SPSS." (Why? What other software was rejected? What does SPSS do that R or Stata do not, for this question?)
- **Justification by tradition.** "This is the standard approach in this field." (Examiners will press: standard for which question? Was the standard appropriate here, or inherited?)
- **Missing pilot.** No pilot study reported. Examiners ask: how do you know the instrument worked?
- **Glossed limitations.** Limitations section is two sentences. Examiners assume more.
- **Methodology divorced from theory.** Methodology chapter reads as if the theory chapter did not exist. The two must speak to each other.
- **Statistical-consultant tell.** Methodology paragraphs the candidate clearly did not write. Orals will expose memorised verbatim answers within minutes.
