# Reference — Brause Dissertation Craft

**Canonical source:** Brause, Rita S. *Writing Your Doctoral Dissertation: Invisible Rules for Success*. Falmer Press / RoutledgeFalmer, 1999. Tier 1. Local extract: `extracted-books/doctoral-dissertation.txt`.

This reference is the practical-craft layer of `academic-reporting-standards`. It encodes the unwritten conventions every Ivy / Oxbridge / LSE-equivalent examination committee assumes the candidate already knows. The formal-reporting layer (PRISMA, CONSORT, STROBE, MOOSE, GRADE, Cochrane, TOP) is in `equator-decision-tree.md`.

## The invisible rules

These are Brause's central "rules nobody states explicitly". Each is non-negotiable for completion.

1. **The dissertation is an apprenticeship, not an examination.** Faculty treat it as proof of capacity to do scholarly work; students wrongly treat it as a final exam on coursework. This mismatch generates most early conflict. Re-frame internally: you are demonstrating you can do the work, not proving you remember the work.
2. **You must self-initiate every interaction.** "If you don't ask for professors' time, professors will assume all is OK. Pester them!" (Brause, Ch 4, Insight #14). Silence is read as progress, not as a cry for help.
3. **The chair-student relationship is quasi-parental and political.** "Doctoral students are, in a sense, the 'children' of their chairs" (Ch 4, Insight #59). Choice of chair predicts completion more than choice of topic.
4. **Working in parallel keeps the project alive.** "While your committee has Chapters 1–3, work on Chapter 4. When they return their chapters, give them the next chapter" (Ch 4, Insight #12). Never wait idle.
5. **Approval is collective and revocable.** Even a chair-approved draft can fail at orals if external readers disagree. Plan for it.
6. **The proposal is a contract.** Once approved, it binds the university to support and the student to deliver — but new administrations can change the rules. Finish fast.
7. **ABD is a trap.** "Never, never accept ABD status. You have invested too much money and energy into this" (Ch 4, Insight #44).
8. **Read dissertations as genre artefacts** before writing one. Most students have never actually read a doctoral dissertation cover to cover. Read 3–5 from your committee's recent students; internalise the genre.
9. **Isolation is the default; community must be manufactured.** The 50% attrition rate tracks isolation, not intellect. Build a peer support group early; meet weekly.
10. **Statistical significance is not significance.** Don't confuse the two; don't chase p-values at the expense of the original research question.
11. **Pilot everything.** Instruments, technology, transcription, interview protocols. A pilot study turns up the failures the full study would amplify.
12. **The recursive process is the process.** Reading-writing-analysing-revising loop back continuously; linear plans fail.

## Choice of chair — the single most consequential decision

Brause is emphatic: choose chair on **track record of graduating students**, not on prestige. Specifically:

- How many doctoral students has this chair graduated? (Number, not percentage — percentage hides small denominators.)
- What was their average time to completion?
- What did their students go on to do?
- Talk to current and former students of the chair before committing.
- Confirm the chair's preferred working style: humble-dependent vs. independent-resourceful. Some chairs want both, at different stages.
- Chairs who have never graduated a student are not chairs; they are "first-time chairs" — accept the risk knowingly.

## Chapter-by-chapter template

Brause's standard template for a doctoral dissertation. Word counts vary by discipline; the structural rules do not.

### Title page / Descriptive title
Variables + population for hypothesis-testing studies; research-focus phrase for hypothesis-generating studies. Revise the title throughout the project. The title is the anchor — every chapter ties back to it.

### Chapter 1 — Statement of the Problem
Three parts:
- **Brief discussion presenting a "problem" in the discipline.** Why does this question matter to the field?
- **Cohesive theoretical rationale.** What theoretical frame are you working in?
- **Statement of potential significance.** What follows if your study succeeds?

The chapter must establish that the topic is (i) doable, (ii) significant, (iii) arises from comprehensive understanding of current knowledge, (iv) addresses a pivotal disciplinary issue. Examiner expectation: persuasion that the study is worth doing **and** that the candidate is competent to do it.

### Chapter 2 — Literature Review / Theoretical Rationale
Selects current relevant theorists; provides historical evolution incorporating seminal work; reflects expertise available at the institution; explicitly links prior work to the proposed study. Organise topically (by theme) rather than chronologically (by author) — Brause's preference, more synthetic. Closes with a potential-significance statement that tightens the contribution claim from Ch 1.

Length: highly variable. The proposal alone can run 70+ pages where the lit review dominates.

### Chapter 3 — Methodology / Procedures
Subsections:
- **Data to be collected** — what, with what justification.
- **Procedures for collecting** — recipe-like, replicable.
- **Procedures for analysing** — explicit, with rationale.
- **Validity and reliability** — addressed for the chosen design.

For each procedural decision, Brause's rule: *"Prepare to explain your rationale for selecting the materials and/or processes in light of the theory or phenomenon under investigation"* (Ch 10). Should read like a recipe enabling replication. Includes pilot evidence, sample-size justification, IRB approval, limitations.

### Chapter 4 — Findings
Order: tables and figures first; narrative explanation second; cohesive synthesis third. Five criteria (Brause, Ch 13): **thorough, clear, logical, relevant, cautious**. Quote raw data (interview excerpts, scores, observations) so readers can independently judge. Every inference must be data-driven; discard inferences not supported by data.

### Chapter 5 — Concluding sections
Four sub-sections:
- **Summary** — recapitulation of problem, design, findings, conclusions.
- **Conclusions** — assertions about whether the theory was supported.
- **Implications** — significance for practice / theory; revisits the proposal's "potential significance" with hindsight.
- **Recommendations** — restricted to a few targeted suggestions for future research and practice.

### Abstract
~350 words for DAI. Title; problem; theoretical frame; data sources; analytic procedures; outcomes. Often the only section read externally.

## Lit review craft (Brause's process)

- Start a topic file with your **first** course assignment; harvest course papers as proto-lit-review fragments.
- Read multiple completed dissertations chapter-by-chapter to internalise genre.
- Make a complete bibliographic card *before* reading further than title/author — capture every field needed for citation so you never re-find a source.
- Organise by **topic/theme**, not chronologically by author, when the field permits — produces a more synthetic argument.
- Three-fold purpose: fill a theoretical/research gap; explore inconsistency; clarify conflict between theories.
- Reflect institutional expertise — connect to your committee's scholarly territory.
- Stop reading when new sources cite only references you already know — but expect to return for targeted reading once writing exposes gaps.

## Working with advisors and committees

- Choose chair on track record of graduating students — not prestige.
- Confirm chair's preferred working style: humble-dependent vs. independent-resourceful.
- Treat the chair as ally; surface ambiguity early; restate your understanding back to confirm shared meaning.
- Expect drafts to be returned slowly — keep working on the next chapter while waiting.
- Readers may be selected by the chair after the chair declares the work "ready"; "ready" is interpreted differently by different faculty.
- Document everything; never throw anything away; back up obsessively.
- Cultivate a peer support group — they know the local terrain and reduce isolation.

## Writing process

- Block daily undisturbed time (Brause: even 4–6 a.m. if needed); write every day, tired or not.
- Divide and conquer — work chapter-section-paragraph; tick sub-goals.
- Expect ~6 years total; expect constant revision of revised text.
- Set personal deadlines; meet them; revise as you progress.
- Never accept ABD; never let a day pass without doing *something*.
- Use a critical reader as devil's advocate.
- Triple back-up (hard, drive, off-site).
- Stuck-ness: typically signals next-chapter readiness, not failure — switch to a different sub-task; consult support group; return to data displays as fresh angle.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the dissertation as a long term paper (it is genre-different).
- Waiting for chair feedback in idle silence.
- Choosing chair by prestige rather than completion track record.
- Outsourcing statistical or methodological work without owning the logic.
- Equating statistical with substantive significance.
- Over-generalising beyond the data ("solved all questions in the world").
- Conversely, under-claiming — refusing to interpret beyond bare data.
- Diluting findings with trivial details — burying the major contribution.
- Saving everything for a "complete" submission rather than rolling drafts.
- Making major life changes (moves, new jobs) mid-dissertation.
- Personal-attack interpretation of academic critique ("blocked writer").
- Romanticising the process as enlightenment rather than incremental detail work.
