# Reference — Viva / Defence Preparation

**Canonical source:** Brause, *Writing Your Doctoral Dissertation*, 1999, Ch 14. Tier 1.

Vocabulary varies by institution: a **defence** (US convention) tends to be more confrontational, with the candidate defending choices; an **orals** (US graduate-school convention) tends toward peer-style discussion of multiple perspectives; a **viva voce** (UK / Oxbridge / Commonwealth convention) is closer to the orals model and may be either short (focused) or long (comprehensive). The term the institution uses signals expectation. Confirm the format before preparing.

## The four purposes

Per Brause:

1. **Quality control** — does the work meet the institution's doctoral standard?
2. **Academic conversation among peers** — the examiner is testing the candidate's standing as a future colleague, not as a student.
3. **Dissemination** — the examiner now understands the work and can speak to it.
4. **Closure** — the project ends; the candidate moves on.

Preparation differs by purpose. A defence emphasises (1); a viva emphasises (2); both depend on (3) being executed well during the session.

## External-reader reconnaissance

The external examiner is the wild card. Brause's discipline:

- Find the external's published work — read the most recent five papers and the most-cited paper.
- Identify their controversial positions and methodological commitments — are they critical of approaches similar to yours?
- Note their departmental philosophy — different institutions have different houses of thought.
- Cross-reference: have they cited your supervisor? Your committee? Anyone you cite extensively?
- If they have written a book, read its introduction and conclusion at minimum.

The external is unlikely to be hostile; they are more often colleagues. But they will press the points where their interests intersect with the dissertation. Preparation lets the candidate engage substantively rather than defensively.

## Predictable question bank

These questions appear in some form in nearly every viva. The candidate prepares a 60–90 second answer for each.

- **What were your main findings?**
- **What surprised you?**
- **What would you do differently if you started over?**
- **Why did you choose A and not B?** (Substitute methodology, theory, sample, instrument.)
- **Which theorists most influenced you, and why?**
- **What are the main conflicts in the field, and where does your work sit?**
- **What is the contribution of this study to knowledge?** (See `references/originality-claim.md`.)
- **Where do you see the specialisation going in the next 5–10 years?**
- **What would you publish from this work, and where?**
- **If you were starting today, what would you do?**
- **What are the main limitations of the study?**
- **How would you respond to a critic who argued [PREDICTABLE CRITIQUE]?**

## Tabbing strategy

Brause's recommendation: tab the dissertation with colour-coded markers for fast reference during the session.

- **Red tabs:** load-bearing data tables and figures.
- **Yellow tabs:** key methodological choices the candidate expects to defend.
- **Green tabs:** the original-contribution claim and supporting evidence.
- **Blue tabs:** the discussion's bias-acknowledgement and limitations passages.

When an examiner asks a question that maps to a tabbed section, the candidate finds it in five seconds and reads or paraphrases — much stronger than recalling from memory under pressure.

## The opening

Many institutions allow the candidate to open with a short overview (5–15 minutes, often slides). Brause notes this is often easier than facing the panel cold — the candidate faces the screen, not the examiners, for the first few minutes.

Opening structure:

1. The research question.
2. The theoretical frame and why it was chosen.
3. The design in one sentence.
4. The two or three main findings.
5. The contribution claim.
6. The biggest limitation and how the candidate has thought about it.

Five slides. Twelve minutes. Then take questions.

## The discipline of admission

Brause: "Acknowledging unfamiliar work honestly is appropriate — admitting non-omniscience." Three patterns:

- "I am not familiar with that paper — could you summarise the relevant point, and I'll engage with it?"
- "That is a fair criticism. I addressed it partially in [CHAPTER X] by [APPROACH], but the criticism still applies in the sense that [HONEST CONCESSION]."
- "I had not considered that interpretation. Let me think about it. [PAUSE.] I see how it would imply [Y]; my data are consistent with it in the sense of [Z]; the prior literature would suggest [W]; on balance I think [POSITION], but it is a stronger reading than I had in the dissertation."

Bluffing is detected. Honest engagement is rewarded.

## Bias and perspective acknowledgement

Brause Ch 14: "It would be prudent to acknowledge your biases and the bases for these, while recognising the possibility of other perspectives on the same issue. Avoid polemics."

If the candidate's standpoint is partisan (politically, methodologically, ideologically), acknowledge it cleanly in the opening or in the response to the relevant question. The examiner who has spotted the standpoint is testing whether the candidate has spotted it too.

## Anti-patterns

- **Defensiveness.** Treating every question as an attack. Most are not. Engage as a colleague.
- **Bluffing on unfamiliar work.** Always exposed within follow-ups.
- **Over-elaborate answers.** A 90-second answer that meanders for 5 minutes signals anxiety. Stop talking when the answer is complete.
- **Apologising.** "Sorry, this is a weak point in my work…" Don't editorialise; address the point and move on.
- **Disagreeing without engaging.** "I just don't agree with that." Engage with the reasons; explain why your evidence supports a different view.
- **Polemics.** "Theorists who disagree with me are wrong." Examiners will end the session in a worse mood than they started it.
- **Memorising verbatim answers.** Sounds rehearsed; closes off conversation. Prepare 60–90 second skeletons; deliver fresh.
- **Skipping external-reader reconnaissance.** Surprises in viva are recoverable; preventable surprises are not.

## After the viva

Whatever the outcome (pass, pass with corrections, revise and resubmit, fail), the candidate writes a one-page debrief within 48 hours: questions asked, answers given, examiner reactions, corrections required. The debrief is for the candidate's own learning and for sharing (sanitised) with junior peers — Brause's "manufactured community" applies after the viva as well as before.
