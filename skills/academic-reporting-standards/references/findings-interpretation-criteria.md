# Reference — Findings Interpretation Criteria

**Canonical source:** Brause, *Writing Your Doctoral Dissertation*, 1999, Ch 13. Tier 1.

The findings chapter is the load-bearing chapter of the dissertation. Brause's five tests apply to every claim that ships from it.

## Brause's five tests

A finding ships if and only if it passes all five.

1. **Thorough.** All relevant data are included; nothing material is omitted. If a sub-finding contradicts the main finding, it is reported, not buried.
2. **Clear.** A reader who has the methodology in mind can follow the chain from data to interpretation without rereading.
3. **Logical.** Each inference follows from the data shown. No leaps; no ungrounded generalisations.
4. **Relevant.** Each finding ties back to a research question stated in chapter 1. Findings that don't tie back are either re-anchored or moved to an appendix.
5. **Cautious.** The interpretation is calibrated to what the data actually support. Over-claiming and under-claiming are both red flags.

## The structural ordering

Brause's order, non-negotiable:

1. **Tables and figures first.** The data are shown before they are explained. A reader can form an independent impression before the candidate's interpretation lands.
2. **Narrative explanation second.** Each table/figure gets its narrative paragraph: what is shown; what stands out; how to read the columns/axes.
3. **Cohesive synthesis third.** Across the tables/figures, the chapter builds toward the answers to the research questions. The synthesis is where interpretation lives.

This order respects the examiner's right to disagree with the candidate's reading. If the data come embedded in interpretation, the examiner cannot easily separate evidence from inference.

## Quote-the-data discipline

For qualitative findings, **raw data** (interview excerpts, observation field-notes, document quotations) appear in the chapter so the reader can independently judge the inference. Brause: "Your data are the evidence supporting your findings… Inferences are data driven" (Ch 13).

For quantitative findings, the analogous discipline is to show **distributions, not just summary statistics**. A mean of 4.5 means very different things at SD = 0.2 vs. SD = 1.8. The reader needs both.

## Inference rules

- **Every inference cites the data it rests on.** Either by row/column reference (table) or by quote (qualitative).
- **Inferences not supported by data are discarded.** Brause is emphatic: "discard inferences not supported by data."
- **Generalisations beyond the sample are flagged.** If the study sampled three districts, claims about the country are speculation, not finding.
- **Statistical significance ≠ substantive significance.** Brause: "Statistical significance is only one type of significance" (Ch 10). A p < 0.001 finding can be substantively trivial; a non-significant trend can be substantively important. Discuss both.
- **Effect sizes and confidence intervals matter.** Report them; interpret them; do not hide them behind p-values alone.

## The discussion section vs. the findings section

These are different. The findings section reports what the data say. The discussion section interprets the findings against the theoretical frame and against the prior literature reviewed in chapter 2.

A common failure: discussion smuggled into findings. The fix: write the findings chapter assuming the reader has not yet read the discussion. The chapter should stand alone as a faithful report.

## Bias acknowledgement (Brause Ch 14)

In the discussion section: "Acknowledge your biases and the bases for these, while recognising the possibility of other perspectives on the same issue. Avoid polemics."

A discussion that does not acknowledge the candidate's standpoint is a discussion the examiner will challenge. Three sentences suffice: what is the candidate's prior commitment; what is the basis for it; what alternative perspectives the candidate has considered.

## The chapter 5 frame

The conclusion chapter has four sub-sections in Brause's template:

- **Summary** — recapitulation of problem, design, findings, conclusions.
- **Conclusions** — assertions about whether the theory was supported.
- **Implications** — significance for practice and theory; revisits the proposal's "potential significance" statement.
- **Recommendations** — restricted to a few targeted suggestions for future research and practice.

The implications sub-section is where the **original-contribution claim** (see `references/originality-claim.md`) is revisited with hindsight. The candidate looks back at chapter 1's prediction of significance and reports honestly: did it hold up?

## Anti-patterns

- **Burying contradicting sub-findings.** Examiners notice; integrity tanks.
- **Selective reporting of statistics that support the hypothesis.** TOP Guidelines (`equator-decision-tree.md`) catch this.
- **Quote-cherry-picking** — selecting interview excerpts that support the leading interpretation while ignoring excerpts that complicate it.
- **Inflating substantive significance from statistical significance** alone.
- **Polemics in discussion** — "this study definitively settles X" → no it doesn't, and the examiner has just decided you don't know it.
- **Interpretation in tables.** Tables are data; interpretation lives in narrative. Don't blur.
