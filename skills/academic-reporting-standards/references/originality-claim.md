# Reference — Originality Claim

**Canonical source:** Brause, *Writing Your Doctoral Dissertation*, 1999, Chs 6, 10. Tier 1.

The "original contribution to knowledge" claim is non-negotiable for a doctoral artefact at Ivy / Oxbridge / LSE-equivalent institutions. Brause's framing: the contribution is established by showing (a) comprehensive command of prior work, (b) the precise location of the unmet need, (c) why your design is the appropriate response, (d) the probable significance of findings to discipline / practice / policy.

The claim appears explicitly in **two** places: the abstract (1–2 sentences) and chapter 1 (paragraph or section). It is referenced again in chapter 5 (implications), where the proposal's "potential significance" statement is revisited with hindsight.

## The five frames for "what's new"

A doctoral contribution must fit at least one of these frames. The strongest dissertations fit two.

### 1. Gap-filling
A theoretical or empirical territory not yet investigated. The claim format: "*X has been studied in domains A and B but not in domain C; this study addresses C.*"

**Defensibility test.** A literature search that demonstrates the gap, run within a reasonable scope and recency. Examiners will replicate the search; the gap must hold up.

### 2. Inconsistency-resolving
Conflicting prior findings on the same question; the dissertation's design adjudicates between them. The claim format: "*Studies P and Q reach conflicting conclusions on X; this study isolates the variable that explains the divergence.*"

**Defensibility test.** Both prior findings are characterised fairly (not strawmanned); the design is one that genuinely could come out either way; the resolution is rigorously argued.

### 3. Conflict-clarifying
Competing theoretical frameworks predict different outcomes on the same data; the dissertation submits both to a critical test. The claim format: "*Theory T1 predicts X; theory T2 predicts Y; on this dataset, T1 holds (or T2 holds, or neither holds, or both hold partially with named conditions).*"

**Defensibility test.** Both theories are characterised at the strongest version their proponents would accept; the test is genuinely diagnostic; the conclusion respects what the data actually show.

### 4. Theory-testing
An untested theoretical claim is submitted to data. The claim format: "*Theory T predicts X under conditions C; this study tests the prediction with a sample drawn from C.*"

**Defensibility test.** The theory is articulated precisely enough to make a falsifiable prediction; the conditions C are met by the sample; the test is appropriately powered.

### 5. Theory-building
No viable theory exists; the dissertation generates categories and propositions from the data inductively. The claim format: "*Phenomenon P has not been theorised; this study generates a grounded-theory account of P from observations of N cases.*"

**Defensibility test.** Inductive process is documented (open coding → axial → selective per Strauss-Corbin, or equivalent); inter-rater reliability if applicable; categories grounded in data with verbatim evidence; theoretical saturation argued.

## How to write the claim

Two sentences for the abstract:

```
This study [VERB FROM A FRAME ABOVE] [SPECIFIC TOPIC] in the context of
[POPULATION / SETTING]. Building on [NAMED PRIOR WORK that established
the gap / inconsistency / conflict / untested theory / theoretical
absence], it contributes [SPECIFIC NEW KNOWLEDGE: a finding, a
distinction, a model, a category set].
```

A paragraph for chapter 1:

```
The contribution of this study is [VERB] [SPECIFIC TOPIC] in [CONTEXT].
The literature [reviewed in detail in Chapter 2] establishes that [WHAT
IS KNOWN]. It also establishes that [WHAT IS NOT KNOWN, or WHERE THE
INCONSISTENCY/CONFLICT LIES]. This study addresses the gap [or resolves
the inconsistency, or adjudicates the conflict, or tests the prediction,
or builds a theory] by [DESIGN IN ONE SENTENCE]. The probable
significance of findings is [FOR DISCIPLINE], [FOR PRACTICE], [FOR
POLICY] — each elaborated in section 1.3.
```

## Examiner-reading patterns

External examiners typically look for the originality claim in three places, in order:
1. The abstract.
2. The first three pages of chapter 1.
3. The opening of chapter 5 (implications).

If the claim is absent or vague in any of these locations, the examiner forms an early adverse impression that is hard to recover from.

## Anti-patterns

- **Topic claim instead of contribution claim.** "This study examines X in Africa" is a topic; it is not a contribution. What is *new*?
- **Contribution claim that is not original.** Frame 1 (gap-filling) is the most-claimed and most-commonly-falsified. The literature search must be thorough enough that an examiner cannot find a recent dissertation or paper covering the same ground.
- **Over-claiming.** "This study revolutionises the field." Examiners punish bombast. Calibrate to what the data support.
- **Under-claiming.** "This study makes a small contribution." If you do not believe in the contribution, the examiner will not either.
- **Multiple un-prioritised contributions.** "This study contributes A, B, C, D, and E." Pick the strongest two; subordinate the rest as secondary contributions.
- **Contribution that emerges only in chapter 5.** If the contribution surfaces only in the implications chapter, the dissertation's structure is wrong; restructure so the contribution is signposted from chapter 1.
- **Claim without defensibility evidence.** Every claim must be defensible at viva by the gap-evidence-fit chain. If the chain is fragile, the claim must be moderated.
