# Reasoning Protocol

Use this reference before drafting any output that explains, interprets, recommends, predicts, evaluates, or persuades.

## Core Stack

| Layer | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Issue | What exactly must be answered? | One precise question |
| Terms | What words or boundaries control the answer? | Definitions and scope notes |
| Facts | What is established by sources or data? | Evidence inventory |
| Claim | What proposition is being advanced? | Claim statement |
| Reason | Why might the claim be true? | Premise or intermediate conclusion |
| Warrant | Why does this reason support this claim? | Linking assumption |
| Countercase | What is the strongest rival explanation or objection? | Objection and response |
| Limit | Where might the claim fail? | Uncertainty and boundary conditions |
| Implication | So what follows? | Decision, action, interpretation, or research next step |

## Argument Map Template

| Conclusion | Intermediate reasons | Evidence | Warrant/link | Assumptions | Countercase | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Main answer or recommendation | 2-5 supporting propositions | Source-backed facts/data/examples | Why evidence supports reason | What must be true | Strongest objection/rival | High/medium/low plus reason |

Do not draft prose until the load-bearing conclusions have an argument map.

## Seven Tests For Every Conclusion

1. **Clarity:** Are the conclusion, terms, and scope specific enough to be tested?
2. **Relevance:** Does each premise actually bear on the conclusion?
3. **Acceptability:** Is the premise supported by credible evidence rather than assertion?
4. **Sufficiency:** Is there enough evidence for the strength of the claim?
5. **Representativeness:** Are examples, cases, data, or sources typical enough?
6. **Coherence:** Do the claims fit together without contradiction or circularity?
7. **Proportionality:** Does certainty match the evidence?

## Inference Tests

### Causal Claims

Use when the output says X caused, drove, reduced, increased, enabled, constrained, or explained Y.

- Establish time order: cause before effect.
- Identify mechanism: how X plausibly affects Y.
- Check alternatives: what else could explain Y?
- Check confounders: what variable might affect both X and Y?
- Avoid post hoc reasoning: after does not mean because of.
- Distinguish necessary conditions, sufficient conditions, triggers, enablers, and background conditions.

### Generalisations

Use when moving from cases, examples, interviews, documents, studies, or datasets to a broader claim.

- Check sample size, selection, representativeness, and missing cases.
- Ask whether the claim holds across time, geography, sector, population, and method.
- Avoid overgeneralising from vivid examples or single organizations.

### Analogies

Use when the argument says this case resembles another case.

- Identify the relevant similarity, not surface resemblance.
- Identify the relevant difference that could break the analogy.
- Do not let an analogy carry more weight than direct evidence.

### Statistical Claims

Use when numbers support the argument.

- Define numerator, denominator, base rate, source, date, geography, and method.
- Check whether the statistic answers the actual question.
- Distinguish correlation, association, trend, estimate, projection, and causal estimate.
- State uncertainty where the number is modeled, sampled, rounded, incomplete, stale, or contested.

### Deductive Or Definition-Based Claims

Use when a conclusion depends on rules, definitions, classifications, or categories.

- Confirm the definition or rule is authoritative for the context.
- Check whether the case actually satisfies every condition.
- Identify exceptions, thresholds, and ambiguous categories.

### Abductive / Best-Explanation Claims

Use when evidence is incomplete and the output selects the best explanation.

- List plausible explanations before choosing.
- Prefer the explanation that fits the most evidence with the fewest strained assumptions.
- State what evidence would change the conclusion.

## Fallacy And Coherence Audit

Check for:

- Equivocation: key term shifts meaning mid-argument.
- Circular reasoning: conclusion is smuggled into the premise.
- Straw man: opposing view is weakened before being answered.
- False alternative: reader is given two options when more exist.
- Hasty generalisation: broad claim from thin cases.
- Post hoc causation: sequence treated as causation.
- Appeal to authority: expert name used where evidence is needed.
- Appeal to majority: popularity treated as truth.
- Appeal to emotion or force: pressure replaces reason.
- Ad hominem: source attacked instead of claim evaluated.
- Slippery slope: chain of consequences asserted without mechanism.
- Composition/division: whole and part properties confused.
- Begging the question: contested premise assumed as settled.
- Appeal to ignorance: lack of disproof treated as proof.
- Weak analogy: irrelevant similarity does the work.
- Overstatement: conclusion outruns evidence.
- Unsupported value leap: factual finding jumps to "therefore should" without criteria.

## Certainty Calibration

Use strong language only when evidence is direct, current, corroborated, and sufficient. Use calibrated language when evidence is partial or contested:

- **Established:** direct, corroborated, current evidence.
- **Strongly supported:** multiple credible lines of evidence, minor gaps remain.
- **Plausible:** evidence fits but alternatives remain live.
- **Uncertain:** evidence is thin, indirect, stale, or contested.
- **Hypothesis:** a possible explanation that needs corroboration.

For forecasts or intelligence products, use `analytic-tradecraft` for probability vocabulary.

## Ship Gate

- [ ] The main question is explicit.
- [ ] Definitions and scope are explicit.
- [ ] Load-bearing conclusions are mapped.
- [ ] The warrant between evidence and conclusion is visible.
- [ ] Countercase is stronger than a token objection.
- [ ] Causal/general/statistical/analogical claims are tested.
- [ ] Fallacy audit passed.
- [ ] Certainty is proportionate.
- [ ] Limits and next actions are stated.
