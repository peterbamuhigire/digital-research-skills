# Essential Questions, Mental Models, and Strategic Logic

Use this reference when the output must read like serious research and analysis rather than polished source summary. It distils local critical-thinking, questioning, mental-model, design-thinking, and strategic-thinking books into operational checks. Do not quote the books; use the patterns as reasoning discipline.

## Core Standard

A serious analytical output must make four things visible:

1. What question is really being answered.
2. Why the evidence supports the conclusion.
3. What could make the conclusion wrong.
4. What practical decision or business action follows.

If the output cannot show these four things, it is not ready.

## Essential Questions

Before drafting, answer these questions in working notes:

| Question class | Ask |
|---|---|
| Purpose | What decision, action, interpretation, or investment does this work support? |
| Problem | What is the real problem beneath the stated topic? |
| Terms | Which words, categories, thresholds, or definitions control the answer? |
| Evidence | What data, sources, observations, or cases would change the answer? |
| Assumptions | What must be true for this conclusion or recommendation to work? |
| Alternatives | What are the strongest rival explanations, options, or objections? |
| Consequences | What happens next if the reader accepts the recommendation? |
| Ethics and fairness | Who bears the cost, risk, burden, or exclusion created by the recommendation? |
| Feasibility | Can this be done with the available time, money, people, permissions, and incentives? |

## Mental-Model Pass

Apply only the models that fit the problem. Record the result, not the model name.

| Model | Use it to test |
|---|---|
| First principles | Are we reasoning from fundamentals or copying convention? |
| Map versus territory | Does the model omit constraints that matter in the real world? |
| Circle of competence | Where does the evidence end and opinion begin? |
| Inversion | What would make the plan, argument, or policy fail? |
| Base rates | What usually happens in comparable cases? |
| Second-order effects | What happens after the immediate benefit? |
| Incentives | Who gains, who loses, and how will behaviour change? |
| Opportunity cost | What is displaced by this recommendation? |
| Margin of safety | What buffer exists if assumptions are wrong? |
| Survivorship bias | Are failed cases missing from the comparison set? |

## Design-Thinking Pass

Use for solutions, services, programmes, products, workflows, or policy recommendations.

1. **Users and jobs:** Who is the real user, buyer, operator, regulator, funder, or affected party?
2. **Context:** Where will the solution actually be used, and under what constraints?
3. **Problem definition:** Is the problem framed from the user's reality or from the provider's preferred solution?
4. **Options:** What alternatives were considered before converging?
5. **Prototype logic:** What small test can validate the riskiest assumption?
6. **Adoption friction:** What makes the solution hard to adopt, sustain, pay for, or govern?

## Strategic-Thinking Pass

Use for market, business, policy, organisational, competitive, or investment work.

| Discipline | Test |
|---|---|
| Pattern recognition | What pattern is visible, and what evidence distinguishes it from noise? |
| Systems analysis | Which actors, constraints, feedback loops, and bottlenecks shape outcomes? |
| Mental agility | What would the answer look like under an opposite assumption? |
| Structured problem-solving | Is the issue tree MECE enough to prevent gaps and duplication? |
| Visioning | What future state is plausible, valuable, and specific enough to guide action? |
| Political savvy | Who must support, approve, fund, operate, or not block the recommendation? |

## Business-Sense Gate

Use when research supports a proposal, business plan, commercial recommendation, investment case, programme design, or strategy.

- **Customer/value logic:** the target user or buyer has a real pain, need, mandate, or willingness to pay.
- **Market logic:** size, segment, access, competition, regulation, and timing support the claim.
- **Economic logic:** revenue, cost, margin, cash flow, funding, and payback are plausible.
- **Operating logic:** people, process, technology, suppliers, partners, and governance can deliver it.
- **Implementation logic:** sequence, owner, timeline, dependency, and critical path are realistic.
- **Risk logic:** major downside cases are named with mitigations or stop rules.
- **Evidence logic:** major claims trace to current, relevant, credible sources or are labelled as assumptions.

## Final Serious-Analysis Check

Before release, every major conclusion should survive this sentence:

`Given [evidence], and assuming [assumptions], [conclusion] is the most reasonable answer because [warrant], although [countercase/limit], so the practical implication is [decision/action].`

If the sentence cannot be completed without vagueness, the analysis needs another pass.
