# Audience And Action Forms

Use this reference after the reasoning protocol. It converts tested reasoning into the right structure, tone, and decision form.

## Universal Form Rule

Form follows reader action. Before drafting, name the reader and the action:

| Reader action | Best form |
|---|---|
| Decide between options | Recommendation brief, business case, options appraisal |
| Understand a contested issue | Analytical report, literature review, history essay |
| Act under uncertainty | Intelligence assessment, risk memo, scenario brief |
| Examine academic competence | Essay, thesis chapter, dissertation chapter, journal paper |
| Approve resources | Proposal, budget note, implementation plan |
| Learn a method or procedure | Technical note, SOP, guide |
| Be persuaded publicly | White paper, policy paper, thought-leadership article |

## Academic

Structure:

1. Research question or thesis.
2. Context and literature position.
3. Gap, dispute, or problem.
4. Method or interpretive approach.
5. Evidence-led argument.
6. Counter-literature or alternative interpretation.
7. Contribution and limits.

Tone: precise, attributed, hedged where evidence requires, direct where evidence is strong. Avoid "this paper will discuss" when a stronger claim is possible.

## Intelligence

Structure:

1. Key judgment.
2. Confidence and evidence base.
3. Competing hypotheses.
4. Indicators and warnings.
5. Implications.
6. Collection gaps.

Tone: concise, explicit about uncertainty, no rhetorical flourish. Preserve dissents and distinguish source confidence from judgment probability.

## Market Analysis

Structure:

1. Decision question.
2. Market definition and scope.
3. Demand, supply, competitors, customers, regulation, channels, and economics.
4. Evidence-tested drivers.
5. Options and tradeoffs.
6. Risks, assumptions, sensitivity, and next moves.

Tone: commercially concrete. Name actors, incentives, margins, constraints, and implementation friction.

## History Essay

Structure:

1. Interpretive thesis.
2. Chronological and contextual setup.
3. Causal argument.
4. Evidence from primary/secondary sources.
5. Historiographical debate.
6. Alternative interpretations.
7. Significance and limits.

Tone: analytical, not merely narrative. Avoid inevitability unless evidence supports it; distinguish cause, condition, trigger, and consequence.

## Business Report Or Proposal

Structure:

1. Situation.
2. Complication.
3. Answer or recommendation.
4. 3-5 reasons.
5. Evidence.
6. Costs, risks, constraints, owners, dates.
7. Ask or next action.

Tone: practical and decision-ready. Findings, conclusions, and recommendations must stay separate.

## Executive Brief

Structure:

1. Bottom line.
2. Why it matters now.
3. Evidence headlines.
4. Choices or implications.
5. Risks and decision required.

Tone: brief, specific, direct. Do not hide uncertainty; translate it into decision consequences.

## Policy / Legal / Technical Argument

Structure:

1. Issue and governing standard.
2. Definitions, jurisdiction, or technical constraints.
3. Evidence or authority.
4. Analysis against criteria.
5. Counterargument.
6. Conclusion and practical implication.

Tone: disciplined and criteria-led. Avoid broad advocacy when the form requires rule, method, or standard application.

## Authenticity And Realism Checks

- Does the output sound like it was written for this exact reader, institution, and use case?
- Are constraints named rather than wished away?
- Are next steps feasible in time, budget, authority, and capability?
- Are tradeoffs explicit?
- Are objections addressed in the body rather than hidden in a caveat?
- Is the tone proportionate to the evidence and stakes?
