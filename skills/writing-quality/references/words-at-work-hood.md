# Words at Work — Hood (2013)

A practical guide to business writing. Hood's central argument: most writing failure stems from applying academic or literary conventions to workplace writing. The fix is Plain English, reversed structure, and relentless reader focus.

---

## 1. Core Plain Writing Principles

Hood identifies two patterns that trap most business writers:

**Pattern 1 — The Complexity Trap:** Writers believe good writing requires longer, more formal words than speech. Hood's antidote: "If the person was sitting opposite me, would I say this to them? If No — don't write it."

**Pattern 2 — The Inverted Triangle:** Academic writing builds from evidence to conclusion (bottom-up). Workplace writing must do the opposite — lead with what you want the reader to know or do, then explain. Reverse the triangle.

**The Plain English Checklist (Hood's core list):**
- Use everyday English wherever possible
- Keep sentences to two lines maximum
- Use headings and lists to direct the reader's eye
- Use tables — a picture tells a thousand words
- Explain technical terms when necessary
- Use the active voice: "we did it", not "it was done by us"
- Use direct speech: "we will", not "the organisation shall"
- Use generous white space — the body language of the document
- Be concise
- Be positive: "when you send the form we will…", not "we cannot assist until you return the form"

**Three elements every effective document must have:** purpose, audience, context.

---

## 2. Sentence Craft — Length, Structure, Variety, Rhythm

- **One idea per sentence.** Long, complicated sentences often signal unclear thinking. Shorter sentences show clear thinking.
- **Two-line rule.** Keep sentence length to no more than two lines. Aim for 15–20 words on average.
- **Active voice.** Passive voice distracts the reader, slows comprehension, and creates ambiguity. "The board approved the budget" beats "The budget was approved by the board."
- **Use "you" and "we".** The first person brings the writer closer to the reader. Third-person constructions ("it is recommended…", "the preferred approach is…") talk past the reader.
- **Avoid split infinitives** when they create ambiguity or awkwardness — but not at the cost of natural rhythm.
- **Read aloud.** If the sentence flows well when spoken, it passes. If you would not say it to someone sitting with you, do not write it.

---

## 3. Paragraph Construction

- **One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph deals with a single point or issue.
- **Maximum three to four sentences per paragraph** in letters and memos.
- **Open with the main point.** Do not build towards it — state it, then explain.
- **White space between paragraphs.** Dense blocks of text discourage reading. Space invites it.
- **Paragraph as signpost.** Headings above paragraphs allow a reader who is scanning to understand the document structure at a glance.

## 4. Document Structure for Business Contexts

Hood describes four types of writing. Only one is appropriate for business:

| Type | Purpose | Suitable for |
|---|---|---|
| Academic | Demonstrate knowledge, reasoning | Essays, theses |
| Evidentiary | Tell the story exactly as experienced | Witness statements, incident reports |
| Literary | Entertain, move | Fiction, poetry |
| Workplace | Get things done | Letters, reports, emails, proposals |

**Business document architecture (reversed triangle):**
1. What do you want the reader to know or do? — State it immediately
2. Key supporting points — in logical order
3. Background and detail — only what is needed

**The Report (special case):**
- A report must lead with an Executive Summary (1–3 pages). Research shows only 10–15% of reports are read in full. If nothing else, the executive summary will be read.
- Executive summary includes: key information, possible impacts, conclusions or options, recommendations
- Structure in tiers: Executive Summary → main body (key points only) → Attachments (supporting detail)
- Reports fail for two main reasons: they provide information only without a recommendation; or the writer has not considered the audience

**Checklist 3 — Report writing stages (Hood's sequence):**
1. Why am I writing this? What do I want to happen?
2. Who is my primary client/audience? Who are the stakeholders?
3. What do I already know? Who else knows? Where can I find information?
4. Mind map the structure, check with your manager
5. Draft → circulate for comment → revise
6. Executive Summary — write last, concise description of why/how/what
7. Plan the finished product: format, copies, binding, distribution

**Letters (key rules):**
- People dislike long letters — keep to two pages or fewer
- First paragraph must state what the reader needs to know or do
- Middle = points, answers, questions in logical order
- Each paragraph = one issue, three to four sentences
- Complex detail goes in an Enclosure/Attachment, not the body
- Close with a next step: phone number, email, deadline

**Email (Hood's Seven Secrets):**
1. Keep it simple — one topic, Plain English
2. Make the subject line relevant and interesting
3. Limit text to one screen — attach longer material
4. Style and content — it is a business document, not a casual note
5. Time — do not assume instant reply; use the phone for urgency
6. Manage — turn off notifications; batch-read twice daily
7. Emotion — if upset, draft then walk away 10 minutes before sending

## 5. Tone and Reader-Centred Writing

Hood's central question for every document: **"What do I want my reader to know or do?"** Write this on a sticky note and keep it visible while writing.

**Reader focus principles:**
- Your audience comes to every document asking: "Do I have to do anything?" Answer this as early as possible.
- It is not the writer's job to make things plain to everyone — it is the writer's job to make things plain to the specifically identified audience.
- Consider what the audience already knows, how much detail they need, what language they will understand, and how they will respond to each idea.
- Document body language: a densely written page says "I'm complicated and I don't want to share my content with you." Short paragraphs, headings, and white space say "I want you to read me."

**On tone:**
- Positive framing outperforms negative: "Once you sign the form we can pay you" produces a better response than "We can't pay you unless you sign the form."
- Avoid jargon unless certain the audience understands it. Jargon signals that you are not interested in being understood.
- Use inclusive language. Check whether any word or phrase excludes a group by gender, culture, disability, or age.

**Context matters:** A document read alongside 20 competing reports will be read differently from a document read in a quiet meeting. Consider the reading environment.

## 6. Common Mistakes Table

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Long, multi-idea sentences | One idea per sentence; max two lines |
| Passive voice throughout | Rewrite as active; name who did what |
| Burying the main point | State it in the first paragraph |
| Formal, inflated language | Ask: would I say this aloud? |
| Unnecessary words/phrases | "in consideration of the fact that" → "because"; "we made the decision to" → "we decided" |
| Jargon unexplained | Define on first use, or replace with plain equivalent |
| Dense paragraphs without headings | Add headings; break into smaller paragraphs |
| No white space | Use margins, lists, spacing; unjustify the right margin |
| Sending sensitive content as email | Use attached letter or memo; remember emails are permanent and shareable |
| Academic structure in a business document | Reverse the triangle — lead with the conclusion |
| Telling everyone everything | Identify who needs what; match detail to audience |

**Unnecessary word/phrase substitutions (Hood's own table):**
- "A survey of volunteers in regional locations was conducted" → "We surveyed regional volunteers"
- "They came to the conclusion that" → "They concluded"
- "in consideration/light/view of the fact that" → "because"
- "henceforth" → "from now on"
- "herein enclosed is" → "I have enclosed"
- "pursuant" → "as we agreed" / "as a follow-up"
- "Written notification shall be made to this office in the event that ownership has been transferred" → "You must notify us in writing if you transfer ownership"

## 7. Editing Process — Self-Editing Checklist

Hood's Checklist 2 (adapted for editing use):

1. **Reader question:** Have I answered "What do you have to know or do?" early in the document?
2. **Planning:** Did I use a mind map or key-points list before writing?
3. **Headings:** Are there appropriate headings? Tables and lists where needed?
4. **Sentence discipline:** One idea per sentence? Could any be shorter?
5. **Information balance:** Does the reader have enough? Too much?
6. **Relevance:** Is all information relevant? Is the material in a logical flow?
7. **Draft review:** Have I shared the draft with a fresh pair of eyes?
8. **Body language:** Does the layout encourage reading? White space, lists, tables?
9. **Context:** Does the document orient the reader at the start?
10. **Voice:** Active voice throughout? Using "you" and "we"?
11. **Plain language:** No unnecessary jargon? Language appropriate for this audience?
12. **Decision:** If a decision is required, is it clearly stated?

**Read-aloud test:** Read the draft aloud. Where you stumble or find yourself paraphrasing, the sentence needs rewriting.

## 8. Uganda/East Africa Application Notes

- **Oral culture context:** Hood's "imaginary person sitting opposite you" technique resonates strongly in Uganda and East Africa, where oral communication is central. When in doubt, write what you would say.
- **Formal language trap:** Many Ugandan writers over-formalise to signal respect or professionalism — "herein enclosed please find…", "kindly be advised that…". Hood's principles are a direct antidote. Respect is shown through clarity, not complexity.
- **Reversed-triangle resistance:** Academic training in Uganda emphasises building to a conclusion (the academic pattern). Business writing for lenders, investors, or government audiences requires the opposite — lead with the ask or the recommendation.
- **White space and layout:** Business plans submitted to Ugandan banks and DFIs are more likely to be read when they use clear headings, bullet lists, and an executive summary. Dense text signals poor preparation.
- **Translation context:** When writing for audiences who read English as a second or third language (common across East Africa), sentence length and vocabulary simplicity become even more important. Hood's two-line rule is a minimum standard.
- **Memos and internal reports:** Ugandan organisations, NGOs, and government bodies make heavy use of memos. Hood's Plain English rules apply fully. The key difference: always state who must do what by when.
