# How to Write Essays — Shiach (2009)

Shiach's framework is built around three-part structure, reader-centred paragraphing, and rigorous avoidance of "waffle." Though written for academic essays, the principles transfer directly to persuasive business writing — proposals, executive summaries, investment cases, and grant applications.

---

## 1. Argument Structure — Building a Logical, Persuasive Case

Shiach's foundational rule: **relevance is all.** Every paragraph, every sentence, every example must answer the question that has been asked or the proposition being argued. Nothing else earns its place.

**Three-part structure — the non-negotiable framework:**
1. **Opening paragraph** — engage immediately, state your position, map the territory ahead
2. **Body paragraphs** — develop the argument point by point with evidence and illustration
3. **Concluding paragraph** — summarise, deliver your judgement, leave the reader with something to think about

This structure applies to every persuasive document: a business plan section, a funding application narrative, an investor letter, or a proposal. The proportions change but the logic does not.

**Planning before writing:**
- Before writing a word, identify the key words of the question or assignment — what exactly is being asked?
- Jot rough notes; then impose an order on them
- Decide on a paragraph structure: which point comes first, which follows, why
- In an examination, Shiach recommends 5–7 minutes of planning for a one-hour essay. In a business document, the equivalent is a brief outline before drafting

**The danger of misreading the brief:** Many writers produce documents that are "absolutely brilliant in their own way, but not an answer to the question that was set." In a business plan, this means producing a document that describes the business but does not address the funder's specific criteria.

## 2. Introduction Techniques — Hooks, Thesis Statements, Signposting

**The opening paragraph has three jobs:**
1. Grab the reader's attention from the first sentence
2. Engage with the topic immediately and specifically — no waffle
3. Map the territory: signal to the reader what you will cover in the body

**Recommended length:** four to five sentences for a standard opening paragraph.

**Waffle — what to avoid:**
Waffle is generalised, empty statement that could apply to any topic. Shiach's example of a waffling opening:
> "This is a very important issue and there are many different approaches that can be taken in regard to it. Many experts have considered this matter, but no one has come up with proven solutions."

This manages to say nothing in 45 words. Business-writing equivalent: an executive summary that opens with "The business environment in Uganda is complex and competitive, and many businesses face challenges in today's market."

**What to do instead:**
- Use some of the key words from the brief or question in your first sentence — this signals relevance immediately
- Make a specific, defensible point from sentence one
- Use signposting phrases to map your approach:
  - "In this essay I intend to explore…"
  - "This proposal will focus on…"
  - "In order to address [the question], I will examine…"

**Shiach's test for an opening paragraph:**
- Does it address the question/topic immediately?
- Is it specific — does it contain real content, not generalities?
- Does it map the ground to be covered in the body?
- Does it avoid waffle?

## 3. Body Paragraph Structure — Topic Sentence, Evidence, Analysis, Link

**One key point per paragraph.** Packing multiple points into a single paragraph confuses the reader and produces superficial treatment of each.

**Paragraph architecture (Shiach's formula):**

1. **Key sentence** — states the main point of the paragraph, usually the first sentence
2. **Development** — two to four sentences that give "flesh" to the key point: examples, evidence, specific illustration
3. **Intermediate conclusion or link** — a final sentence that summarises, draws an interim conclusion, and/or points towards the next paragraph

This structure has a direct parallel to the CAMPARI framework in business plans: state the issue → provide evidence → draw the implication.

**Example of the formula in action (Shiach's celebrity culture example):**
- Key sentence: "Celebrity culture is a well-established feature of our mass media."
- Development: specific examples of television programmes, viewing figures, the economics of celebrity attachment
- Closing sentence beginning "Thus…" draws a conclusion and points to the next point

**The word "Thus"** is one of Shiach's favourite signals. It marks a conclusion drawn from evidence. Business-writing equivalent: "This demonstrates that…" or "The evidence therefore shows that…"

**Assertions must be backed by evidence.** If you make a claim, the following sentences must substantiate it. An unsupported assertion is not an argument — it is an opinion.

---

## 4. Conclusion Techniques

**The concluding paragraph's function:** round off the essay emphatically; deliver the judgement the body has been building towards; leave the reader with something to think about.

**The conclusion must NOT:**
- Simply repeat the opening paragraph
- Introduce new arguments or evidence
- End weakly with a non-statement

**The conclusion MUST:**
- Signal its function ("In conclusion…", "Thus, the evidence points to…", "The bulk of the available evidence, then, suggests…")
- Refer back to key points from the body without simply retelling them — find fresh, concise language for the summary
- Deliver a specific judgement or recommendation
- End with a sentence that carries finality and gives the reader something to remember

**Signalling phrases for conclusions:**
- "In conclusion, I would argue that…"
- "Thus, a whole raft of factors led to…"
- "The bulk of the evidence, then, points to the fact that…"
- "As has been demonstrated…"

**The final sentence:** Shiach calls this the "rounding-off sentence" — relevant, specific, and note of finality. In a business plan, this might be the single sentence that crystallises the investment case or the call to action.

## 5. Transitions and Coherence Between Ideas

**Linking words and phrases** are the connective tissue of persuasive writing. They serve three purposes:
1. Signal direction to the reader
2. Create the impression that the argument is ordered and controlled
3. Move seamlessly from one point to the next

**Shiach's master list of linking devices:**
- Continuing a point: "Furthermore…", "Moreover…", "In addition…"
- Introducing a counter-argument: "However…", "Nevertheless…", "On the contrary…"
- Drawing a conclusion within the body: "Thus…", "Therefore…", "Hence…"
- Introducing evidence: "For example…", "To illustrate this point…", "As has been shown…"
- Moving to the next point: "Having analysed this aspect, I would now like to…", "Another essential feature of…"
- Qualifying: "While it can be argued that…, it is also true that…"
- Emphasising: "In order to emphasise this point, I would like to point to…"
- Summarising: "The bulk of the available evidence, then, points to the fact that…"

**Positioning rule:** Linking words and phrases need not open every sentence, but they must appear within the first sentence of a new paragraph. When "however" falls mid-sentence, it takes a comma before and after.

**Continuity test:** Read your paragraphs in sequence. Does each opening sentence pick up a thread from the closing sentence of the previous paragraph? If the essay can be rearranged in any order without consequence, the argument lacks structure.

## 6. Clarity and Precision — Word Choice and Concision

**Precision over generality.** Vague claims are the mark of a writer who has not thought through the argument. Every claim should be as specific as possible.

**Avoid the "waffle words":** "many", "various", "some people feel", "it has been suggested", "there are arguments both for and against" — unless followed immediately by specifics.

**Evidence over assertion.** When you make a claim, name your evidence. In a business plan: do not write "the market is growing" — write "the market grew at 14% annually between 2021 and 2024 (UBOS)."

**The close reference principle (adapted for business writing):**
In literary analysis, Shiach requires "close references" — brief but specific citations from the text. In business writing, the equivalent is specific data, named sources, concrete examples, and dated facts. Assertions without these are opinions, not arguments.

**Concision:**
- Every sentence should earn its place by advancing the argument
- Remove any sentence that merely restates what has already been said
- Remove any phrase that could be cut without loss of meaning
- Prefer the shorter word when two words mean the same thing

## 7. Application Table — Shiach Technique → Business Plan Section

| Shiach technique | Business plan application |
|---|---|
| Three-part structure (intro/body/conclusion) | Every section: executive summary, market analysis, financial narrative |
| Avoid waffle in opening paragraph | Executive summary: open with the business proposition, not background filler |
| Key sentence opens each paragraph | Each paragraph in market analysis, competitive analysis, risk section |
| Assertions backed by evidence | All market claims, financial assumptions, risk assessments |
| Linking words between paragraphs | Long narrative sections (operations, implementation) — signal logical flow |
| Conclusion delivers judgement | Funding request: close with the specific ask and repayment rationale |
| Final sentence with finality | Executive summary final line; cover letter close |
| Underline key words in the brief | When responding to a funder's specific criteria — answer precisely what was asked |
| One key point per paragraph | Financial projections narrative, CAMPARI responses |
| Map territory in opening paragraph | Section introductions: tell the reader what this section covers before diving in |

## 8. Uganda/East Africa Application Notes

- **Question-answering discipline:** Ugandan banks and DFIs publish specific loan application criteria. Shiach's discipline of "answer exactly what was asked" directly applies — read the criteria, underline the key requirements, structure your response around them, not around what you want to say.
- **Avoiding waffle in the East African context:** A common weakness in Ugandan business plans is opening with general statements about the economy, population growth, or the importance of the sector — statements that could appear in any plan. Shiach's principle demands that the opening sentence of every section names the specific business, the specific claim, and the specific evidence.
- **Evidence culture:** In environments where many business plans are based on projections rather than historical data, the "assertion must be backed by evidence" principle is doubly important. Label estimates clearly, cite sources for market data, and distinguish between proven facts and informed projections.
- **Oral tradition and argumentation:** East African rhetorical traditions often build argument through narrative and illustration before reaching the conclusion (similar to Shiach's academic pattern). Business writing for lenders requires the reversal: conclusion first, then evidence. Shiach's structure is a practical bridge between the two traditions.
- **Grant applications to DFIs and NGOs:** Many Ugandan DFI grant applications require a written narrative responding to specific criteria. Shiach's three-part structure (opening that maps territory → body that develops each criterion with evidence → conclusion that summarises the case) is exactly what evaluators are trained to look for.
- **Linking words and formal registers:** Ugandan formal writing tends to use transitions like "furthermore" and "in addition" freely, but less often uses the concluding signals ("thus", "therefore", "the evidence demonstrates"). Shiach's emphasis on drawing explicit intermediate conclusions is a significant upgrade to the typical Ugandan business plan narrative.
