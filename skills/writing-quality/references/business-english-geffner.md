# Business English — Geffner Reference

## 1. Business Writing Style Principles

### The Core Rule: Sound Natural, Not "Businesslike"
The first sign of an inexperienced writer is the obvious attempt to sound too "businesslike." Stilted expressions add nothing while making writing stiff and unreadable. The first step to successful business writing is to **relax**. Within the limits of standard English, say things in a regular way.

**Before:** "As per your request, please find enclosed herewith a check in the amount of $16.49."
**After:** "As you requested, I am enclosing a check for £16.49."

The second version is more personal, more efficient, and uses fewer words.

### The Four Cs of Business Writing
Every piece of business writing must be:
- **Complete** — include all information the reader needs to act
- **Accurate** — facts, figures, and claims verified before writing
- **Clear** — meaning is immediately apparent
- **Concise** — no redundancy, no padding, no unnecessary words

### Organisation Before Writing
Before writing, jot down: (a) the main point, (b) all supporting details — facts, reasons, explanations, appeals — then rearrange into logical order. A letter or report must be **logical, complete, and concise**.

### When to Stop
Resist the temptation to pad a brief message. If you have said what you need to say in three paragraphs, do not add a fourth. Do not reiterate thanks already given. Do not tack on a separate unrelated message after the main point — it dilutes the impact.

## 2. Grammar Rules Most Relevant to Business Documents

### Active vs. Passive Voice
The subject of an **active verb** acts. The subject of a **passive verb** does not act.

- Active: "We have received your order." (subject acts)
- Passive: "Your order has been received by us." (subject is acted upon)

In business writing, **active voice is almost always more effective**. It is more direct, more personal, and uses fewer words. Use passive voice only when the doer of the action is unknown, unimportant, or deliberately being withheld.

### Parallel Structure (Parallelism)
Items of equal grammatical weight must be expressed in equal grammatical forms. This applies to lists, bullet points, headings, and any series.

**Faulty:** "The proposal covers market size, competitive analysis, and how we will price the product."
**Correct:** "The proposal covers market size, competitive analysis, and pricing strategy."

Rules for common parallel constructions:
- "not only __________ but also __________" — both blanks must use the same grammatical form
- "either __________ or __________" — both blanks must use the same grammatical form
- "both __________ and __________" — both blanks must use the same grammatical form

In business plan bullet lists, every bullet must follow the same grammatical pattern (all noun phrases, all verb phrases, or all complete sentences — never a mixture).

### Misplaced Modifiers
A modifier must be placed immediately next to the word it modifies. Misplaced modifiers make sentences confusing or ridiculous.

**Misplaced:** "He had explained to his assistant why the company was in trouble on Monday." (Was the company only in trouble on Mondays?)
**Corrected:** "On Monday, he explained to his assistant why the company was in trouble."

### Dangling Modifiers
A dangling modifier describes a noun that does not appear in the sentence — often the implied subject "I" or "we."

**Dangling:** "Having reviewed the financials, the projections appear conservative."
(Who reviewed the financials? Not the projections.)
**Corrected:** "Having reviewed the financials, we believe the projections are conservative."

### Subject-Verb Agreement
When subjects are joined by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the subject closer to it. Collective nouns (team, board, management) take singular verbs in formal business writing.

### Pronoun Use
- Use "I" when referring to yourself (or the person signing the letter).
- Use "we" when referring to the company.
- Do **not** use the company name or "our company" as a substitute for "we" — it sounds stilted.
- Do not overuse the reader's name in the body of a letter; it can seem condescending.

## 3. Common Business Document Conventions

### Business Letter
**Structure:** Opening → Middle paragraphs → Closing
- **First paragraph:** State the main idea clearly and concisely
- **Middle paragraphs:** Develop the main point with supporting details
- **Final paragraph:** State the objective conclusion; include recommendations if called for

**Format styles** (Geffner describes five):
1. Full-blocked — all elements aligned left; most modern
2. Blocked — date and closing indented; common
3. Semi-blocked — date and closing indented; first line of each paragraph indented
4. Square-blocked — variant of full-blocked
5. Simplified — omits salutation and complimentary close; used for impersonal communication

**Avoid these archaic opening and closing formulas:**
- "Please find enclosed herewith" → "I am enclosing"
- "Attached please find" → "Attached is"
- "Pursuant to our conversation" → "As we discussed"
- "I beg to inform you" → "I am pleased to tell you"
- "The undersigned" → "I"
- "In compliance with" → "As you requested"

**Never end a letter with a participial phrase fragment:**
- Wrong: "Hoping to hear from you soon."
- Right: "I hope to hear from you soon."

### Memo (Interoffice Memorandum)
TO / FROM / DATE / SUBJECT header. Tone can be more direct than a letter. Still requires courtesy and tact. Itemise data (number paragraphs or use lists) for clarity.

### Email
Apply all four Cs. Additional rules:
1. Keep messages short — aim for one screen
2. Make the message easy to answer — state subject and required action up front
3. Never send anything you would not want forwarded or printed — emails persist even after deletion

### Business Report (Formal)
Nine components:
1. **Title Page** — title, author, recipient, date
2. **Table of Contents** — headings and page numbers; prepared last
3. **Introduction** — purpose, scope, method of research
4. **Summary** — concise statement of main points (for the executive reader)
5. **Body** — organised presentation of data; use subheadings, charts, tables
6. **Conclusion** — objective statement of what the report has shown
7. **Recommendations** — flowing logically from the conclusion; based on facts
8. **Appendix** — supplementary data (charts, graphs, forms) that would interrupt the body
9. **Bibliography** — sources cited alphabetically

**Three rules for any report:**
1. Cite your sources — let readers verify information
2. Date your report — facts change rapidly
3. Keep a copy for your own reference

### Business Proposal
A proposal is "a sales pitch for an idea." All proposals must:
1. **Define the idea** — state purpose, scope, and limitations clearly
2. **Be persuasive** — offer specific reasons and benefits in logical progression
3. **Anticipate objections** — address questions before they are raised (costs, credentials, alternatives)
4. **Explain how to proceed** — what action is needed, from whom, by when

Required structural elements:
- Title (terse but clear)
- Headings (divide argument into labelled sections)
- Budget/cost analysis
- Credentials of people involved
- Optional for long proposals: Summary, Appendices, Cover

Tone note: "While you want the logic of your idea to predominate, supported by specific facts and information, you must also convey your own enthusiasm for the idea. You must communicate a sense of urgency if you want your reader to act."

## 4. Tone Framework

### Matching Tone to Audience and Purpose
Business letters vary in tone from familiar to formal — but **all should sound natural**. Tone is determined by:
- Relationship to reader (colleague vs. senior lender vs. unknown regulator)
- Purpose (request, refusal, complaint, congratulation, collection)
- Subject matter (pleasant vs. unpleasant)

### The Positive Outlook Rule
Even when the subject is unpleasant, remain courteous and tactful. Building and sustaining the reader's goodwill should underlie nearly every communication.

- Add "please" and "thank you" where appropriate
- Replace impersonal statements with reader-aware ones:
  - "We have received your order." → "Thank you for your recent order."
  - "Checking our records, we have verified the error." → "Please accept our sincere apologies for the error."

### Delivering Bad News Diplomatically
Never accuse the reader. Attack the situation, not the person.

**Antagonistic:** "Because you have refused to pay your long overdue bill, your credit rating is in jeopardy."
**Diplomatic:** "Because the $520 balance on your account is now over ninety days past due, your credit rating is in jeopardy."

The second sentence avoids personal accusation, includes specific detail, and will be received more receptively.

### The "You Approach"
Make your writing reader-oriented. Sound as if you share the reader's point of view.

- "Please accept our apologies for the delay." (polite)
- "We hope you have not been seriously inconvenienced by the delay." (reader-oriented)

The "you approach" does **not** mean avoiding "I" and "we." Use them naturally. What it means is keeping the reader's interests, concerns, and perspective at the centre of every paragraph.

## 5. Word Choice

### Expressions to Eliminate from Business Writing
| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| according to our records | our records show |
| acknowledge receipt of | thank you for |
| as per | as / according to |
| at this time / at this writing | now / at present |
| beg to inform | I am pleased/sorry to tell you |
| deem | believe / consider |
| due to the fact that | because |
| enclosed please find | I am enclosing / enclosed is |
| forward | send |
| herewith / heretofore / hereby | (omit entirely) |
| in accordance with | as you requested |
| in the amount of | for |
| in the event that | if |
| kindly | please |
| pursuant to | following / as agreed |
| said (as adjective) | the / this |
| same (as noun) | it / them |
| subsequent to | after |
| the undersigned | I |
| up to this writing | until now |

### Redundant Expressions to Remove
| Redundant | Replace with |
|---|---|
| at this point in time | now |
| both alike | alike |
| cooperate together | cooperate |
| each and every | each or every |
| enclosed herewith | enclosed |
| free gift | gift |
| in spite of the fact that | although |
| past experience | experience |
| repeat again | repeat |
| whether or not | whether |

### Strong Verbs, Concrete Nouns
Replace verb + noun phrases with a single strong verb:
- "make a decision" → "decide"
- "give consideration to" → "consider"
- "place an order for" → "order"
- "perform an analysis of" → "analyse"
- "be of the opinion" → "believe"
- "handled the job in an efficient manner" → "handled the job efficiently"

Replace abstract nouns with concrete ones wherever possible. Concrete language is more credible and more memorable. "Our revenue grew by 34% in Year 1" is stronger than "We achieved significant financial improvement."

## 6. Positive vs. Negative Framing

### The Principle
Framing a message positively makes it easier to receive and more likely to get the desired result — even when the content is unwelcome.

| Negative Frame | Positive Frame |
|---|---|
| "We cannot ship before 15 March." | "We can ship from 15 March onwards." |
| "Your credit application has been refused." | "We are unable to extend credit at this time, but we look forward to reconsidering your application..." |
| "You failed to include a purchase order number." | "Please include your purchase order number so we can process your request promptly." |
| "We do not offer refunds after 30 days." | "We offer full refunds within 30 days of purchase." |

### In Business Plans and Proposals
Apply positive framing to risk disclosures, financial constraints, and competitive weaknesses. Acknowledge limitations honestly, then immediately pivot to mitigation or upside:
- "Although the market is competitive, [Company Name] targets an underserved niche in which the three largest players have no presence."
- "Start-up losses in Year 1 reflect investment in infrastructure that supports Year 2–3 growth."

## 7. Editing Checklist — Geffner's Revision Rules

Before submitting any business document, check the following:

**Style and Tone**
- [ ] Does it sound natural, not artificially "businesslike"?
- [ ] Have all stilted expressions been removed?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for the audience and purpose?
- [ ] Is it reader-oriented (you approach)?
- [ ] Are all bad-news statements framed diplomatically?

**Completeness and Concision**
- [ ] Does it include all information the reader needs?
- [ ] Has all padding and repetition been removed?
- [ ] Are there redundant words or phrases (e.g., "repeat again")?
- [ ] Has every unnecessary clause been cut?
- [ ] Does it stop when finished (no tacked-on extra messages)?

**Grammar and Structure**
- [ ] Are all verbs active (unless passive is deliberately chosen)?
- [ ] Are all parallel items expressed in parallel grammatical form?
- [ ] Are modifiers placed immediately next to the words they modify?
- [ ] Are there any dangling participles?
- [ ] Do subjects and verbs agree?
- [ ] Are pronouns (I/we/you) used correctly and sparingly?

**Format and Presentation**
- [ ] Does it have a logical opening → middle → close structure?
- [ ] Are lists and data itemised for readability?
- [ ] Are all sources cited?
- [ ] Is it dated?
- [ ] Has it been proofread for spelling, capitalisation, and punctuation?

## 9. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

### Register Norms in East Africa
Formal English is the norm for all business correspondence addressed to banks, government bodies, and institutions in Uganda and East Africa. The Ugandan banking and regulatory environment (URA, Bank of Uganda, KCCA) expects formal written communication — but Geffner's rule still applies: **formal does not mean stilted**.

Specific considerations:
- **Salutations:** "Dear Sir/Madam" is standard for formal institutions. Use "Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]" where the name is known.
- **Closing:** "Yours faithfully" when opening with "Dear Sir/Madam"; "Yours sincerely" when using the recipient's name. Both are standard across EA.
- **Titles and courtesy:** In Uganda, professional titles (Dr., Eng., Prof.) carry significant social weight. Use them in correspondence and in the plan's management section when listing key personnel.
- **Passive voice:** Ugandan government and legal documents favour passive voice. When writing the regulatory compliance section or addressing a regulator, a slightly more formal passive construction is acceptable — but executive summaries and funding requests should use active voice.
- **Directness:** The "you approach" is culturally compatible in East Africa. Demonstrating awareness of the reader's interests (lender's security, investor's return, funder's social objectives) is expected.
- **Avoiding sycophancy:** Geffner's warning against over-flattery applies doubly in EA business writing, where phrases like "I humbly submit" or "your esteemed organisation" have become formulaic and are viewed by sophisticated readers as padding.

### Common Ugandan Business Writing Problems (Apply Geffner's Fixes)
| Common Error | Geffner Fix |
|---|---|
| "I beg to submit this business plan for your favourable consideration." | "I am pleased to present [Company Name]'s business plan for your consideration." |
| "Due to the fact that we have experience..." | "Because we have experience..." |
| "The said project will commence..." | "The project will begin..." |
| Lists mixing verb phrases and noun phrases | Make all bullets parallel (all noun phrases or all verb phrases) |
| Passive throughout the plan | Convert to active; reserve passive for regulatory/compliance sections |
