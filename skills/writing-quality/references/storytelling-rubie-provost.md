# How to Tell a Story — Rubie & Provost Reference

## 1. Story Structure — The Three-Act Model

### The Gary Provost Paragraph (Master Story Template)
Gary Provost distilled the structure of 90% of all compelling stories into a single paragraph:

> "Once upon a time, something happened to someone, and he decided that he would pursue a goal. So he devised a plan of action, and even though there were forces trying to stop him, he moved forward because there was a lot at stake. And just as things seemed as bad as they could get, he learned an important lesson, and when offered the prize he had sought so strenuously, he had to decide whether or not to take it, and in making that decision he satisfied a need that had been created by something in his past."

This is classic Aristotelian dramatic structure. It applies equally to fiction and **narrative nonfiction** — including business plans, proposals, pitches, and founder origin stories.

### The Eight Story Elements
| Element | What It Is | Business Narrative Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| **Inciting Incident** | The event that upsets the status quo and launches the story | The market problem or founder's moment of realisation |
| **Goal / Prize** | What the protagonist is trying to achieve | The business opportunity being pursued |
| **Strategy** | The plan of action to achieve the goal | The business model and go-to-market plan |
| **Opposition / Conflict** | The forces trying to stop the protagonist | Competition, regulatory barriers, market scepticism |
| **Stakes** | What will be lost if the protagonist fails | The scale of the problem unsolved; investor return; social impact |
| **Bleakest Moment** | When everything seems to have gone wrong | The setback that proves resilience (if the founder has one) |
| **Revelation** | The important lesson learned | The insight that makes this business uniquely qualified to win |
| **Decision / Hole Filled** | The choice that resolves the emotional engine of the story | The call to action for the reader (invest, lend, partner) |

### The Inciting Incident Rule
Do not begin the story on "Wednesday" if the watershed event begins on "Thursday." Start the story at the moment the status quo is disrupted. In business documents this means: do not open with company history or founding date — open with the **problem that created the business**.

### Status Quo and Disruption
Every story begins with a functioning system (the status quo). The inciting incident disrupts that system and launches the story. For a business plan: describe the world as it is (the problem), then show how the business disrupts that status quo to create value.

## 2. The Protagonist Principle — Who Is the Hero?

### The Hero Question
In a business document, the hero depends on the purpose:
- **In a founder narrative / executive summary:** The founder is the protagonist — showing journey, conviction, and unique qualification
- **In a market problem section:** The customer is the protagonist — showing their frustration, the gap in the market, and the relief your business provides
- **In a funding request or investor pitch:** The business itself is the protagonist — pursuing a goal (market capture), facing opposition (competition), with high stakes (the investor's return)

Never confuse these. Mixing protagonists in the same document creates structural incoherence — the narrative loses focus and readers disengage.

### Deep Character vs. Surface Character
Rubie distinguishes two layers of characterisation that apply equally to real people in non-fiction:

1. **Outer character (observable):** How they speak, how they dress, body language — what anyone can observe
2. **Deep character (revealed under pressure):** The emotional and psychological traits that only emerge when circumstances force them to

In business writing:
- Outer character = founder's CV, qualifications, industry experience
- Deep character = what the founder did when the business faced a crisis; what drove them to leave employment; what problem they could not stop thinking about

**Deep character is more persuasive than credentials.** A founder who walked away from a safe job because she identified a structural market failure is more compelling than a founder who lists three degrees and a decade of corporate experience.

### The "Villain Is the Hero of His Own Story" Rule
Apply this to competitor analysis. Do not caricature competitors as incompetent or malicious. Describe them as rational actors with coherent strategies — then explain specifically why those strategies create a gap your business fills. A villain described accurately is more frightening, and defeating an accurate villain is more convincing.

## 3. Conflict and Stakes — Why Business Plans Need Tension

### Conflict as the Emotional Engine
Rubie: "Conflict is the basis of all drama." Without conflict, there is no story. Without tension, a business plan is a collection of facts the reader skims.

The business plan's conflict triangle:
- **External conflict:** Market competition, regulatory hurdles, supply chain constraints
- **Internal conflict:** Founder's trade-offs (growth speed vs. quality, debt vs. equity)
- **Philosophical conflict:** The status quo the business exists to challenge

Do not eliminate conflict from a business plan in an attempt to appear risk-free. Readers (especially lenders and investors) are trained to look for it. If they cannot find explicit conflict, they invent it from gaps in the narrative — and their invented version is always worse.

### High Stakes
"The stakes in a story have to be high." Life or death, lovers lost forever, implacable enemies — "something important we can all relate to."

In business documents, stakes translate to:
- Scale of the problem unaddressed (how many people suffer, how much waste occurs, how large the market gap is)
- Urgency (why now — what has changed in the environment that makes this the right moment)
- Consequences of failure (what the entrepreneur, their team, and their customers lose if this does not succeed)

Geffner's rule and Rubie's rule converge here: **say everything you can to elicit from the reader the response you want.**

### The Ticking Clock
One technique for sustaining reader engagement: introduce a deadline or time-bound constraint. "This investment window closes when [competitor/technology/regulation] changes" creates urgency that transforms a passive proposal into a live opportunity.

### The "Just Say No" Framework for Conflict
Throughout any compelling narrative, something or someone is saying **no** to the protagonist. Every "no" increases emotional potency. In a business narrative:
- Market sceptics said "no" — the founder kept going
- The bank first declined — the founder revised and returned
- Early customers pushed back — the product was refined

Each "no" that was eventually converted to "yes" demonstrates resilience. Showing the journey from no to yes (and explaining what caused the conversion) is far more persuasive than simply stating qualifications.

## 4. Scene-Setting and Specificity — Showing vs. Telling

### The Specificity Rule
Mark Twain (quoted by Provost): "Don't tell me there was a little old lady. Bring the old bag out on the stage and let me see her."

**Telling:** "There is significant demand for affordable housing in Kampala."
**Showing:** "Namutebi pays UGX 450,000 a month in rent for a single room in Bwaise. Her landlord raised rent twice last year. She has been on the National Housing waiting list for four years."

The second version creates a specific, memorable person whose situation readers understand and care about. The market data that follows this specific scene lands with more force.

### Scene-Building Elements
A scene must have:
1. A specific time and place
2. A character with a goal within that scene
3. Opposition to that goal
4. Movement — by the scene's end, something has changed

Apply this to case studies, customer testimonials, and problem-statement sections. A testimonial that describes a specific moment ("The morning I realised our bookkeeping system had failed...") is more persuasive than a generalised endorsement.

### The Show/Tell Hierarchy in Business Writing
| Document Section | Show (Specific) | Tell (Abstract) |
|---|---|---|
| Market problem | A named individual facing the problem with concrete details | "There is a gap in the market" |
| Founder narrative | A specific moment of realisation, a named obstacle overcome | "The founder has 15 years of experience" |
| Financial projections | Named customers, signed MOUs, named unit costs | "Revenue will grow significantly" |
| Competitive analysis | Named competitors with specific pricing, named weaknesses | "The competition is weak" |

Always prefer the specific. Specificity signals credibility. Abstract claims signal that the writer has not done the work.

### Description as a Function of Viewpoint
Rubie: "Description is a function of viewpoint." The same facts described from the customer's perspective versus the founder's perspective versus the investor's perspective produce entirely different emotional impact. Before writing a section, choose whose eyes the reader is looking through — and stay consistent within that section.

## 5. The Hook — Opening Lines That Pull Readers In

### What a Hook Does
A hook is not decoration. It does three things simultaneously:
1. Signals that this story has a specific, original focus
2. Shows the writer knows what kind of story this is
3. Makes the reader want to know more immediately

The Rubie/Provost definition: "Hooking a reader is about catching that reader from the outset: no explanations, no setup or slow windup to your story, but bang — straight into it."

### The High-Concept Hook
A high-concept hook summarises the entire proposition in one compelling sentence. It must be:
- Immediately understood
- Surprising or counter-intuitive
- Exciting enough to make the reader want to read the rest

Formula used in publishing pitches: "What if [X] + [Y]?" or "[Famous Book] meets [Famous Book]."

**Adapted for business plans:**
- "The problem of [specific unmet need] is a [market size] opportunity that [Competitor A and B] have explicitly declined to address."
- "What if smallholder dairy farmers in eastern Uganda could access chilling infrastructure without buying it?"
- "We are building the logistics layer that [major platform] cannot provide — and they know it."

### Opening Lines That Work (and Why)
**Catch-22** (Heller): "Yossarian was in love. The first time he saw the Chaplain it was love at first sight."
Why it works: unexpected, specific, raises an immediate question.

**The Honor Farm** (Westermann): "It began on a night when he thought he was finished, when all he wanted was peace."
Why it works: places the protagonist at their lowest point; creates immediate sympathy; raises stakes from the first sentence.

Adapted for a business plan executive summary:
- **Weak opening:** "This business plan presents a proposal for the establishment of a poultry processing enterprise in Wakiso District."
- **Hook opening:** "Uganda imports UGX 12 billion worth of processed chicken monthly from Brazil. [Company Name] will make that unnecessary within three years."

### The Query Letter Test (Rubie)
"If you can't write a compelling one-page query letter, how can you expect to write a compelling [document]? It is not so much what a query letter says that catches an agent or editor's eye, it's the way it's said. In other words, its dramatic structure."

Applied to business plans: if the executive summary does not hook a lender or investor in the first paragraph, the remaining sections will be read with scepticism rather than interest. The executive summary is the query letter — it must do its job in one page.

## 6. Voice and Authenticity

### The Problem of Corporate Voice
Corporate voice — impersonal, passive, full of abstractions — removes the human presence from a document and makes everything feel bureaucratic. Rubie's principle: readers invest emotionally in **people**, not institutions.

The solution is not to be unprofessional — it is to write with genuine conviction and specific knowledge rather than generic assertions.

**Corporate voice:** "The company's management team possesses extensive industry expertise and is committed to delivering stakeholder value."
**Authentic voice:** "Maria has spent eight years buying coffee at farmgate in Mbale. She knows why traders undercut each other on price and exactly when it destroys quality. That knowledge is this company's first competitive advantage."

### Consistency of Voice
Rubie: "The 'voice' of the story should not only be appropriate for the story you're telling, but should also be in harmony with the readers' expectations. Consistency is the key. If you write chapters in conflicting styles, you will no longer have the readers hooked."

A business plan that is conversational in the executive summary but bureaucratic in the financial section and hyperbolic in the marketing section signals inconsistent preparation. The voice should be consistent throughout — authoritative but accessible, specific but not boastful.

### Earned Enthusiasm
Rubie on proposals: "Make sure your passion for and interest in your topic shine through." But passion must be grounded in evidence. Unearned enthusiasm — claiming the product will "revolutionise the industry" without supporting detail — reads as inexperience. Earned enthusiasm emerges from specific data, named customers, described problems, and demonstrated knowledge.

## 7. Emotional Engagement Techniques

### Readers Care About People, Not Data
Rubie: "What interests readers is not so much what happens, but who things happen to. If they don't care about the characters, readers are not really interested in the outcome."

In business writing, every statistic should be anchored to a human situation. The statistic alone is forgettable. The statistic alongside a specific person's experience is memorable.

### Character Motivation Must Be Believable
"Make sure the character's doing something he wants to do, not just something you want him to do."

In business documents: the founder's motivation must be genuinely grounded in their background and experience. An investor or lender reads for authenticity. A motivation that reads as constructed ("I was inspired to start this company after attending a conference") carries less weight than one that emerges from lived experience ("After eight years as a produce trader I noticed the same problem recurring every harvest season").

### The Emotional Hole (Backstory as Engine)
Every protagonist is driven by a gap — something missing from their past that the story allows them to fill. In founder narratives, this is the origin story: the experience that makes this founder uniquely motivated to solve this problem, in this place, at this time.

The backstory should not be a biography — it should be the one experience that explains why this founder cannot stop, even when it would be rational to quit.

### Subplot and Theme Coherence
Rubie: "The subplot in your narrative is not just something a character happens to be doing... it is something that relates to the plot in some structural way."

In a business plan, the "subplots" are the secondary sections (operations, HR, risk) that must all serve and reinforce the central theme. A plan about a premium health-food company should have an operations section that reflects premium quality standards, a management section whose team bios reflect health expertise, and a risk section that demonstrates deep knowledge of the regulatory environment for food safety. Sections that do not reinforce the central theme weaken the narrative coherence.

| High-concept hook | 01-Executive Summary | One sentence that encapsulates the proposition and makes the reader want to read on |
| Protagonist | 01-Executive Summary | Decide: is the hero the founder, the customer, or the business? Choose one and be consistent |
| Deep character | 09-Management Team | Show what founders did under pressure, not just their qualifications |
| Stakes | 04-Market Analysis, 11-Funding Request | Quantify the scale of the problem unsolved; state what the investor/lender gains |
| Conflict | 06-Competitive Analysis, 12-Risk Analysis | Do not sanitise opposition — describe it accurately then explain the competitive advantage |
| Ticking clock | 11-Funding Request, 13-Implementation Timeline | State why now — what creates urgency for this opportunity |
| Show don't tell | 05-Target Market, 03-Products/Services | Use named customer scenarios, specific unit economics, real conversations |

| Status quo disruption | 02-Company Overview | "The world was like X. Then Y changed. We exist because of Y." |
| Call to action | 11-Funding Request | The "decision" — what you are asking the reader to do and what they gain by doing it |

## 9. Uganda / East Africa Application Notes

### The Specificity Imperative in EA Markets
Abstract claims in Ugandan business plans often arise from lack of primary data. Rubie's "show don't tell" principle demands specificity — but specificity requires research. The discipline of storytelling forces the business owner to talk to real customers, visit real sites, and obtain real prices. A plan that can name its first ten potential customers, describe their current workarounds, and quote their stated willingness to pay is a storytelling plan — and it is far more bankable.

### Founder Origin Stories in the EA Context
Ugandan and East African founders often have origin stories of extraordinary richness — rural-to-urban journeys, informal market experience, remittance economies, family obligation, subsistence farming backgrounds. These are not weaknesses to be hidden behind corporate language. They are the "emotional hole" that Rubie describes — the backstory that explains what drives the founder. Used honestly and specifically (not as sentiment), they are compelling evidence of market knowledge that no consultant can replicate.

### The Oral Storytelling Tradition as Asset
East Africa has a deep oral storytelling tradition. The business plan is a foreign written form — but the storytelling instincts that make a Ugandan entrepreneur compelling in conversation can be transferred to the written document. The hook, the protagonist, the conflict, the stakes — these are not alien concepts. They are the structure of every good story already being told around tables and in markets. The task is translation, not invention.

### Conflict and Face-Saving
Ugandan business culture values harmony and face-saving in interpersonal contexts. But in a written business plan, conflict must be acknowledged. Omitting competition from a competitive analysis, omitting risks from a risk register, or omitting challenges from a management section does not make the plan more polished — it makes it less credible. Rubie's framework gives permission to name the conflict because naming it and showing how it is overcome is what makes the story convincing.

### Cultural Adaptation of Stakes
Stakes in a Ugandan business plan often extend beyond financial return to community, family, and national economic development. These are legitimate and powerful stakes — and they resonate with DFI funders, grant bodies, and impact investors. They should be articulated specifically (how many jobs, which community, what measurable social outcome) rather than vaguely ("benefit the community"). Specificity is the rule, regardless of whether the stake is financial or social.
