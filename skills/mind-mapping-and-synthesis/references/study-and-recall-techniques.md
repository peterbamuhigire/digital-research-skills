# Study and recall techniques (analyst learning)

**Date written:** 2026-04-27
**Drawn from:** Tony Buzan, *Mind Map Mastery* (in particular the chapters on memory and recall, and the Solution Finding chapter); Buzan's broader study-skills work referenced through *Mind Map Mastery*. (Buzan's *Study Skills Handbook* EPUB the engine has access to is image-only and could not be text-extracted; method here is taken from *Mind Map Mastery*, which covers the same ground.)

This reference is for the situation in which an analyst is **learning a domain new to them** — a regulatory regime they haven't worked in before, a sector they don't know, a methodology they've never applied. Buzan's mind-map-centric study technique compresses learning time and improves retention. The engine uses it when:

- A research wave will hit a domain the analyst is shallow in
- The analyst is preparing to brief a sub-agent on a domain they're learning themselves
- A returning project has been dormant and the analyst needs to reload context fast
- The user wants a learning artifact (briefing pack, summary, study guide)

## The Buzan study principle

Buzan's repeated insight: **knowledge organised radially and revisited at spaced intervals is retained dramatically better than the same knowledge held as linear notes**. Mind maps are the organisation; spaced revision is the maintenance.

Concretely:

1. **Survey the territory** — TOC pass, headings pass, summary pass; build a *first* mind map from this alone, before deep reading
2. **Read and annotate** — fill in the mind map's branches and sub-branches as the reading progresses
3. **Re-organise** — after each major reading session, redraw the mind map (the redrawing *is* the learning)
4. **Test recall** — close the source, reproduce the mind map from memory; the gaps reveal what wasn't actually learned
5. **Spaced revision** — revisit the mind map at lengthening intervals (1 day, 1 week, 1 month) to consolidate

## The structured reading routine

Adapted from Buzan's solution-finding framework:

### 1. Preview (20% of time)

- Read the table of contents
- Read all section headings
- Read the introduction and conclusion
- Skim the index / glossary
- Build a **preview mind map** — central topic, BOIs from the headings, a few sub-branches from the intro/conclusion
- *Goal: orient the territory before any deep reading*

### 2. Active reading (50% of time)

- Read each section with the preview mind map open
- Annotate the mind map as the reading progresses; never just highlight in the source
- For each branch, ask: what is the **operative noun phrase** that captures this section's idea?
- For each connection, ask: which *other branch* does this section link to?
- *Goal: convert reading into structure*

### 3. Synthesis (20% of time)

- Close the source
- Redraw the mind map from memory
- Compare to the annotated version; the differences are the learning gaps
- Re-read only the gaps
- *Goal: convert structure into recall*

### 4. Spaced revision (10% of time, distributed)

- Day 1: 5-minute revisit of the mind map
- Day 7: 5-minute revisit
- Day 30: 5-minute revisit
- Before each revisit, attempt blind recall first; only then check the mind map

This cadence is folklore-level conservative; the literature on spaced repetition supports more aggressive intervals, but the conservative cadence is reliable for analyst use without scheduling overhead.

## Recall techniques the engine can deploy

### Blind reproduction

Close the source. Draw the mind map from scratch. The gaps and errors are diagnostic. **This is the single most effective recall exercise in Buzan's method.**

### Branch-by-branch interrogation

Pick a main branch. Ask: "what's at the third level here?" Recall before checking. Repeat for each main branch.

### Cross-link discovery

Scan the mind map looking for branches that *should* connect but don't yet have a cross-link drawn. Each newly-discovered cross-link is a learning event.

### Re-centre exercise

Take the mind map and re-centre it around a different topic. (E.g., a regulatory landscape map centred on "the regulator" → re-centre on "the regulated entity".) Re-centring forces the same content through a different organising frame and reveals which knowledge survives reframing (deeply learned) vs which collapses (memorised but not understood).

### Teach-it test

Explain the mind map's content out loud, as if to someone unfamiliar. Where the explanation stalls, the knowledge isn't yet portable. Mind-map nodes that can't be explained orally aren't really learned.

## Learning a domain for sub-agent briefing

When the analyst has to brief a sub-agent on a domain the analyst is also learning, the discipline is:

1. **Build the mind map first.** The brief writes itself off the mind map.
2. **Each sub-agent gets one main branch's worth of scope.** The mind map's BOIs are natural sub-agent boundaries.
3. **The brief includes the centre and the analyst's current best guess at sub-branches.** The sub-agent will refine.
4. **The sub-agent's output gets folded back into the mind map.** That's the cumulative learning artifact.

## When *not* to use mind-map study

- The material is genuinely sequential (a procedure, a chronology, a runbook). Linear notes are right.
- The material is mathematical / formal — the symbols and the steps are the content; mind maps add nothing
- The audience for the artifact wants prose; mind maps are upstream of that
- Time pressure is so severe that any structure is better than none — write linear notes and move

## Anti-patterns

- Highlighting text without converting to a mind map (highlighting is recognition, not recall — passive learning)
- Building the full mind map before previewing (no skeleton to hang detail on)
- Re-reading without blind-recall in between (re-reading produces the *illusion* of learning, not learning)
- Treating one mind map as the final artifact when the topic merits decomposition into sub-maps
- Skipping spaced revision — the structural learning fades without it
- Using a mind-map *template* instead of building one — the building is the learning
- Over-engineering the visual style at the expense of the structural work
