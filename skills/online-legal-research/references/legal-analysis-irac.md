# Legal analysis — IRAC

**Date written:** 2026-04-27
**Drawn from:** Putman & Albright, *Legal Research, Analysis and Writing*, Chapters 2 and 9–13.

IRAC is the legal-analysis spine the engine uses for any legal output. Putman & Albright (Chapter 2): *"Once the facts have been gathered and reviewed, follow the four steps of the IRAC legal analysis process."*

## The four steps

| Step | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **I — Issue** | What is the precise legal question raised by the facts? | A one-sentence question naming the rule and the disputed element |
| **R — Rule** | What rule of law governs the issue? | The statute, regulation, or case-law principle, cited |
| **A — Application (Analysis)** | How does the rule apply to *these* facts? | Side-by-side comparison of facts to rule elements; counter-analysis |
| **C — Conclusion** | What is the answer to the issue, given the rule applied? | A direct answer plus its limits |

## Step 1 — Issue

Putman: *"The first and most important step in the analytical process is to identify the issue. You must identify the problem before you can solve it. The issue is the starting point. If it is misidentified, each subsequent step in the process is a step in the wrong direction. Time is wasted, and malpractice may result."*

A well-stated issue includes three elements:

1. **The applicable law** (statute section, doctrine, regulation)
2. **The legal question** (what is being decided)
3. **The key facts** (the specific facts that make the question live)

**Bad issue:** "Did Mr X breach the contract?"
**Better:** "Under [contract law of the jurisdiction], does a builder's failure to install the agreed grade of tile constitute a breach of an express warranty when the contract document specified Grade A and Grade B was installed?"

Multiple-issue cases are common. One fact pattern can raise battery, negligence, and contract claims at once. List every plausible issue before narrowing.

## Step 2 — Rule

The rule is the controlling statement of law. Sources, in order of preference:

1. **Statute / regulation** — quote the operative section
2. **Case-law holding** — quote or closely paraphrase the controlling rule from the highest binding court
3. **Synthesised rule** — when no single case states the rule, synthesise from a line of cases (must be cited)
4. **Secondary authority** — only as scaffolding to interpret the primary

Each rule citation must include pinpoint reference (paragraph for judgments, section/sub-section for statutes).

## Step 3 — Application (Analysis)

This is where most legal writing fails. The application step:

1. **Decomposes the rule** into its elements
2. **Maps each element** onto the facts of the case
3. **Argues each disputed element** with reference to authority
4. **Includes counter-analysis** — the strongest argument against the position

### Counter-analysis (Putman, Chapter 13)

Putman & Albright dedicate a whole chapter to **counteranalysis**: the discipline of thinking through how an opponent (or the court) would attack the position. A legal analysis without counter-analysis is incomplete.

Counter-analysis covers:

- **Adverse cases** — cases that go the other way; explain why they don't control or can be distinguished
- **Adverse statutory readings** — alternative interpretations a court could reach
- **Factual distinctions** — facts that weaken the application
- **Policy arguments against** — the consequentialist case the other side will run

A position that survives counter-analysis is robust. A position that hides counter-analysis is fragile and unprofessional.

## Step 4 — Conclusion

The conclusion answers the issue, in the same terms as the issue was framed. It states:

- **What the rule says** (briefly)
- **How it applies to the facts** (briefly)
- **The likely outcome** (with its uncertainty acknowledged)

Conclusions in legal writing rarely speak in absolutes. "The court will likely hold..." "There is a strong argument that..." Putman's general guidance: be confident enough to be useful, qualified enough to be honest.

## "Is a case on point?" — case-law analysis (Putman, Chapter 12)

When applying a precedent, the analyst must show the precedent is **on point**. Putman's two-step test:

1. **Are the key facts sufficiently similar?** Identify the facts in the precedent that drove the holding; map onto the present facts.
2. **Are the legal issues sufficiently similar?** Identify the issue decided in the precedent; confirm the present issue raises the same legal question.

If both are satisfied, the case is on point and stare decisis applies (within jurisdiction). If facts or issues are materially different, the case can be **distinguished** — meaning the present court is not bound to follow it.

## Issue-spotting (Putman, Chapter 10)

Issue-spotting is the discipline of recognising what *legal* questions a fact pattern raises. Putman's checklist:

1. Identify each party's complaint or objective
2. List the possible causes of action / defences
3. For each, identify the rule and its elements
4. Match facts onto elements; the matched-or-disputed elements are the issues
5. Repeat across all relevant areas of law (one fact pattern can raise issues in multiple fields)

## Statute interpretation — the analyst's discipline

When the rule is a statute, application has its own steps:

1. **Read the statute** in full, including definitions and exceptions
2. **Identify the operative section** and break it into elements
3. **Apply ordinary-meaning** to each element first; then technical meaning where defined
4. **Check for binding judicial interpretation** of the section
5. **Check the legislative-history index** if the wording is ambiguous (per Putman Chapter 6 on legislative history)
6. **Apply maxims of construction** (e.g., *expressio unius est exclusio alterius*; *ejusdem generis*) only as last resort

## Anti-patterns

- Stating the issue too generally ("Did the defendant act wrongly?")
- Stating the rule from a secondary source instead of the statute or controlling case
- Skipping counter-analysis to make the position look stronger than it is
- Conclusion in absolute terms ("the court will hold X") with no acknowledged uncertainty
- Treating dicta as the rule
- Quoting a statute that has been amended without checking the in-force version
- Mapping facts onto rule elements only where they fit; ignoring the failed mappings
- Using policy arguments without citing primary authority that supports them
- Stating multiple issues but applying the rule to only one
