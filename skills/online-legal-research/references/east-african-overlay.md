# East African overlay — applying the methodology in Uganda, Kenya, and the EAC

**Date written:** 2026-04-27
**Drawn from:** Method derived from Putman & Albright, applied via the categories of authority used in East African common-law jurisdictions. **No specific case names, statute section numbers, or repository contents are asserted in this file** — those are details the analyst must verify in the live repository at search time.

This overlay translates the universal methodology of `source-hierarchy-and-authority.md`, `online-research-workflow.md`, `legal-analysis-irac.md`, and `citation-and-quoting-discipline.md` onto the legal systems the engine most often serves: **Uganda**, **Kenya**, the **East African Community (EAC)**, and adjacent Commonwealth common-law jurisdictions.

The overlay names *types* of source — never specific cases or section numbers — because the books the skill is derived from do not cover East African law and inventing concrete instances would breach evidence-discipline.

## The common-law inheritance

Both Uganda and Kenya are common-law jurisdictions tracing through the British colonial inheritance, modified by post-independence constitutions and codes. The hierarchy of authority therefore tracks the universal common-law structure (Putman & Albright, Chapter 1) with national specifics overlaid:

```
Constitution (national)
    ↓
Statutes / Acts of Parliament (national)
    ↓
Subsidiary legislation (statutory instruments, regulations, by-laws)
    ↓
Court rules
    ↓
Judgments of the highest national court
    ↓
Judgments of intermediate appellate courts
    ↓
Judgments of high courts at first instance
    ↓
Treaties, ratified into national law as the relevant statute provides
    ↓
EAC instruments where applicable
```

**EAC dimension:** The Treaty for the Establishment of the East African Community and instruments made under it create a supranational layer. EAC law applies in Partner States in the manner each Partner State's constitution and implementing legislation provides — verify on a case-by-case basis. The East African Court of Justice (EACJ) decides EAC-treaty matters; analyse its decisions as treaty-interpretation primary authority.

## Categories of primary authority — by jurisdiction

### Uganda (categories)

- The Constitution of Uganda (in force from 1995, as amended; verify amendments)
- Acts of Parliament of Uganda
- Statutory instruments made under enabling Acts
- Court rules of the Supreme Court, Court of Appeal (sitting as Constitutional Court for constitutional matters), and High Court
- Judgments of the Supreme Court of Uganda
- Judgments of the Court of Appeal (and the Constitutional Court constituted as a Court of Appeal bench)
- Judgments of the High Court (and its specialist divisions)
- Magistrate's Court decisions (limited binding effect; usually persuasive only)
- Uganda Gazette — official publication of Acts, statutory instruments, government notices, court appointments
- Hansard — parliamentary record (legislative-history aid for statutory interpretation)

### Kenya (categories)

- The Constitution of Kenya, 2010 (as amended; verify)
- Acts of Parliament of Kenya
- Subsidiary legislation made under enabling Acts
- Court rules of the Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, High Court, and specialist courts (Environment & Land Court, Employment & Labour Relations Court)
- Judgments of the Supreme Court of Kenya
- Judgments of the Court of Appeal of Kenya
- Judgments of the High Court of Kenya (including specialist divisions and the constitutionally-equivalent specialist courts)
- Magistrate's Court decisions (persuasive only above)
- Kenya Gazette — official publication
- Hansard of the National Assembly and Senate
- County legislation under the Constitution's devolution provisions

### EAC level (categories)

- The Treaty for the Establishment of the East African Community
- Protocols made under the Treaty
- EAC Acts and Regulations
- Judgments of the East African Court of Justice (EACJ)
- EAC Council, Summit, and Secretariat decisions where they have legal effect

## Online repositories — types, named only where the engine can verify them

The methodology does not require any specific tool; the analyst must verify which live repositories serve the jurisdiction at the time of research. Categories the analyst should locate before searching:

- A national gazette repository (for Acts as enacted, statutory instruments, official notices)
- A judgments repository (for court decisions; check whether it covers all courts and how current it is)
- A consolidated-laws repository (for statutes as amended and currently in force)
- A parliamentary records repository (for Hansard)
- The court's own website (for practice directions, court rules, latest judgments)
- The EAC institutional website (for treaty, protocols, EACJ judgments)

Examples named in widely-used legal scholarship include **KenyaLaw** (the National Council for Law Reporting's portal, Kenya) and **ULII** (Uganda Legal Information Institute). These are commonly relied on in Commonwealth East African legal practice. **Verify each repository's coverage and date-of-currency before treating it as authoritative.**

## Method translations

### Mandatory authority — what binds in each jurisdiction

A Ugandan court is bound by:

- The Constitution of Uganda (supreme)
- Ugandan Acts of Parliament in force on the relevant date
- Decisions of the Supreme Court of Uganda (subject to stare decisis exceptions)
- Decisions of the Court of Appeal binding the High Court and below
- Court rules and statutory instruments lawfully made

A Kenyan court is bound by the equivalent Kenyan-jurisdiction sources.

**Cross-jurisdictional note:** A Ugandan judgment in a Kenyan court is at most **persuasive**, not binding. The same is true in reverse. Commonwealth East African courts often cite each other and English authorities — but only as persuasive authority, never mandatory.

### Currency check — without commercial citators

In jurisdictions where Shepard's or KeyCite are not standard, currency must be checked manually:

1. **Statute currency** — locate the consolidated/revised version; check the gazette amendment index for any amending Acts since
2. **Case currency** — search the judgments repository for later judgments citing the case; read each to determine treatment (followed / distinguished / criticised / overruled)
3. **Constitutional provisions** — cross-check the amendment index of the constitution itself; some clauses have been amended

A case is "good law" if no later decision of equal or higher rank has overruled it on the relevant point. This is harder to verify without commercial citators; budget more time for it.

### IRAC — unchanged at the structural level

The IRAC frame travels intact. The change in East African application is in:

- The **Rule** step — pull from the local constitution / Act / case-law line, not US authority
- The **Application** step — argue using local authority; persuasive comparative authority (UK, India, South Africa, other Commonwealth) cited explicitly as persuasive
- The **Counter-analysis** — anticipate adversaries' use of comparative authority and the limits of its persuasiveness

### Citation discipline — local style

OSCOLA is widely used in East African academic legal writing. Court filings follow each court's practice rules. The invariants from `citation-and-quoting-discipline.md` apply universally; the surface form follows the local style.

## EAC-specific points

- **EAC Treaty interpretation** — EACJ has compulsory jurisdiction over treaty interpretation. National-court interpretations of EAC instruments are subject to the Treaty's primacy clauses — research carefully.
- **Direct effect / supremacy** — these are contested doctrines in EAC law and require pulling primary authority (Treaty articles, EACJ judgments, national-court decisions on the Treaty's status) before asserting any answer.
- **Free movement, common market, monetary union protocols** — each protocol has distinct enforcement mechanisms; never generalise across them.

## Anti-patterns specific to East African work

- Citing a US case as authority (it is at most persuasive comparative material)
- Citing a UK case as binding (English judgments are persuasive only in independent East African courts; never authoritative)
- Quoting "the Constitution" without specifying which one and which version
- Treating an EAC-treaty article as automatically having effect in national law without checking the implementing legislation
- Inventing case names that look East African (e.g., "*Mukasa v Republic*" with no verifiable docket) — strike on sight
- Using a foreign-jurisdiction citator's currency status as a substitute for local currency-checking
- Treating an EACJ judgment as binding in a national-court matter that does not raise EAC-treaty issues
- Searching only English-language sources where official Swahili / Kinyarwanda / French texts may exist (gazettes and court records may have language complications in Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi)
