# Misinformation and bias checks

Use this reference when the engine is evaluating a viral claim, politicized information stream, or high-noise news environment.

## What this adds

The standard credibility ladder and Tudor rubric tell you how strong a source is. This reference adds a fast filter for claims that are spreading because they are emotionally effective rather than evidentially strong.

## Five checks

1. **Fact vs opinion.** Separate what is being asserted happened from what the author thinks it means.
2. **Primary-origin trace.** Follow the claim back to the earliest reachable source, not the loudest repost.
3. **Bias check.** Name the source's likely agenda and the analyst's likely predisposition before adopting the claim.
4. **Virality is not validation.** A claim repeated across platforms can still be one rumor with many mirrors.
5. **Context check.** Screenshots, cropped video, and quote-snippets are leads until the full surrounding context is verified.

## Operating rule

When a claim survives only in commentary, reaction posts, or unattributed screenshots, it does not enter the corpus as fact. It may enter as a lead, allegation, narrative, or disinformation example, clearly labeled.

## Output tags

- `(fact)` — directly supported by a traceable source
- `(claim)` — asserted by a source but not yet independently verified
- `(opinion)` — interpretation or value judgment
- `(narrative)` — repeated framing that matters socially or politically even if not established as fact
- `(disinformation-risk)` — signs of manipulation or coordinated distortion detected

## Common anti-patterns

- Treating a fact-check article as the only evidence instead of opening the sources it cites
- Mistaking many reposts for independent corroboration
- Treating a partisan outlet's certainty as proof
- Adopting a claim because it matches the analyst's prior view
- Using "people are saying" as evidence of truth rather than evidence of circulation
