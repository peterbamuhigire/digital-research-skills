# Thesis Craft (Eco)

Encodes Umberto Eco's *How to Write a Thesis* as operational rules. Use whenever the engine produces a thesis, dissertation, or scholarly long-form argument. Pair with `source-synthesis-craft` (Trzeciak/Bailey) and `data-quality-assessment`.

## 1. Topic-selection gate (Eco's four rules, §1.4)

A topic is admissible only if all four hold:

1. Reflects prior studies and experience.
2. Sources are materially accessible (you can reach them).
3. Sources are manageable (you can read and understand them).
4. The methodological framework is one you have practiced.

Master rule: *"You must write a thesis that you are able to write."* If any rule fails, narrow or change the topic.

## 2. Narrowing discipline (§2.1)

Required progression: **field → topic → angle → secret title.**

- **Public title** is generic and registrar-safe.
- **Secret/working title** is the exact question being answered, written as a question.
- Always prefer a monograph to a survey: *"the more you narrow the field, the better and more safely you will work."*

Convert any "first-principles" topic ("Freedom", "Being") to a historiographic one ("The Concept of Freedom in Kant"). Original ideas emerge through an author, not in a vacuum.

## 3. Scientificity test (§2.6.1)

Output must satisfy all four:
1. Deals with a publicly recognizable, defined object.
2. Says something not yet said about it (or revises from a new perspective).
3. Is useful to others — future workers must take it into account at least in theory.
4. Provides elements to verify or disprove the hypothesis.

## 4. Source taxonomy (§3.1)

| Class | Examples | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Primary direct | Critical edition, original document, your own data | Required for the thesis center |
| Translation | Translated editions | Prosthetic — limited use |
| Anthology | Collected excerpts | First approach only |
| Critical / secondary | Books and articles about the object | Indirect — never quoted as primary |

**Absolute rule:** *"I should not quote my author through another quote."* Always check the original of any quotation that matters to your argument.

**Indirect-citation format** when unavoidable:
- To pin responsibility on the secondary author: `Sedanelli, p. 45, quoting Smith, p. 56.`
- To pin responsibility on the primary author: `Smith, p. 56, as quoted in Sedanelli, p. 45.`

## 5. Index-card system (§3.2.2, §4.2)

Multi-file, color-coded, abbreviation-coded, cross-referenced.

| Card | Purpose | Required fields |
|---|---|---|
| **Bibliographical** | Locate and re-locate works | One per work; full author, title, edition, place, publisher, date; library abbrev + call number top-right |
| **Readings** | Capture content survives loss of book | Bibliographic info, summary, verbatim quotes with page numbers, square-bracket personal comments in different color |
| **Idea / theme** | Cross-author thematic collection | Theme heading + references and short excerpts |
| **Quote (QT)** | Quote bank | `QT` marker, theme, author surname, original-language quote |
| **Connection (Conn.)** | Undeveloped link | Idea sketch, books to consult, deferred decision |
| **Disposal / question / recommendation** | Process control | Records what was rejected, open questions, leads |

Cross-referencing protocol: card color = type; top-right abbreviation = link to TOC section (e.g. `4.5.6`); same code on margins, on adhesive markers, on the work plan.

## 6. Working bibliography construction (§3.2)

Order of operations:

1. **Catalog reconnaissance** — subject catalog before author catalog.
2. **Reference-room sweep** — encyclopedias, periodical bibliographic indexes.
3. **Footnote-mining / pearl-growing** — photocopy bibliographies; titles cited most often = first-tier.
4. **Annotated bibliographies** — borrow the author's own ranking of fundamental works.
5. **Librarian consultation** — useful but never a substitute for your own discovery.
6. **Union catalogs and interlibrary loan**.
7. **New-acquisitions list** — catalog lags weeks behind.

**Coverage rule:** complete coverage of the *center* of the thesis (defined via the introduction); periphery may be lighter. A 10,000-title bibliography with a 10-title readings file is a thesis that "began extraordinarily well but ended very badly."

## 7. Provisional TOC discipline (§4.1)

Write the **table of contents, title, and introduction first**, before any chapter. The TOC is a **research instrument**, not the document's final index.

Mandatory triplet at start: title (public + secret), provisional TOC, fictitious introduction (written as if reviewing the completed work).

Every TOC must contain:
1. State of the issue
2. Previous research
3. Hypothesis
4. Supporting data
5. Analysis of the data
6. Demonstration of the hypothesis
7. Conclusions and suggestions for further research

The TOC will be rewritten "many times." If it is *not* rewritten, the research produced no new ideas.

Final introduction must promise less than the first draft and be more cautious.

## 8. Prose rules (§5.1, §5.2)

- **You are not Proust.** Short sentences. Repeat the subject rather than chain pronouns and subordinates.
- **You are not e. e. cummings.** A thesis on avant-garde poetry is not avant-garde poetry. Thesis prose is metalanguage.
- **Define before use.** *"If you do not know the definition of a term, avoid using it. If it is one of the principal terms of your thesis and you are not able to define it, call it quits."*
- **Begin new paragraphs often** — every logical period.
- **No ellipses, no exclamation points, no apologetic quotation marks.** Quotation marks reserve for direct quotes, titles, jargon-on-first-mention, mention-vs-use.
- **Do not explain ironies.**
- **"We" not "I"** — *"a writer says 'we' because he presumes that his readers can share what he is saying."* Or use impersonals.
- **Use the advisor as guinea pig** before submitting.

## 9. Ten Commandments of Quotation (§5.3.1)

1. Quote your interpretive object with reasonable abundance.
2. Quote critical literature only when its authority corroborates your statement.
3. Distance yourself before or after the quote when you don't share the author's view.
4. Author and source must be clearly identifiable.
5. Quote primary sources from the critical edition or canonical edition; specify both first and most-recent edition; clarify which one is cited.
6. Quote literary primary sources in the original language; provide translation in parentheses or note.
7. Reference must be unambiguous; never use "ibid." across an interrupting citation.
8. ≤3 lines: inline with quotation marks; >3 lines: block-indented, single-spaced, no quotation marks.
9. Quotes must be accurate — verbatim, ellipsis for omissions, square brackets for interpolations, `[sic]` for author errors, `[emphasis mine]` for added emphasis.
10. Quotes are testimony — every witness must be locatable and reliable.

## 10. Plagiarism gates (§5.3.2)

The card-discipline gate is the system: every readings card must mark verbatim quotes (in quotation marks **on the card**), paraphrase (no quotation marks), or personal comment (square brackets, different color). Apply at note-taking time, not writing time.

Three paraphrase tests:

1. **Length test** — honest paraphrase is usually shorter than the original.
2. **Closed-book test** — *"The most reassuring test of your paraphrases will come when you are able to paraphrase the text without looking at it."*
3. **Honesty-via-quotation-marks fallback** — when a phrase truly belongs to the source, mark it as a quote.

A footnote does not clear plagiarism. Verbatim copy + footnote = plagiarism.

## 11. Citation forms (§5.4)

| System | When | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Notes-and-bibliography | Humanities; heterogeneous bibliographies; classical sources | First mention full footnote; later: short form or `ibid.` (only immediate) |
| Author-date | Specialized field; mostly last 200 years; scholarly works | `(Chomsky 1965, 162)`; `1965a`/`1965b` for same-year |

Author-date eliminates most footnotes, compresses literature reviews, and survives late-stage edits without renumbering.

## 12. Anti-patterns

- Panoramic / survey thesis when monograph would work.
- First-principles theoretical thesis ("Freedom") at age 22.
- Topic in a language you cannot read.
- Skipping the TOC + introduction + title triplet at the start.
- Half-filing — splitting the index-card system with memory.
- Photocopying as a substitute for reading.
- Quoting indirect sources as if direct.
- Padding the bibliography with unread works.
- Proustian sentences in critical prose.
- Avant-garde voice in a thesis on avant-garde art.
- Scare-quotes, ellipses, exclamation points in argument.
- Explaining your own ironies.
- "Ibid." across an interrupting citation.
- Filler notes to keep numbering tidy.
- Same-fifth-word paraphrase plus footnote — plagiarism.
- Thanking the advisor in acknowledgements: "if he helped you, he has simply done his job."

## 13. Pride & humility (§4.2.4, §5.6)

- **Humility while reading**: record minor authors; *"the best ideas may not come from the major authors"*; even ideologically distant authors stay in the file.
- **Pride while writing**: no apologetic prefaces; *"on your specific topic, you are humanity's functionary who speaks in the collective voice."* You are the utmost living authority on the strip of reality you have studied. Behave accordingly.

## 14. Eight quotable principles (cite as rationale)

1. *"You must write a thesis that you are able to write."*
2. *"The more you narrow the field, the better and more safely you will work."*
3. *"Work on a contemporary author as if he were ancient, and an ancient one as if he were contemporary."*
4. *"The only absolute rule is that I should not quote my author through another quote."*
5. *"We must listen with respect to anyone, without this exempting us from pronouncing our value judgments."*
6. *"On your specific topic, you are humanity's functionary who speaks in the collective voice."*
7. *"Quotes are like testimony in a trial."*
8. *"The most reassuring test of your paraphrases will come when you are able to paraphrase the text without looking at it."*
