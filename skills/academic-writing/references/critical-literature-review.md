# Critical Literature Review

Use this reference when drafting or revising a literature review. It hardens the review against the common failure mode: a list of sources with no argument.

## Required Function

A literature review must:

- Define the research problem or question.
- Map the intellectual territory.
- Group sources by theme, theory, method, chronology, school, case, or finding.
- Show agreement, disagreement, silence, and contradiction.
- Evaluate source quality and method limits.
- Identify the gap, inconsistency, conflict, or unresolved question.
- Justify the current project's method and contribution.

## Six-Step Workflow

1. Frame the research question.
2. Find and evaluate sources.
3. Read for concepts, disputes, methods, findings, and limits.
4. Build a thematic or argumentative outline.
5. Draft sections with claim, synthesis, evidence, and implication.
6. Revise for gap logic and contribution.

## Review Questions

- What does this source add to the problem?
- Which sources agree, and on what grounds?
- Which sources disagree, and is the disagreement about evidence, method, definition, theory, or values?
- Which assumptions are shared across the field?
- Which populations, places, periods, methods, or data are missing?
- What evidence would change the debate?
- Why is this project needed now?

## Anti-Patterns

- One paragraph per author.
- Chronology where theme or dispute is needed.
- Web commentary substituted for academic or primary sources.
- Source summary with no claim.
- Gap stated as "little research exists" without showing search discipline.
- Method chosen before the literature justifies it.

## Ship Gate

- [ ] Review is organized by argument, not bibliography.
- [ ] Every section advances the research question.
- [ ] Source quality and method limits are evaluated.
- [ ] Counter-literature is handled fairly.
- [ ] Gap and contribution are explicit.
- [ ] Methodological implications are stated.
