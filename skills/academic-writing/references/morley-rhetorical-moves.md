# Reference — Morley Rhetorical Moves (Academic Phrasebank)

**Canonical source:** Morley, John. *The Academic Phrasebank: An Academic Writing Resource for Students and Researchers*. Kindle 4th edition, 2017. University of Manchester. Tier 1. Local extract: `extracted-books/academic-phrasebank.txt`. Built on Swales' (1990) CARS genre-analysis "moves" model and corpus-linguistic phraseology (Sinclair; Pawley & Syder).

**Reuse permission.** Per Morley's Preface: "*The phrases are content neutral and generic in nature; in using them, therefore, you are not stealing other people's ideas and this does not constitute plagiarism.*" The Davis & Morley (2015) acceptability rule still binds: a reused phrase should not have a unique or original construction; should not exceed ~9 words; should not contain more than 4 generic content words; and should not express another writer's distinct viewpoint.

This reference is a **lookup table** organised by rhetorical move. Use a phrase as a starting point; substitute domain content; vary phrasing across paragraphs to avoid formulaic prose.

## Section A — Rhetorical structure of a research paper / chapter

### A.1 Introducing work

**Establishing importance of topic**
- "X is a major area of interest within the field of …"
- "Investigating X is a continuing concern within …"
- "X has long been a question of great interest in a wide range of fields."
- "Central to the entire discipline of X is the concept of …"
- "The issue of X has received considerable critical attention."
- "One of the most significant current discussions in X is …"

**Time-framing the importance**
- "In recent years, researchers have shown an increased interest in …"
- "Recently, a considerable literature has grown up around the theme of …"
- "The past thirty years have seen increasingly rapid advances in the field of …"
- "In the last few decades, there has been a surge of interest in the effects of …"

**Identifying the problem / issue**
- "However, X remains poorly understood."
- "Despite this, the role of X in Y has not been examined in detail."
- "While considerable work has been done on X, much less is known about Y."

**Identifying the gap**
- "There is a paucity of empirical research on …"
- "Few researchers have addressed the question of …"
- "No previous study has investigated …"

**Stating the question / aim**
- "This study aims to examine …"
- "The purpose of this study is to investigate …"
- "The research question this paper addresses is …"

**Stating the significance**
- "An understanding of X has potential implications for …"
- "This work contributes to the field by …"

**Method synopsis**
- "Data were collected through …"
- "A mixed-methods design was adopted to …"

**Outline of the paper / thesis**
- "The remainder of this paper is structured as follows."
- "Chapter 2 reviews the literature on …"

### A.2 Referring to literature

**General descriptions**
- "A large and growing body of literature has investigated …"
- "Much of the current literature on X pays particular attention to …"
- "There is a relatively small body of literature that is concerned with …"
- "What we know about X is largely based upon empirical studies that investigate how …"
- "The academic literature on X has revealed the emergence of several contrasting themes."

**Historical orientation**
- "Early studies in this area concentrated on …"
- "The first systematic study of X was undertaken by …"

**Approaches**
- "Two broad approaches to X can be distinguished."
- "Recent years have seen a growing interest in approaches that …"

**Multiple-author references**
- "Several studies (e.g. Smith, 2018; Jones, 2020; Liu et al., 2022) have shown that …"
- "A number of researchers have argued that … (Smith 2018; Jones 2020; Liu et al. 2022)."

**Current knowledge**
- "It is now well established that …"
- "There is general consensus that …"

**Contradictions and inconsistencies**
- "The findings of these studies are inconsistent with respect to …"
- "However, other studies have found no such effect."

**Comparing / contrasting sources**
- "In contrast to Smith (2018), Jones (2020) finds that …"
- "Building on Smith (2018), this study extends the analysis to …"

### A.3 Describing methods

- "Data were collected using …"
- "Participants were recruited via …"
- "The study was approved by …"
- "The data were analysed using …"
- "Inter-rater reliability was assessed by …"
- "Ethical approval was granted by …"

(For the *justification* of every choice — see `academic-reporting-standards/references/methodology-justification-checklist.md`. This reference provides the *phrasing*; the rationale-chain discipline is in the other reference.)

### A.4 Reporting results

- "The results of the correlational analysis are set out in Table 2."
- "Table 1 presents …"
- "Closer inspection of the table shows …"
- "There was a significant difference between the two conditions …"
- "Of the participants who completed the study, X% reported …"

### A.5 Discussing findings

- "The findings of this study suggest that …"
- "Taken together, these results suggest that …"
- "These findings further support the idea of …"
- "Contrary to expectations, this study did not find a significant difference …"
- "A possible explanation for this might be that …"
- "These results are consistent with those of earlier studies …"

### A.6 Writing conclusions

- "This study set out to determine …"
- "Returning to the hypothesis posed at the beginning of this study, it is now possible to state that …"
- "The findings of this study have a number of important implications for future practice."
- "Further research might explore …"

## Section B — General rhetorical functions

### B.1 Defining and classifying

**Defining**
- "X can be defined as …"
- "X refers to …"
- "The term X is used here to refer to …"
- "In this paper, the term X will be used in its broadest sense to refer to …"
- "X is generally understood to mean …"

**Classifying**
- "X can be classified into Xi and Xii."
- "X can be categorised into Xi, Xii and Xiii."
- "Several taxonomies for X have been developed."
- "X may be divided into several groups: a) …, b) …, c) …"
- "It has become commonplace to distinguish 'Xi' from 'Xii' forms of X."

### B.2 Compare and contrast

- "Whereas X tends to …, Y is more often …"
- "X differs from Y in several important ways."
- "By contrast, …"
- "Unlike X, Y …"
- "In a similar vein, …"

### B.3 Signposting / signalling transitions

**Causality**
- "Therefore, …"
- "Consequently, …"
- "Because of this, …"
- "As a result, …"
- "thereby creating …"

**Section transitions**
- "The section that follows examines …"
- "Having discussed X, the next section addresses Y."
- "Returning briefly to …"
- "Turning now to the experimental evidence on …"

**Contrast**
- "However, …"
- "In contrast, …"
- "On the other hand, …"
- "Conversely, …"
- "Despite this, …"

**Building argument**
- "Moreover, …"
- "Furthermore, …"
- "In addition, …"
- "What is more, …"
- "A further point is that …"

### B.4 Giving examples as support

- "For example, …"
- "For instance, …"
- "An illustration of this is …"
- "A case in point is …"

### B.5 Describing trends

- "There has been a steady rise in …"
- "X declined sharply between Y and Z."
- "Y has fluctuated over the period."
- "X reached a peak in Y."

### B.6 Describing quantities

- "A small number of …"
- "The vast majority of …"
- "Approximately half of …"
- "A significant proportion of …"

### B.7 Explaining causality

- "X may be attributed to …"
- "X is likely to be a consequence of …"
- "One reason for this is that …"
- "This may be due in part to …"

### B.8 Writing the past (literature, methods, history)

- "Smith (2018) reported that …"
- "Earlier work demonstrated that …"
- "Until recently, it was thought that …"

## Section C — Notes on academic style

Morley's eight-feature style summary (pp. 122–124) captured as a checklist for the engine:

- [ ] **Evidence-based.** Every claim grounded in data or cited prior work.
- [ ] **Cautious.** Hedge appropriately; over-certainty is a red flag (see `morley-reporting-verbs-and-hedges.md`).
- [ ] **Formal.** Avoid contractions ("it's", "don't"), colloquialisms, slang.
- [ ] **Impersonal.** Avoid first / second person ("you", "I", "we") outside disciplines that license it.
- [ ] **Precise.** Choose technical / classical-origin vocabulary over everyday register.
- [ ] **Concise.** Avoid wordiness; one idea per sentence.
- [ ] **Objective.** No emotive language; no rhetorical questions.
- [ ] **Structured.** Paragraph = topic sentence + evidence + linking sentence.

**Register substitutions Morley flags** (avoid → prefer): a lot of → considerable; big → significant; get rid of → eradicate / eliminate; story → anecdote / account; thing → object / phenomenon / element; way of doing → method / approach; worry → concern.
