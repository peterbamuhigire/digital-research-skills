# Academic source mining

Journalism dates fast. Statute changes. Academic literature is the slowest-moving but highest-credibility tier — and most research projects under-mine it.

- [Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/)
- [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/)

## Decision rules

- **Always include ≥5 academic sources** in any non-trivial research project.
- **Cite by formal citation, not just URL.** Author, title, journal, volume, year. Year is non-negotiable.
- **Read abstracts, at minimum.** Don't cite a paper from its title alone.
- **Methodology matters.** A study with n=104 in three Morogoro universities is useful but not the same as n=10,000 nationwide. Note sample sizes.
- **Identify the academic gap.** Most EA research domains have published gaps you can name — they often become the report's most credible flagged finding.

## Output target

`<cohort>/research/academic-publications.md` — separated from general sources, formatted for citation in pitch decks / grants / academic writing.

## Anti-patterns

- Citing ResearchGate as if it were a source itself — it's a host
- Listing journal URLs without author / year / methodology
- Stopping at search-engine snippets without retrieving the abstract
- Treating one dissertation as if it were a peer-reviewed journal article
- Ignoring grey literature (working papers, DFI reports) — often where the freshest data lives
