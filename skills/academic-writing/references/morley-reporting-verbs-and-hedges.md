# Reference — Morley Reporting Verbs and Hedges

**Canonical source:** Morley, John. *The Academic Phrasebank* (Kindle 4e, 2017), University of Manchester. Tier 1. Local extract: `extracted-books/academic-phrasebank.txt`.

This reference codifies two of Morley's most consequential disciplines: (1) **reporting-verb selection** (the verb the writer chooses signals stance toward the cited work) and (2) **hedging** (the writer's calibration of certainty to the evidence). Both are non-negotiable for any academic artefact at Ivy / Oxbridge / LSE-equivalent quality.

---

## Part 1 — Reporting verbs by stance

The verb chosen to introduce a citation is not neutral. It signals the writer's stance toward the cited work and the strength the writer is willing to claim. Morley's three-tier register:

### Tier A — Neutral / descriptive (the writer reports without endorsing)

state · describe · report · observe · note · present · find · show · identify · examine · investigate · analyse · use · employ · conduct · carry out

**Sentence frames**
- "It has been **noted** that …"
- "Previous studies of X have **reported** …"
- "Smith (2018) **describes** how …"
- "The authors **examine** …"

**Use when:** introducing background or context where no stance-taking is warranted; in literature-review summarisation; when the cited work's findings are stable and uncontroversial.

### Tier B — Tentative / interpretive (the writer signals provisional weight)

suggest · indicate · propose · imply · postulate · hypothesise · assume · believe · think · appear to · seem to

**Sentence frames**
- "Smith (2018) **suggests** that …"
- "It has been **suggested** that …"
- "The findings **appear to indicate** …"
- "Jones (2020) **proposes** a model in which …"

**Use when:** the cited claim is partially supported, contested, or one of several plausible accounts; when introducing the writer's own emerging interpretation; when the evidence does not warrant a stronger verb.

### Tier C — Strong / evaluative (the writer signals confidence in the cited claim)

argue · claim · contend · assert · maintain · demonstrate · establish · prove · confirm · show conclusively · reveal · highlight · emphasise

**Sentence frames**
- "Smith (1998) **argues** that …"
- "It has been **demonstrated** that …"
- "Jones **conclusively shows** that …"
- "A number of studies have **established** that …"

**Use when:** the claim is well-evidenced and the writer endorses the strength; when a deliberate contrast with weaker prior work is intended; sparingly — over-use of strong verbs erodes credibility.

### Tier D — Critical-stance verbs (the writer signals disagreement or qualification)

question · challenge · contest · criticise · dispute · refute · take issue with · call into question · overlook · fail to acknowledge

**Sentence frames**
- "Jones (2020) **challenges** Smith's claim that …"
- "These findings **call into question** …"
- "The study **fails to acknowledge** …"
- "Critics have **disputed** this account on the grounds that …"

**Use when:** in a literature review section that adjudicates between positions; in the discussion when prior literature does not survive comparison with current findings.

### Selection discipline

| Mistake | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| All verbs in one tier | Either bland (all neutral) or hectoring (all strong) | Vary across paragraphs; reserve Tier C for genuine endorsement |
| Strong verb on weak evidence | "Smith (2018) **proves** that …" when Smith offers correlational evidence | Demote to "Smith finds / suggests / reports" |
| Tier B verb on settled fact | "It has been **suggested** that water freezes at 0°C" | Use Tier A: "Water freezes at 0°C." |
| No critical verbs in a contested literature | All Tier A/B; no engagement with disagreement | Add Tier D where the field genuinely disagrees |

## Part 2 — Hedging devices

Morley, on hedging discipline (p. 85): "*Analysis of research reports have shown that discussion sections tend to be particularly rich in hedging devices, particularly where writers are offering explanations for findings.*"

Hedges calibrate certainty to evidence. Under-hedging signals over-confidence (examiner red flag). Over-hedging signals timidity or evasion. The discipline is **calibration**, not omission.

### Distancing constructions

The writer reports a proposition without endorsing it as their own position.

- "It is **thought** that …"
- "It is **believed** that …"
- "It has been **reported** that …"
- "It is a **widely held view** that …"
- "It has commonly been **assumed** that …"
- "There is **some evidence** to suggest that …"
- "There is a **growing body of evidence** to suggest that …"

### Cautious explanations of results

For the discussion section. The writer offers an explanation while signalling that other explanations remain plausible.

- "This inconsistency **may be due to** …"
- "This discrepancy **could be attributed to** …"
- "**A possible explanation** for this might be that …"
- "It **seems possible** that these results are due to …"
- "There are **several possible explanations** for this result."

### Cautious implications

- "The findings of this study **suggest that** …"
- "These results **would seem to suggest** that …"
- "These initial results **are suggestive of** a link between X and Y."
- "There would therefore **seem to be** a definite need for …"

### Avoiding over-generalisation

- "X is **generally assumed** to play a role in …"
- "Studies which show no effect are **not generally** published."
- "Generally accepted methods for X include: …"
- Modal stacks: often / generally / frequently / sometimes / occasionally / nearly always.

### Cautious about future

- "Severe weather **may / could / might / is likely to / will probably / will almost certainly** become more common in the future."
- "There is a **strong possibility** that the situation will improve …"

(Note: for forward-looking judgments at the level of estimative discipline, escalate to the Sherman Kent / ODNI lexicon in `analytic-tradecraft/references/kent-estimative-probability.md` — verbal term + numeric band.)

### Advising cautious interpretation of own findings

- "These findings **cannot be extrapolated** to all patients."
- "These results therefore **need to be interpreted with caution**."
- "However, with a small sample size, **caution must be applied** …"
- "These findings **should be interpreted in the light of** the limitations described in §X."

### The hedging dial — calibrating intensity

For any claim in the discussion, the writer chooses a hedge intensity that matches the evidence:

| Evidence strength | Hedge intensity | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Strong, replicated | None / minimal | "X is …" |
| Strong, single study | Light hedge | "X appears to …" / "These results indicate …" |
| Suggestive | Moderate hedge | "These findings suggest that …" / "There is some evidence that …" |
| Tentative | Strong hedge | "It seems possible that …" / "These initial results are suggestive of …" |
| Speculative | Maximum hedge + explicit caveat | "One possible interpretation is …; this would need to be tested in further work." |

**Anti-pattern.** Hedging the same intensity across all claims regardless of evidence strength. Examiners and reviewers notice. Calibrate per claim.

## Part 3 — The plagiarism-prevention guardrail

Morley's reuse-acceptability rule (Davis & Morley, 2015), p. 4 of the Phrasebank:

> *Reusable phrases should not have a unique or original construction; should not express a clear point of view of another writer; depending on the phrase, may be up to nine words in length; beyond this 'acceptability' declines; may contain up to four generic content words.*

Engine rule:

- A phrase from this reference may be reused **as-is** if it is ≤ 9 words and ≤ 4 generic content words.
- A phrase that is longer or that carries unique construction must be **paraphrased**.
- A phrase that expresses another writer's distinct viewpoint is **never reusable** as scaffolding — it is a quotation and must be cited.

## Part 4 — Anti-patterns Morley catalogs

- **Personal pronouns as default voice** ("you", "I", "we") outside disciplines that license them.
- **Contracted forms** ("it's", "don't", "isn't") — disallowed except when transcribing speech.
- **Rhetorical questions** to introduce ideas — replace with declarative statements.
- **Everyday register** — substitute classical-origin equivalents (see `morley-rhetorical-moves.md` Part C).
- **Over-generalisation** — claims of certainty where exceptions plausibly exist.
- **Excessive nominalisation** (Morley, p. 124: "There is an argument that too much nominalisation should be discouraged.")
- **Formulaic over-use** — phrases require "a certain amount of creativity and adaptation" (Preface).
- **Over-hedging.** Hedge calibrated to evidence, not deployed everywhere as cover.
