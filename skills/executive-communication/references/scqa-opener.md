# Reference — SCQA Opener

**Source:** the SCQA frame is articulated in Minto, *The Pyramid Principle*. Used universally across consulting and management-writing literature.

SCQA = **S**ituation → **C**omplication → **Q**uestion → **A**nswer.

It is the introduction of any pyramid-structured artefact. Done well, it earns the reader's attention by minute one and lands the governing thought before the reader can disagree with the framing.

## The four moves

1. **Situation.** A statement the reader already knows and accepts. The point of the Situation is to put the reader and writer in the same world. Often one sentence; rarely more than three.
2. **Complication.** Something has changed, broken, or come into question — and the reader knows it. The Complication is the trigger for the document existing. One sentence is best; never more than two.
3. **Question.** The implicit question the Complication raises. Often phrased explicitly ("Should we…?", "How do we…?", "Why is X happening?"); sometimes left implicit when obvious. One sentence.
4. **Answer.** The governing thought of the document, in one sentence. This is the apex of the pyramid. Everything below the SCQA serves to defend this sentence.

## Worked example — strategy memo

> **Situation.** Acme Insurance has grown East African gross written premium by 14% CAGR over five years and now leads the Kenya and Tanzania motor segments. **Complication.** Three new digital-first competitors entered Kenya in 2025; two of them now offer instant-bind motor at 22% lower price. **Question.** Should Acme cut prices, launch a digital-first sub-brand, or hold? **Answer.** Acme should launch a separate digital-first sub-brand within nine months and hold prices on the legacy book; cutting prices on the legacy book would destroy ~KES 1.4 bn in annual margin without slowing the new entrants.

The Answer is the governing thought. The rest of the document defends "launch the sub-brand AND hold legacy prices" against the sceptic who would propose any other path.

## Three SCQA variants

| Variant | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Standard SCQA** | Decision-maker who needs to be brought into context (board meeting, new committee). |
| **Direct (Answer / Situation / Complication / Question)** | Decision-ready audience who will not tolerate setup. Bain "answer-first" deck convention. The Answer comes first; the SCQ follows as justification. |
| **Implicit Q (Situation / Complication / Answer)** | The Question is so obvious it doesn't need stating. Used in cover memos and short briefings. |

For an executive summary on a one-pager, use **Direct** unless the reader genuinely doesn't know the situation.

## Tests

The opener is correct when:

- [ ] The Situation is something the reader already knows and accepts. (If the reader could push back on the Situation, it's not a Situation; it's a claim.)
- [ ] The Complication is the actual reason the document exists. (If the document would exist anyway, the Complication is wrong.)
- [ ] The Question is the question the Complication raises — not the question the writer wants to answer. These are not always the same; if not, the document is misaligned with the reader.
- [ ] The Answer is one sentence and is the governing thought of the rest of the document.
- [ ] Total opener length: 4–8 sentences for an executive summary; up to a paragraph for a long-form report.

## Anti-patterns

- **Background-section bloat.** Three paragraphs of context before the Complication. The reader has already left.
- **Two Complications.** "Three competitors entered AND interest rates rose." If the document needs both, it has two governing thoughts; split it.
- **Question that isn't asked by the Complication.** The writer asks the question they want to answer rather than the question the audience would ask. Re-anchor on the audience.
- **Answer-as-question.** "We should consider whether to launch a sub-brand" is not an Answer; it is a deferral. Commit to a sentence that is a position.
- **SCQA-then-restart.** The opener gives an Answer; the body doesn't defend it; a second governing thought emerges later. Pick one.
