# Reference — Action Titles

**Source:** McKinsey internal slide-construction discipline. Documented in Rasiel, *The McKinsey Way* (1999), and reproduced in McKinsey-alumni training material and consulting-prep texts (Tier 1 / 2).

## The rule

Every section heading, slide title, and table caption is a **complete declarative sentence stating the takeaway**.

Topic labels are wrong. Action titles are right.

| Topic label (wrong) | Action title (right) |
|---|---|
| "Market overview" | "The East African motor-insurance market is consolidating around three players." |
| "Methodology" | "We sized the market top-down via regulator filings and bottom-up via dealership receipts; the two estimates agree within 6%." |
| "Findings" | "Three of five hostel cohorts share the same scam pattern; product addressing it captures the largest reachable population." |
| "Cost analysis" | "Building in-house costs 3.2× outsourcing over a 24-month horizon, even after counting capability retention." |
| "Recommendation" | "Launch the digital sub-brand within 9 months; hold legacy pricing." |

## Why this matters

The skim-path test (`references/pyramid-principle.md`) only works if the headings carry the argument. If the headings are topics, the reader has to enter the body to know what the section says — which defeats the purpose of the pyramid.

Senior readers do not read documents linearly. They scan headings, drop into one or two sections, and decide. Action titles let them decide correctly without reading the whole document.

## How to write an action title

1. **Write the section first.** Don't set the title until you know what the section concludes.
2. **Ask: what is the one sentence I would say if I had only one sentence?** That is the title.
3. **Make it specific.** Numbers and proper nouns where possible. "Three players" beats "few players"; "by 2028" beats "soon".
4. **Make it a complete sentence.** Subject + verb + object. No fragments.
5. **Test it on its own.** Read only the title; does it stand up? If yes, ship.

## The five-second test

Show only the title to a colleague who has not read the document. Ask: "what does this section say?" If they can answer, the title is right. If they can only describe the topic, rewrite.

## Action titles for charts and tables

The rule extends to chart titles and table captions:

- **Chart title (wrong):** "Market share by player, 2020–2026"
- **Chart title (right):** "Acme's share has fallen 11pp since 2022; two new entrants captured most of it"

The chart title says what the chart shows. The chart shows the evidence. Together they form one message (the Zelazny rule — see `references/zelazny-chart-selection.md`).

## Action titles in an academic context

Academic papers use neutral topic headings ("Methods", "Results", "Discussion") because the conventions of journals and examiners require it. Do **not** apply action titles to a thesis chapter heading. Apply them inside the discussion section to **section sub-headings** if the journal allows.

## Anti-patterns

- **Cliffhanger titles.** "What we found in Kenya..." — no. State what you found.
- **Question titles.** "Should Acme cut prices?" — no. State your answer; you have one. (Question titles are acceptable in marketing copy but not in executive analysis.)
- **Two-sentence titles.** If the title needs a second sentence, the section is doing two things; split it.
- **Wishy-washy verbs.** "Several factors may suggest..." — commit. The reader gives credit for clarity, not hedging.
- **Title that disagrees with the section.** Title says "Acme should hold"; section concludes "Acme should cut". Edit one.
