# Business Report Typology (Clippinger)

Encodes Dorinda Clippinger's *Business Report Guides: Research Reports and Business Plans* (Business Expert Press, 2019) as the engine's per-type template library. Pair with `reports-proposals-craft` (Forsyth) for general report craft and with `writing-that-works` for prose discipline.

## 1. Two flagship formal types

Clippinger's book centres on two complex artifacts that demand the highest report discipline. Get these right and shorter forms inherit the structure.

| Type | Purpose | Reader's decision | Length norm |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Formal Research Report** | Investigate a defined business question; present data, findings, conclusions, recommendations | Accept findings; act on recommendations | 30–80 pages including front and back matter |
| **Business Plan** | Persuade investors, lenders, or boards to fund a venture or significant change | Fund / approve / decline | 25–50 pages including financials and appendices |

## 2. Wider report typology (when to choose what)

| Report type | Purpose | Distinguishing feature |
|---|---|---|
| Informational | Convey facts without analysis or recommendation | No conclusions section |
| Analytical | Interpret data and draw conclusions | Conclusions but no required action |
| Recommendation | Recommend an action with rationale | Numbered, owned, dated recommendations |
| Progress / status | Report on work against plan | RAG status; variances; next-period plan |
| Feasibility | Assess viability of a proposed initiative | Criteria-based go / no-go |
| Justification | Justify a past or proposed decision | Pros/cons against alternatives considered |
| Audit / compliance | Verify conformance to standards | Findings ranked by severity; remediation plan |
| Research (formal) | Answer a defined business question | Methodology section; primary or secondary data |
| Business plan | Win funding or approval for a venture | Financial projections; market analysis |

**Pick the type before drafting.** Mixing types (e.g., audit body with recommendation framing) confuses readers and weakens both functions.

## 3. Formal Research Report — section template

### Preliminary parts (front matter)

1. **Cover** — title, author, recipient, date, classification.
2. **Title page** — full title, prepared for, prepared by, date.
3. **Letter / memo of transmittal** — purpose, scope, acknowledgements, principal finding, offer to discuss.
4. **Letter / memo of authorization** (if external) — original commissioning document.
5. **Letter / memo of acceptance** (if external) — acknowledgement and engagement terms.
6. **Table of contents** — decimal numbering, page numbers.
7. **List of figures and tables** — separate or combined; numbered consecutively.
8. **Executive summary** — stand-alone; situation, method, findings, recommendations; ≤1 page for ≤30 page reports, ≤2 pages otherwise.

### Body

9. **Introduction** — background, statement of the problem, scope, limitations, definitions, brief method, plan of presentation.
10. **Methodology / procedures** — replicable detail: research design, data sources (primary vs secondary), instruments, sampling, analysis methods, ethical considerations.
11. **Findings** — themed subsections; each subsection presents one finding with supporting evidence; visuals integrated with prose anchors.
12. **Analysis / discussion** — what the findings mean against the question; alternative interpretations; limitations honestly named.
13. **Conclusions** — answers to the research question(s); no new evidence introduced.
14. **Recommendations** — numbered, action-form (do X, by Y, owner Z, cost £A); ranked.

### Supplementary parts (back matter)

15. **References / bibliography** — full citations in a single style (APA, Harvard, Chicago).
16. **Appendices** — instruments (questionnaires, interview guides), raw data tables, calculations, glossary, supplementary visuals.
17. **Index** — for reports >50 pages.

## 4. Business Plan — section template

1. **Cover and title page** — venture name, "Business Plan," date, version, confidentiality marking.
2. **Executive summary** — venture in one page: opportunity, solution, market, traction, team, ask, use of funds.
3. **Company description** — mission, legal structure, ownership, history.
4. **Market analysis** — industry, target segments, size, growth, trends, competitive landscape.
5. **Organization and management** — org chart, key bios, board / advisors.
6. **Service or product line** — what is sold, lifecycle stage, IP position.
7. **Marketing and sales strategy** — positioning, pricing, channels, sales process, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value.
8. **Funding request** — amount, structure, use of funds, future funding rounds.
9. **Financial projections** — historical (if any), 3–5 year P&L, cash flow, balance sheet; assumptions table; sensitivity analysis.
10. **Appendices** — resumes, full financials, letters of intent, market research, IP filings.

## 5. Data presentation

- **One message per visual.** Title carries the takeaway sentence.
- **Numbering** — figures and tables numbered consecutively (Figure 1, Table 1, …) with chapter prefix optional (Figure 2.3).
- **Captions** — figure captions below the figure; table titles above the table.
- **Source line** — every visual cites the source, even your own data ("Source: author").
- **Type of visual to type of data:**
  - Line chart → trend over time.
  - Bar chart → comparison among categories.
  - Pie chart → composition (≤6 slices; never 3-D).
  - Table → exact values; multi-attribute comparisons; reference look-ups.
  - Pictograph → public-facing impact; never for precise values.
- Every visual referenced in prose **before** it appears; orphan visuals are forbidden.

## 6. Findings vs Conclusions vs Recommendations — three-way discipline

Clippinger emphasises that these three are different artifacts and must not be mixed:

| Artifact | What it answers | Voice |
|---|---|---|
| **Finding** | What the data shows | Descriptive, neutral |
| **Conclusion** | What the finding means against the research question | Interpretive, no new evidence |
| **Recommendation** | What to do about it | Imperative, owned, dated, costed |

Mixing them — e.g., a "finding" that recommends action, or a "conclusion" that introduces new data — is one of the most common business-report failures.

## 7. Methodology section — replicability standard

A methodology section is adequate only if a competent reader could **reproduce the study** from the description. Required elements:

- Research design (descriptive, exploratory, causal, longitudinal, etc.)
- Population and sampling frame; sample size and selection
- Data sources distinguished as primary vs secondary
- Instruments (with copies in appendix)
- Data-collection procedures and timing
- Analysis methods (statistical tests used, software, thresholds)
- Ethical considerations (consent, confidentiality, conflicts)
- Limitations honestly named (what the study cannot conclude)

## 8. Appendices — what belongs where

Body content: anything required to follow the argument once.
Appendix content: anything referenced more than once, or detailed support that would slow the body.

| Belongs in body | Belongs in appendix |
|---|---|
| Headline finding | Underlying raw data table |
| Summary chart | Complete dataset |
| Selected quotation | Full interview transcript |
| Recommendation | Detailed costing model |
| Glossary on first use | Full glossary |

## 9. Front matter & back matter — when each is needed

| Element | Required when |
|---|---|
| Cover | All formal reports |
| Title page | All formal reports |
| Transmittal letter | External report; commissioned work |
| Authorization letter | Commissioned by an external party |
| Acceptance letter | Commissioned by an external party (response) |
| TOC | Document >5 pages |
| List of figures/tables | Document with ≥3 figures or ≥3 tables |
| Executive summary | Document >10 pages or any decision-grade report |
| Glossary | Specialised technical vocabulary |
| References | Any cited external work |
| Appendices | Supporting material referenced from body |
| Index | Reports >50 pages |

## 10. Formal vs informal — threshold rule

A report is **formal** when any of these is true: external recipient, decision involves contractual or financial commitment, length >10 pages, multiple readers with conflicting interests, audit or regulatory context. Otherwise informal (memo or letter format).

## 11. Tone, voice, objectivity

- **Third person** for findings and analysis ("the data show," "the study finds").
- **First-plural** acceptable for conclusions and recommendations in commissioned work ("we recommend").
- **Active voice** with a deliberate impersonal subject; avoid agentless passive that hides authorship.
- **No emotive language** in findings; emotive cost language acceptable in recommendations to convey stakes.
- **Hedging** matches the evidence — strong evidence supports declarative conclusions; weaker evidence requires "the data suggest…"
- **Honesty about limitations** — declared in methodology and conclusions; not buried.

## 12. Length norms per type

| Type | Pages |
|---|---|
| Informational report | 2–10 |
| Progress report | 1–5 (recurring) |
| Analytical report | 5–25 |
| Feasibility report | 10–30 |
| Recommendation report | 5–20 |
| Audit / compliance report | 15–50 |
| Formal research report | 30–80 |
| Business plan | 25–50 |

## 13. Anti-patterns specific to formal business reports

- **Narrative drift** — chronological lab-notes story instead of thematic findings.
- **Methodology hand-waving** — "we surveyed the market" without instrument, sample, dates.
- **Findings padded with interpretation** — drift into conclusions territory.
- **Recommendation softening** — "the company might consider…" with no owner or date.
- **Pie-chart abuse** — 3-D, exploded, more than 6 slices, percentages not summing.
- **Orphan visuals** — figure dropped in, never referenced in text.
- **Front matter inflation** — three-page transmittal, three-page TOC, two-page list of tables on a 12-page report.
- **Appendix-as-graveyard** — material left in appendix that the body needs.
- **Inconsistent citation style** — APA in chapter 1, footnotes in chapter 2.
- **Limitations buried** — methodology silent on what the study cannot say.
- **Business plan with only optimistic financials** — no sensitivity, no downside.

## 14. Ship-readiness checklist

- [ ] Type identified and structure matches.
- [ ] Front matter scaled to length and formality.
- [ ] Executive summary stand-alone.
- [ ] Methodology replicable.
- [ ] Findings, conclusions, recommendations strictly separated.
- [ ] Recommendations numbered, owned, dated, costed, ranked.
- [ ] Every visual has takeaway-title, caption, source, prose anchor.
- [ ] Citation style consistent throughout; references complete.
- [ ] Limitations declared honestly.
- [ ] Length within type norm.
- [ ] Tone third-person; passive only by design.
- [ ] Appendices contain only what body cannot.
- [ ] List of figures/tables matches body.
- [ ] Page numbers, cross-references, TOC regenerated after final edit.
- [ ] Confidentiality / classification marking present.
- [ ] Two human eyes on the final mechanical pass.
