# Reference — Case Study Method

**Canonical source:** Hancock, Dawson R., and Algozzine, Bob. *Doing Case Study Research: A Practical Guide for Beginning Researchers*. Teachers College Press, Columbia University, 2006 (1st ed.; later editions 2011, 2017). Tier 1. Local extract: `extracted-books/case-study-research.txt`.

**Companion sources** referenced by Hancock & Algozzine (Tier 1, separate works):
- Yin, Robert K. *Case Study Research: Design and Methods*. Sage, 5th ed. 2014. (Provides the construct/internal/external/reliability validity framework, pattern-matching, explanation-building, time-series analytic moves not deeply elaborated by Hancock & Algozzine.)
- Stake, Robert E. *The Art of Case Study Research*. Sage, 1995. (Provides the intrinsic / instrumental / collective typology and member-checking discipline central to Hancock & Algozzine's treatment.)
- Merriam, Sharan B. *Qualitative Research and Case Study Applications in Education*. Jossey-Bass, 2001.

This reference encodes case-study **method** — definitions, types, validity, reporting. The companion `references/case-selection-and-analysis.md` covers the operating discipline (sampling, interview-protocol design, content analysis, member checking).

## Definition

Hancock & Algozzine (Ch 3) adopt Yin's (2003) definition almost verbatim:

> *An empirical investigation of a contemporary phenomenon within its natural context using multiple sources of evidence.*

Three distinguishing properties:

1. **Bounded phenomenon.** A case is bounded by space and time — an event, situation, programme, activity, or organisational unit, not a culture (ethnography), a lived experience (phenomenology), a process (grounded theory), or a life (biography).
2. **Naturalistic context.** The phenomenon is studied where it occurs, not in a controlled setting.
3. **Multiple sources of evidence.** Triangulation across interviews, observations, and documents is the baseline, not a sophistication.

The book's framing (Ch 3) is **more exploratory than confirmatory**: case study seeks themes / categories / patterns rather than testing pre-stated hypotheses. (For confirmatory case study, escalate to Yin directly.)

## Two parallel typologies

### Typology 1 — by disciplinary orientation (Merriam, 2001; Hancock & Algozzine Ch 5)

| Type | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Ethnographic** | Observable patterns of a culture-sharing group; immersion required |
| **Historical** | Evolution of an event / programme / organisation over time; descriptive interpretation of cause and effect |
| **Psychological** | Individual behaviour examined through psychological theory |
| **Sociological** | Social institutions, structures, relationships (families, religion, gender, race, aging) |

### Typology 2 — by function and scope (Stake, 1995 + Yin, 2003)

| Type | Use |
|---|---|
| **Intrinsic** (Stake) | Interest in this particular case; no theorising intended |
| **Instrumental** (Stake) | Case used to illuminate a theoretical question |
| **Collective / multiple-case** (Stake) | Several instrumental cases pooled to theorise about a larger class |
| **Exploratory** (Yin) | Defines questions for follow-on research |
| **Explanatory** (Yin) | Establishes cause-and-effect |
| **Descriptive** (Yin) | Complete description of a phenomenon in context |

The two typologies are orthogonal — every case is positioned in both. A **historical, instrumental** case study of one school's literacy programme (Stake-flavoured) sits alongside a **sociological, explanatory, multiple-case** study of three urban schools (Yin-flavoured).

## Data sources and standards

Three primary sources, each with a dedicated chapter in the book:

### Interviews (Ch 6)

- **Semi-structured preferred** for case study (gives the participant room to surface categories the researcher did not anticipate).
- **Develop an interview protocol** — see `references/case-selection-and-analysis.md` for the engine's protocol template.
- **Audiotape with consent.**
- **Transcribe in full.**
- **Open-ended questions only.** No yes/no, no leading, no multi-part.
- Hancock & Algozzine's discipline (Ch 6): "*Time spent talking to the interviewee would be better spent listening.*"

### Observations (Ch 7)

- **Specific and concrete notes**, not vague or overgeneralised. (See Table 7.1 of the original for examples.)
- Note: date, location, persons present, circumstances.
- Distinguish description from inference inside the field-notes file.

### Documents (Ch 8)

- Records, archives, artefacts, audiovisual materials.
- Apply the engine's source-evaluation discipline (`source-evaluation/SKILL.md`) — every document tier-rated; primary documents passed through Burke pentad.

The recurring standard (Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 11):

> *Findings based on evidence attained from interviews, observations, and documents are more convincing than those based on evidence from only one or two of these information sources.*

Triangulation across all three is the baseline.

## Validity threats and remedies

Hancock & Algozzine frame credibility through **Stake's lens** (member checking, vicarious experience, naturalistic generalisation) more than Yin's four-test framework. The book's five confirmation strategies (Ch 11):

1. **Member checking** — share results with participants for plausibility check. Stake's example: a draft report a teacher found "objectionable and embarrassing" prompted Stake to revise — surfacing the disagreement, not silencing it.
2. **Peer review by case-study colleagues** — independent reading by a methodologically informed colleague.
3. **Expert review** by topical experts outside the case.
4. **Bias disclosure** — articulate personal biases and how they were mitigated.
5. **Triangulation** — convergence of interview, observation, document evidence.

**Generalisation discipline.** Case findings support **analytic, not statistical, generalisation**. The report should let others "generalize from it, replicate it, or both" — rather than asserting the findings hold across populations.

**Where Hancock & Algozzine are thin.** Yin's full four-test framework (construct validity, internal validity, external validity, reliability) and Yin's pattern-matching / explanation-building / time-series analytic moves are **not named** in this book. If those are needed, route to Yin directly — flag in the methodology chapter.

## Reporting structure

No universal format, but the recurring components Hancock & Algozzine identify:

1. Define the bounded case (event / situation / programme / activity, time, space).
2. Researcher's relationship to the case + bias disclosure.
3. Literature review framing the questions.
4. Disciplinary orientation + design (intrinsic / instrumental / collective; exploratory / explanatory / descriptive).
5. Detailed description of all data sources.
6. **Rich description** with key participants' verbatim statements.
7. Strategies used to interpret and confirm findings.
8. Discussion that does **not** generalise beyond outcomes.

### Stake-derived narrative checklist (Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 10)

- [ ] **Sense of story** — the case has a beginning, a development, and a resolution (even if the resolution is "the question remains open").
- [ ] **Vicarious experience for the reader** — quotations and field-notes give the reader a sense of being there.
- [ ] **Quotations used effectively but not over-extensively** — every quotation earns its place.
- [ ] **Sound assertions** — neither over- nor under-generalising.
- [ ] **Conceptual structure visible in headings** — section names point to the analytic frame, not just to topics.

## Anti-patterns

- **Vague observation notes** (Ch 7, Table 7.1) — overgeneralised; hard to use later.
- **Yes/no, leading, or multi-part interview questions** (Ch 6, Table 6.3).
- **Interviewee selection on availability** rather than information richness.
- **Failure to label / store data** with date, location, persons, circumstances — jeopardises later interpretation.
- **Spending time on irrelevant data** (e.g., software-vendor interviews when studying internal adoption).
- **Skipping member checks** because findings might embarrass participants.
- **Over-generalising** findings beyond the bounded case.
- **Treating case study as a catchall** for "research that is not a survey, an observational study, or an experiment" (Merriam, 2001, quoted by Hancock & Algozzine as a misuse).
- **Reporting themes that overlap or contradict** without further synthesis.
- **Talking more than listening** during interviews.

## Quotable passages

1. *"Case study research ... means conducting an empirical investigation of a contemporary phenomenon within its natural context using multiple sources of evidence."* — Ch 3
2. *"Sometimes people use the term case study as a catchall category for research that is not a survey, an observational study, or an experiment and is not statistical in nature."* — Ch 3, citing Merriam (2001)
3. *"Case study research is generally more exploratory than confirmatory; that is, the case study researcher normally seeks to identify themes or categories of behavior and events rather than prove relationships or test hypotheses."* — Ch 3
4. *"Case study research is richly descriptive, because it is grounded in deep and varied sources of information."* — Ch 3
5. *"Researchers engage in intrinsic case study research when they want to know more about a particular individual, group, event, or organization."* — Ch 5
6. *"The most important consideration is to identify those persons in the research setting who may have the best information with which to address the study's research questions."* — Ch 6
7. *"Time spent talking to the interviewee would be better spent listening to the interviewee."* — Ch 6
8. *"Findings based on evidence attained from interviews, observations, and documents are more convincing than those based on evidence from only one or two of these information sources."* — Ch 11
9. *"No apparent pattern is a pattern."* — Ch 9 (from Berg's content-analysis stage model)
10. *"Do not overemphasize limitations and do not generalize beyond the outcomes of the study."* — Ch 13
