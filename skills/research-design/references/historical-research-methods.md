# Historical research methods

Historical research lives or dies on **primary sources** and **source appraisal**. Secondary literature is necessary but never sufficient.

## Primary vs secondary

- **Primary** — created during the period, by participants or witnesses. Documents, letters, photographs, oral histories, official records, contemporary news, court filings.
- **Secondary** — created later, by historians or analysts using primary sources.
- **Tertiary** — encyclopedia, textbook summaries.

## Five-step method

1. **Define period & scope.** "Kenya independence 1963" is a period. "Kenya colonial history" is a period. "Kenya in 1960" is too narrow without a topic.
2. **Build primary-source inventory.** Archives, government records, contemporary press, oral history, photography.
3. **Build secondary-source map.** What major works exist? What are the historiographical debates?
4. **Construct chronology.** Reliable timelines first, narrative interpretation after.
5. **Appraise sources.** Provenance, authorship bias, audience, completeness.

## Source appraisal questions

For each primary source:
- **Who created it?** What is their position / interest?
- **When?** Contemporary to the event, or written later from memory?
- **For whom?** Audience changes how things are recorded.
- **Why?** Promotion, advocacy, record, defence?
- **What is missing?** What would we expect that's not there?

## Decision rules

- **Primary first, always.** A secondary source claim should always be checkable against primary.
- **Date-stamp the chronology.** Disputed dates get marked as such.
- **Surface historiographical debates.** Don't pick a side without explaining the alternatives.
- **Distinguish reconstruction from interpretation.** "What happened" vs "what it meant".
- **Acknowledge gaps.** Periods without records are part of the finding.
- **Use Chicago / footnoted citation** — historians expect it.

## Output structure

- `<project>/research/period-scope.md`
- `<project>/research/primary-sources.md` — annotated inventory
- `<project>/research/secondary-sources.md` — historiographical map
- `<project>/research/chronology.md` — timeline with disputed dates marked
- `<project>/research/source-appraisal.md` — per-source provenance + bias
- Final Word doc uses Schema I

## Anti-patterns

- Citing Wikipedia as primary
- Using secondary syntheses without checking the underlying primary
- Constructing narrative before the chronology is solid
- Ignoring inconvenient sources
- Treating one historian's interpretation as the consensus
