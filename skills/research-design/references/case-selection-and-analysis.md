# Reference — Case Selection and Analysis

**Canonical source:** Hancock, D. R., and Algozzine, B. *Doing Case Study Research: A Practical Guide for Beginning Researchers*. Teachers College Press, 2006. Tier 1. Local extract: `extracted-books/case-study-research.txt`.

This reference is the **operating** companion to `references/case-study-method.md`. Use this when actually executing a case study — sampling, gatekeeper engagement, interview-protocol design, content analysis, member checking.

## Case selection — the discipline

### Purposeful (purposive) sampling, never convenience

Every case is selected for a reason that links to the research question. The selection rationale is documented in the methodology chapter; an examiner who cannot reconstruct the reason from the text concludes the selection was opportunistic.

### Selection criteria (Hancock & Algozzine, Tables 3.1 / 3.2)

- Define an **atypical**, **maximum-variation**, or **extreme** case explicitly. ("This site was chosen because …")
- Identify a **gatekeeper** to provide access and rapport before sampling. The gatekeeper grants entry; without them, the case may not be accessible at all.
- For **collective designs**, choose cases that allow theoretical saturation across the class.
- For **interview participants**, select on **information richness**, not availability. Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 6: "*The most important consideration is to identify those persons in the research setting who may have the best information with which to address the study's research questions.*"
- Define site, number of cases, and rationale up front in the proposal's methodology chapter.

### Sample-type quick reference

| Yin / Stake type | Selection logic | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Typical case** | Average / representative of the class | Descriptive case study; first study in an under-explored domain |
| **Extreme / deviant case** | Far from the norm | Theory-building; "what makes this one different?" |
| **Critical case** | Hardest test; if X holds here, X likely holds anywhere | Theory-testing |
| **Maximum-variation** | Span the diversity in the class | Collective design seeking the boundary conditions of a pattern |
| **Longitudinal** | Same case observed at multiple time points | Process / change |
| **Intrinsic (Stake)** | This particular case is itself the question | Single-case, no theorising claimed |
| **Instrumental (Stake)** | Case illuminates a theoretical question | Single case as a vehicle |
| **Collective (Stake)** | N instrumental cases for synthesis | Cross-case theorising |

## Gatekeeper engagement

The gatekeeper controls access. Hancock & Algozzine treat gatekeeper relations as part of the design, not as logistics:

1. **Identify the gatekeeper** — the person whose permission opens the site (head of school, organisation lead, community elder).
2. **Brief them honestly** — research question, methods, time commitment, deliverables, risks, what they will see before publication.
3. **Negotiate the access conditions** — what may be observed, what is off-limits, what is anonymised.
4. **Document the agreement** — even an informal note signed by both parties.
5. **Maintain the relationship** — periodic updates; surface friction early.
6. **Honour exit conditions** — if the gatekeeper withdraws consent mid-study, the agreement governs (within the limits of IRB approval and the candidate's institutional obligations).

## Interview-protocol design

Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 6 + Tables 6.1 / 6.2 / 6.3.

### Question types to use

- **Open-ended** — "Tell me about …", "Walk me through …", "How did you experience …"
- **Probe questions** — "Can you give me an example?", "What did that look like?"
- **Clarification questions** — "When you said X, did you mean …?"

### Question types to avoid (Table 6.3)

- **Yes/no questions** — they yield monosyllables and shut conversation.
- **Leading questions** — "You found it difficult, didn't you?" plants the answer.
- **Multi-part questions** — the interviewee answers the part they remember.
- **Jargon** the interviewee may not share.
- **Hypothetical / speculative** questions early in the interview — wait until rapport is established.

### Protocol structure

1. **Opening** — re-state purpose, confidentiality, recording consent, right to skip questions or withdraw.
2. **Warm-up** — easy descriptive questions to settle the interviewee.
3. **Core** — the substantive questions, ordered from concrete (what happened) to interpretive (why it happened, what it meant).
4. **Closing** — "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?" — Hancock & Algozzine's standard prompt for surfacing what the protocol missed.
5. **Logistics** — thank, confirm next steps, end recording.

### Listening discipline

Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 6: "*Time spent talking to the interviewee would be better spent listening.*" Practical:

- After asking a question, count to five before adding a follow-up.
- Pursue the interviewee's pause; silence often surfaces the most important content.
- Take minimal notes during the interview; rely on the recording.
- Note non-verbal cues (laughter, hesitation, body language) in field-notes immediately after.

## Content / thematic analysis — Berg's stage model

Hancock & Algozzine, Ch 9, building on Berg (2004):

1. **Identify research question** — re-anchor the analysis on what is being asked. The same data answer different questions differently.
2. **Determine analytic categories** — *a priori* categories from theory (deductive) and / or grounded categories from data (inductive). Most case studies blend both.
3. **Establish grounded categories** — code raw data; surface what the *a priori* categories miss.
4. **Sort** — assign every coded segment to its category.
5. **Count** — frequency per category. ("*No apparent pattern is a pattern.*" — Ch 9.)
6. **Interpret against the literature** — what do the patterns mean in light of the theoretical frame from chapter 2?

### Counting units Berg's model uses

- words · themes · characters · items · concepts · semantics

The unit chosen depends on what is being claimed. Word-count for vocabulary patterns; theme-count for conceptual patterns; semantic units for meaning-frame patterns.

### Recursive analysis

Analysis runs **in parallel** with collection, not after. Each round of data sharpens the analytic categories; each refinement of categories sharpens the next round of collection. Hancock & Algozzine's discipline: do not save analysis for the end. The case-study design is recursive by construction.

### Thematic-saturation criteria

A theme is saturated when:

- New data confirm the theme rather than extending it.
- The theme is **distinct** from other themes (no large overlap).
- The themes are **comparably complex** (not one cavernous theme and several small ones).
- The themes are **as specific as the data permit** — generic themes ("communication", "trust") are usually under-coded.

## Cross-case analysis (collective designs)

For multiple-case designs:

1. Complete within-case analysis for each case independently.
2. Build a **case-by-theme matrix** — rows = themes; columns = cases; cells = degree-of-presence summary.
3. Look for **convergence** (theme present across cases — strong cross-case finding).
4. Look for **divergence** (theme present in some, absent in others — surface the variable that explains).
5. Look for **negative cases** — cases where the dominant pattern does not hold. These are the most theoretically informative.
6. Synthesise: what does the cross-case pattern say about the larger class?

## Member checking

Hancock & Algozzine treat member checking as the highest-leverage validity strategy. Workflow:

1. **Draft the case-study report** to a state where the participants can read it.
2. **Share** with the named participants (or, where consent allowed, a representative sample).
3. **Invite reaction** — what is accurate? What is not? What was misinterpreted?
4. **Document the responses** — including disagreements.
5. **Revise** — Stake's example: a teacher found a draft "objectionable and embarrassing"; Stake revised to surface the disagreement rather than silence the teacher.
6. **Note any unresolved disagreement** explicitly in the final report — credibility comes from surfacing, not from concealing.

Member checks are **not optional** for a Hancock & Algozzine-style case study; they are part of the design.

## Anti-patterns

- **Convenience sampling** dressed as purposeful sampling.
- **Gatekeeper neglect** — assuming access is yours by right.
- **Yes/no, leading, multi-part questions** in the interview protocol.
- **Talking more than listening** — Hancock & Algozzine's repeated warning.
- **Failure to label and store data** — the field-notes file without date, location, persons, circumstances is unusable in six months.
- **Saving analysis for the end** — defeats the recursive nature of the design.
- **Skipping member checks** because the candidate fears disagreement.
- **Themes that overlap or that conflict** without resolution — re-code; re-cluster; re-interpret.
- **Over-generalisation** beyond the bounded case.
