# Reports & Proposals Craft (Forsyth)

Encodes Patrick Forsyth's *How to Write Reports and Proposals* (Kogan Page, Creating Success series) as operational rules. Use whenever the engine produces a business report or proposal. Pair with `writing-that-works` (Roman/Raphaelson) for sentence-level discipline and `business-report-typology` for type-specific structures.

## 1. Reports vs proposals

| Dimension | Report | Proposal |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Inform / explain / recommend on findings | Persuade a buyer or sponsor to authorise action |
| Reader's decision | Accept findings? Act on recommendations? | Award the work? Sign? |
| Stance | Analytical, evidence-led, neutral surface | Persuasive, benefit-led, competitive |
| Time | Backward + present | Forward |
| Spine | Background → Method → Findings → Conclusions → Recommendations | Situation → Need → Approach → Deliverables → Price → Why us |

Hybrid form (investigative proposal): report structure for the front half, proposal structure for the back half — never blur the join.

## 2. Reader-first planning

### Audience grid (fill before drafting)

| Role | Proxy | Cares about | Decision power | What they must see | Likely objection |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Decider | (e.g., MD) | ROI, risk, reputation | Approves / kills | Bottom line + risk envelope on p.1 | "Too expensive / risky" |
| Influencer | (e.g., Ops) | Operational fit | Shapes view | Practicality of approach | "Won't work here" |
| Blocker | (e.g., Finance) | Cost, controls | Veto | Pricing logic, payment terms | "Numbers don't add up" |
| User | (e.g., team lead) | Day-to-day impact | Adoption | Transition plan | "Disrupts my team" |
| Gatekeeper | (e.g., procurement) | Compliance, format | Can stop document reaching decider | Required headings, format | "Wrong template" |

### Purpose statement template

> *After reading this [report/proposal], [primary reader] will [decide / approve / fund / change / understand] **X**, because the document shows **Y**, and the next action they will take is **Z** by **[date]**.*

If X/Y/Z is vague, planning is incomplete — do not draft.

## 3. Report anatomy (10–30 page report)

| Section | Must contain | Length | What to cut |
|---|---|---|---|
| Title page | Informative title, author, recipient, date, version, classification | 1 page | Logos that dominate |
| Distribution / version control | Recipients, revision history | 0.25 page | If single reader, omit |
| Contents | Decimal numbering, page refs | 1 page | Beyond 3 levels |
| Executive summary | Stand-alone digest of situation, work done, findings, recommendations, ask | 0.5–1 page | Background, method detail |
| Introduction | Why the report exists, scope, exclusions, terms of reference, brief method | 0.5–1.5 pages | Restating title; thanks |
| Body | Findings grouped thematically (not chronologically); claim → evidence → implication per section | 60–70 % of total | Raw transcripts, undigested data |
| Conclusions | What the findings *mean*, in priority order; no new evidence | 1–2 pages | New facts; hedging without consequence |
| Recommendations | Numbered, action-form: do X by Y, owner Z, cost £A; ranked | 1–2 pages | "Consider", "explore", "look into" |
| Appendices | Data tables, methodology, calculations, glossary, references | as needed | Anything cited only once and short — push into body |

**Cut rule:** if removing a paragraph would not change the recommendation, it belongs in an appendix or out.

## 4. Proposal anatomy

1. **Cover letter** — one page; names the buyer, names the problem in their words, signposts headline benefit, asks for the next step.
2. **Executive summary** — sells, doesn't summarise. Their problem, your approach, the outcome, price band, why you. Stand-alone.
3. **Situation / understanding of need** — proves you listened. Mirrors their language. Quantifies cost of inaction.
4. **Objectives** — measurable, time-bound, framed as *their* outcomes (not your activities).
5. **Proposed approach / methodology** — phased; logic for each phase; show why this beats alternatives.
6. **Deliverables** — concrete artefacts and acceptance criteria; explicit in-scope vs out-of-scope.
7. **Timeline / milestones** — table or Gantt; mark client dependencies.
8. **Team** — named people, role, % allocation, one-line credential.
9. **Pricing & commercial terms** — fixed / T&M / capped; payment schedule tied to milestones; expenses; validity period.
10. **Assumptions, risks, dependencies** — own risks before they do; pair each risk with a mitigation.
11. **Why us / track record** — 2–3 directly comparable references with measurable outcomes.
12. **Acceptance** — signature block, contract terms reference, named contact.
13. **Appendices** — CVs, case studies, certifications, draft contract.

The buyer's silent question at every section: *"So what does this mean for me?"*

## 5. Executive summary discipline

- **Stand-alone rule** — assume the only thing the decider reads is the executive summary.
- **Length** — 1 page max, ideally 250–400 words. Long-document exception: 2 pages for >50-page reports, never more.
- **Structure (4 moves)** — context (1–2 sentences) · core finding/proposition · 3–5 evidence headlines · ask/recommendation (1–2 sentences).
- **"If they only read this" test** — delete the rest mentally; can the reader still decide correctly?
- **No new material** — anything in the summary must appear, expanded, in the body.
- **No hedging verbs** — decision-grade prose only.
- Write last; **placeholder first** to constrain scope.

## 6. Headings, structure, signposting

- **Descriptive over label headings** — "Costs rose 18 % in Q3" beats "Costs."
- **Decimal numbering** for >8 pages; stop at 3 levels.
- **Parallel structure** — same grammatical form per heading level.
- **One-line section preview** after each major heading on long documents.
- **Pull quotes** ≤1 per page, used to surface the line you'd want quoted back.
- **White space is structure** — aim for ≤60 % ink density per page.
- **Front-load every section** — first sentence of every section = its conclusion.

## 7. Persuasion structure — SCQA

The Forsyth/Kogan Page persuasion spine (Minto-style):

- **S — Situation** — stable, agreed background.
- **C — Complication** — change, threat, or opportunity that destabilises it.
- **Q — Question** — implicit reader question ("So what do we do?").
- **A — Answer** — recommendation, with the supporting pyramid below it.

Building the case: state answer first → 3–5 MECE reasons → evidence per reason → action.

**Rebutting objections** — surface, validate, dismantle. Silence reads as concession.

## 8. Tone & language

- **Plain English** — *use* not *utilise*; *help* not *facilitate*; *now* not *at this point in time*.
- **Sentence length** — average 15–20 words; nothing over 30 without reason.
- **Paragraphs** — 3–5 sentences; one idea each; first sentence = topic sentence.
- **Active voice default** — passive only to (a) hide an irrelevant agent, (b) keep topic in subject slot, (c) soften bad news.
- **Jargon discipline** — define on first use; ban acronyms recurring fewer than three times; glossary for technical reports.
- **Concrete > abstract** — "Saved £42k in Q2" beats "achieved significant savings."
- **No throat-clearing** — strike "It is important to note that," "In order to," "At the end of the day."
- **Person** — reports: third person or first-plural ("we recommend"); proposals: second person ("you will receive") + first-plural ("we will deliver").
- **Numbers** — spell out one to nine; numerals for 10+; numerals for money, percentages, dates, measures.

## 9. Visuals & evidence

- **Chart beats prose** when trend, comparison, distribution, proportion is the point and the reader needs to feel magnitude.
- **Table beats chart** when exact values matter or reader will look things up.
- **Prose beats both** when ≤3 numbers fit a sentence.
- **One message per visual** — chart title = takeaway sentence, not the variable name.
- **Captions** carry the interpretation; **callouts** mark the single point that drives the argument.
- **Pattern** — claim sentence → "(Figure 3)" → chart → one-line so-what.
- **Source line on every visual.**
- **Accessibility** — colour + shape so the chart survives B&W and colour-blind readers.

## 10. Drafting cycle (4 passes)

1. **Skeleton pass** — headings + one-line per section. Get sign-off on structure before prose.
2. **Argument pass** — first sentence of every paragraph; document still works as a skim.
3. **Evidence pass** — fill in support, visuals, citations.
4. **Polish pass** — language, rhythm, formatting, proofread.

**Cold-reader test** — non-context reader restates recommendation and three main reasons after one read. If they cannot, structure has failed.

**Proofreading levels (separately, never combined):** structural · logical · linguistic · mechanical. Read the mechanical pass on paper, out loud, or backwards paragraph-by-paragraph — screen-reading misses ~30 % of typos.

## 11. Anti-patterns

- **Data-dump report** — every fact gathered, no synthesis.
- **Buried lead** — recommendation appears on p.14.
- **Kitchen-sink proposal** — every capability listed, none mapped to the buyer's specific need.
- **Weak recommendations** — "consider," "explore" with no owner, date, or cost.
- **Methodology theatre** — pages on process to pad thin findings.
- **Mismatched audience** — peer voice sent to executives.
- **Unanchored visuals** — charts dropped in without claim or interpretation.
- **Credentials avalanche** — proposal opens with 8 pages of "about us" before mentioning the client.
- **Hedge soup** — every sentence qualified.
- **Inconsistent numbering** — body £2.4m, summary £2.6m.
- **Format tax** — wrong template; fails procurement before reaching decider.
- **No ask** — document ends without telling the reader what to do next.
- **Risk-blind proposal** — no risks named.
- **Plagiarised boilerplate** — prior client's name still in the file.
- **Late-stage scope creep** — author keeps adding sections; document loses spine.
- **Passive-voice fog** — "it was decided that…" — accountability vanishes.

## 12. Reusable templates (one-line per heading)

- **Short report (≤5 pages, internal):** Purpose · Background · Findings · Conclusions · Recommendations · Next steps.
- **Long report (15–40 pages):** Title · Contents · Executive summary · Introduction & ToR · Methodology · Findings (themed) · Analysis · Conclusions · Recommendations · Implementation outline · References · Appendices.
- **Formal external proposal:** Cover letter · Exec summary · Situation · Objectives · Approach · Deliverables · Timeline · Team · Investment · Risks · Why us · Acceptance · Appendices.
- **Internal proposal / business case:** Recommendation · Strategic context · Problem & cost of inaction · Options considered · Preferred option · Costs & benefits (NPV/payback) · Risks · Implementation · Decision requested.
- **Executive briefing (1–2 pages):** Headline (decision) · Why now · Three supporting facts · Risk envelope · Ask & deadline.
- **Bid response:** mirror RFP order exactly · compliance matrix as section 1 · exec summary as section 2 · then RFP-mandated sections · appendices last.
- **Status / progress report:** RAG status · Achievements · Plan · Risks/issues with owners · Decisions needed.

## 13. Ship gate

- [ ] Purpose statement (X/Y/Z) written, met, matched by document.
- [ ] Executive summary stands alone — passes "if they only read this" test.
- [ ] Lead on page 1 — recommendation/proposition not buried.
- [ ] Every recommendation specific, owned, dated, costed.
- [ ] Every claim sourced; numbers reconcile across summary, body, appendices.
- [ ] Every visual has takeaway-sentence title, source line, prose anchor.
- [ ] Headings descriptive, parallel, ≤3 levels.
- [ ] Known objections surfaced and rebutted (proposal) or limitations stated (report).
- [ ] Audience grid revisited — decider, influencer, blocker, gatekeeper each served.
- [ ] Tone consistent: person, voice, tense, terminology, number formatting.
- [ ] Risks/assumptions section present and honest (proposal).
- [ ] Pricing validity period and acceptance mechanism present (proposal).
- [ ] Cold-reader test passed.
- [ ] Four proofreading passes completed separately.
- [ ] Format/template compliance verified.
- [ ] No client/competitor leftovers from prior boilerplate.
- [ ] Version number, classification, distribution list correct.
- [ ] Required signatures secured before despatch.
- [ ] Next-action and deadline stated explicitly on the last page.
