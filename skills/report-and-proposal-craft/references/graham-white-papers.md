# White Paper Craft (Graham)

Encodes Gordon Graham's *White Papers For Dummies* (Wiley, 2013) as operational rules. Use whenever the engine produces a white paper. Pair with `reports-proposals-craft` for boundary cases, and with the engine's source-evaluation skills for evidence sourcing.

## 1. Mantra (Graham's foundation)

> *"A truly effective white paper helps business people understand an issue, solve a problem, or make a decision."*

If a draft does none of those three, it is not a white paper — it is a brochure or a blog post. Apply the mantra at every drafting decision.

## 2. The three flavors (Part II)

| Flavor | Position | Purpose | Length | Vendor named |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Backgrounder** ("vanilla") | Late funnel | Detailed merits of one offering, standard, or methodology | 6–8 pages | Yes, throughout |
| **Numbered list** ("strawberry") | Early funnel | Light, scannable list with a fresh angle, warning, or checklist | 5–7 pages + cover | Only at the close |
| **Problem/Solution** ("chocolate") | Middle funnel | Educate the market on a problem and a *category* of solution | 8–12 pages | Only in About + CTA |

**Hard rule:** never name your product before page 2 in a problem/solution. Doing so collapses it into a brochure.

**Picking the flavor (Chapter 5):**
- Reflect on purpose (awareness vs consideration vs decision).
- Consider target audience (executive vs technical vs influencer).
- Match the target sector's content norms.
- A single white paper carries a single flavor; mash-ups are advanced and fail without discipline (Chapter 9).

## 3. Two readers per paper

- **Skimmer**: title, subtitle, executive summary, callouts, headings, captions, conclusion. Must reconstruct the full argument from these alone.
- **Deep reader**: reads everything, checks footnotes, forwards to colleagues.

Subtitle is mandatory and must disclose audience + flavor (e.g., "A problem/solution white paper for IT leaders in mid-market manufacturing").

## 4. Anatomy by flavor

### Backgrounder
1. Cover (title, subtitle, date, vendor logo)
2. Executive summary
3. Introduction — offering and the business problem
4. Market context — standards, regulations, trends
5. How it works — architecture diagram + plain-language walkthrough
6. Key capabilities — feature → benefit table mapped to buyer pains
7. Specifications — performance, scale, environments, integrations
8. Standards & compliance
9. Deployment & operations
10. Use cases — 2–3 short, quantified
11. Summary
12. About the vendor (boilerplate)
13. Call to action — single, specific

### Numbered list
1. Cover with the number in the title ("7 Hidden Costs of …")
2. Short introduction (≤1 page)
3. Items 1…N — bold heading, 1–3 paragraphs each, optional mini-stat or example
4. Conclusion — pattern across the items
5. About the vendor (short)
6. Call to action — usually a follow-on problem/solution paper

Item count: **brainstorm 15–20, ship 7–8 strongest**.

### Problem/Solution
1. Cover (problem-led title, subtitle)
2. Executive summary
3. Introduction — industry context, trend, why now (no product)
4. The problem — drivers, who feels it, cost of inaction
5. Existing approaches and why they fall short — fair survey
6. A better approach — described as a *category*, not a product
7. Buyer's guide / selection criteria — checklist or comparison table
8. Proof — analyst data, customer outcomes, benchmarks, expert quotes
9. Implementation considerations
10. Conclusion
11. About the vendor (short, factual)
12. Call to action

## 5. The hook & lead

Allowed openings (Chapter 13):
1. Startling third-party statistic.
2. Anecdote or mini-case (3–5 sentences).
3. Provocative question the reader cannot dismiss with "no."
4. Quotable analyst or regulator pull-quote.
5. Historical pivot ("Until 2019 X was true. It isn't anymore.").
6. Cost of inaction in dollars.

**Forbidden openings**: "In today's fast-paced business environment…"; company history; product announcement; dictionary definition; generic trend statement.

The first 100 words must answer "why should I keep reading right now." Vendor/product cannot appear in those 100 words for problem/solution or numbered-list flavors.

## 6. Five-step pipeline (Part III)

1. **Plan** — confirm flavor, audience, decision stage, single CTA, the one takeaway sentence. Sponsor-approved 1-page brief before any drafting.
2. **Research** — source dossier with analyst data, primary survey, 3–5 expert interviews, 2–3 customer case data points; fact sheet with footnotes ready before drafting.
3. **Write** — body first, executive summary last; draft each section in one sitting to preserve voice; do not edit while drafting.
4. **Design** — layout in template, add visuals, single-final proof on paper.
5. **Promote** — landing page, gating decision, syndication, atomization (10 derivative pieces from one paper).

Three review passes in fixed order: SME for accuracy, editor for voice/structure, sponsor for strategic fit.

## 7. Evidence hierarchy

Strongest → weakest:

1. Independent analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, IDG, 451)
2. Peer-reviewed or government data
3. Original primary survey (n ≥ 100, methodology disclosed)
4. Named customer case data with numbers
5. Named industry-expert interview quotes
6. Trade-press reporting
7. Vendor's own benchmarks (only with methodology, never sole proof)
8. Anonymous customer quotes (color, not proof)

**Required source mix per problem/solution paper**: ≥1 analyst figure, ≥1 independent dataset, ≥2 expert/customer voices, ≥1 quantified outcome.

**Footnote everything that could be challenged.** No "studies show" without citation. Statistics older than three years are flagged or replaced.

## 8. Voice and tone

- **Vendor-neutral frame.** "Useful first, persuasive second." If a competitor could republish your paper with their logo and it would still be ~70% true, the framing is right.
- **Third person**, no "we/our/us" outside About + CTA.
- **No second person** except in CTAs.
- **Strike**: "leading," "best-in-class," "world-class," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "seamless," "robust," "synergy," "leverage" (verb).
- **Concrete > abstract.** Numbers, names, dates, dollar amounts.
- **Sentence average 17 words; max 30.** Active voice. Reading-grade 9–11.

## 9. Visuals (Chapter 13, Chapter 18)

- ~1 visual per 1–2 pages of text in problem/solution and backgrounders.
- ~25–35 % of page area is visual.
- Every visual stands alone with title, caption, source line.
- One idea per chart; label axes; cite source; no 3-D, no exploding pies.
- Pull-quotes are third-party voices only — never the vendor's CEO.
- Anti-patterns: stock handshakes/globes/gears, decorative visuals carrying no information, screenshots with unreadable text.

## 10. Length & exec summary discipline

| Flavor | Pages | Words | Exec summary |
|---|---|---|---|
| Backgrounder | 6–8 | 2,500–4,000 | 150–200 words |
| Numbered list | 5–7 + cover | 2,000–3,500 | optional, 100–150 |
| Problem/Solution | 8–12 (≤15) | 3,000–5,500 | 200–250 words |

Executive summary: written last, stand-alone, never longer than one page.

## 11. Anti-patterns (Chapter 17 — Ten White Paper Problems)

- Selling too hard / brochure-in-disguise.
- Naked claims; "studies show"; "industry-leading."
- Frankenpaper — multiple flavors mashed without discipline.
- Weak intro: "In today's…", company history, dictionary definition.
- No proof — zero analyst data, zero customer numbers, all vendor benchmarks.
- Strawmanned alternatives.
- Five CTAs instead of one.
- Wall of text with no visuals or callouts.
- Tone whiplash — neutral body then sudden marketing voice in the conclusion.
- Stale stats (>3 years without flag).
- Anonymous everything — no named expert, no named customer.
- No subtitle / no flavor signal.
- Buried CTA.
- Over-promising: "eliminates," "guarantees," "100%."

## 12. Ship gate

- [ ] Flavor labeled in subtitle and matches structure.
- [ ] Mantra test: paper helps a B2B buyer understand an issue, solve a problem, or make a decision.
- [ ] First 100 words contain no vendor/product (problem-solution and numbered-list).
- [ ] Single CTA; specific next step.
- [ ] Every load-bearing claim footnoted.
- [ ] Evidence mix met (analyst + independent + named voices + quantified outcome).
- [ ] Visual budget met; every visual stands alone with source.
- [ ] Executive summary stand-alone; under one page; written last.
- [ ] Vendor named no earlier than allowed for the flavor.
- [ ] Strike-list words audited.
- [ ] Page count within range.
