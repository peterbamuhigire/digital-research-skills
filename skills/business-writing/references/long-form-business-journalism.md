# Long-form business journalism

Craft lessons drawn from Dean Starkman, Martha Hamilton, Ryan Chittum & Felix Salmon (eds.), *The Best Business Writing 2014* (Columbia University Press / Columbia Journalism Review Books, 2014). The anthology is the engine's reference for what excellent business writing looks like at the **literary / journalism** register — long-form essays, investigative reporting, narrative profiles, magazine criticism, and explanatory finance pieces.

Use this reference when:

- The deliverable is a long-form magazine essay, op-ed, profile, investigative piece, or "explainer" feature — not a memo, brief, or report.
- The brief invites narrative, voice, and scene rather than recommendation.
- The writer needs an exemplar to anchor structure, voice, or pacing — and to see how master journalists handle the same craft problems the engine handles.

It does **not** replace `roman-raphaelson-principles.md` (sentence-level discipline) or `persuasion-and-narrative.md` (the four pillars). It supplies the next octave above them: pieces of journalism that *use* those principles to do real work.

## The four families in the anthology

The 31 pieces fall into four working families. Each family is a recognisable form the engine may be asked to write inside.

### 1. Long essay / criticism

System critique with the author's voice front and centre. Not a report — a sustained argument with literary scaffolding.

| Piece | Author | Outlet | Craft notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Why We Are Allowed to Hate Silicon Valley" | Evgeny Morozov | *Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung* | Three numbered reasons; each opens with a sharp claim and unpacks via concrete example; reframes the topic ("the digital debate" is the trap) before arguing |
| "Diary: Google Invades" | Rebecca Solnit | *London Review of Books* | Diaristic voice carries a structural argument; Google bus as recurring image; historical analogue (Gold Rush) does the heavy lifting in the second half |
| "Facebook Feminism, Like It or Not" | Susan Faludi | *The Baffler* | Opens with a vivid scene (Sandberg's auditorium); the scene *is* the analysis; quoted speech of the subject indicts itself |
| "Russell Brand and the GQ Awards" | Russell Brand | *Guardian* | First-person comic register; gets away with what most writers can't because the voice is the argument |

**Craft lessons.**
- A long essay earns its length by carrying a thesis through scenes, not through hedged paragraphs. State the thesis once, then dramatise it.
- Reframing — rejecting the terms in which the topic is usually discussed — is often the most generative move in the first 800 words.
- Voice is not optional in the long essay; voice *is* the structural choice that lets the argument breathe.

### 2. Investigative reporting

Documents-and-sources work that proves a claim about how something actually happened.

| Piece | Author | Outlet | Craft notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Secret Currency Traders' Club Devised Biggest Market's Rates" | Liam Vaughan, Gavin Finch, Bob Ivry | *Bloomberg* | Names, dates, dollar amounts, and chat-room transcripts do the work; the reporters stay invisible |
| "How the Case Against Bank of America CEO Fizzled" | Jesse Eisinger | *ProPublica* | Procedural argument; reconstructs decision-making inside the SEC; named officials, dated meetings, exact pleadings |
| "A Word from Our Sponsor" (Koch / PBS) | Jane Mayer | *The New Yorker* | Pattern reporting — six examples, same structure, builds a system claim from particulars |
| "Use Only as Directed" (Tylenol) | Jeff Gerth, T. Christian Miller | *ProPublica* | Decades-long timeline; one product; relentless documentary chain; FDA records throughout |
| "Merchants of Meth" | Jonah Engle | *Mother Jones* | Industry-influence reporting; the named-source pattern with public lobbying records |

**Craft lessons.**
- Investigative work proves a claim by stacking documentary anchors. Every meaningful sentence is footnotable in the writer's notes.
- Where insider quotation appears, it does new work — it is not decorative. If the quote could be paraphrased without loss, paraphrase.
- Pattern is the form: one example is anecdote; six is evidence. The transitions between examples are themselves structural argument.
- The reporter's voice is calm, neutral, almost absent. The drama is in the documents.

### 3. Narrative profile

A subject — person, company, product — used as a vehicle for a larger claim about an industry or moment.

| Piece | Author | Outlet | Craft notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "How Jenna Lyons Transformed J.Crew" | Danielle Sacks | *Fast Company* | Subject's voice and physical detail open; the brand transformation is shown through specific decisions |
| "The Mysterious Story of the Battery Startup" | Steve LeVine | *Quartz* | Engineering profile inside a corporate-failure narrative; technical detail used as plot |
| "Declara Co-Founder Ramona Pierson's Comeback" | Ashlee Vance | *Businessweek* | Personal arc + tech industry; the personal *is* the business angle |
| "Lunch with the FT: Meredith Whitney" | Lucy Kellaway | *Financial Times* | The interview-as-meal form; what the subject orders is character work; the reporter's reactions are a frame, not a topic |
| "A Toast Story" | John Gravois | *Pacific Standard* | Specific cultural artefact (toast at $4 a slice in San Francisco) becomes a window onto wider economic anxiety |

**Craft lessons.**
- The first paragraph either grounds the reader in a scene with the subject *or* states the larger claim and earns the right to a profile. Pick one; do not hedge.
- Specific physical and decision detail beats summary. *"She redrew the catalogue grid"* beats *"she reorganised brand presentation"*.
- The "small object as window" form (toast, the Google bus, the Shard) is one of the most reliable ways to make business writing literary without losing rigour.

### 4. Explainer / magazine feature

A complex system explained through a particular case or story arc.

| Piece | Author | Outlet | Craft notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "How the Fed Let the World Blow Up in 2008" | Matthew O'Brien | *The Atlantic* | Chronology-driven; FOMC meeting transcripts as primary evidence; one paragraph per inflection point |
| "Maximizing Shareholder Value: The Goal That Changed Corporate America" | Jia Lynn Yang | *Washington Post* | Idea-history form; one named idea (Friedman 1970) traced through institutional consequences |
| "How Technology and Hefty Subsidies Make U.S. Cotton King" | NPR Planet Money team | *All Things Considered* | Broadcast-style chase narrative ("we follow a t-shirt") translated to print; one supply chain, one product |
| "The Extraordinary Science of Addictive Junk Food" | Michael Moss | *NYT Magazine* | One trade-association meeting as opening scene; corporate-archive deep dive as backbone |
| "Invisible Child" | Andrea Elliott | *New York Times* | Single subject (a homeless girl) carries the systemic argument about NYC's shelter system; multi-day series; verité scene-work |

**Craft lessons.**
- Chronology is the most under-used structural pattern in business writing. When the question is "how did we get here?", chronology beats every other arrangement.
- The "follow one object through the system" form (the t-shirt, the toast, Dasani's school day) is the engine's most reliable explainer scaffold.
- A piece can run 5,000–25,000 words if every section earns its place. The cut is at the section level, not the sentence level.

## Cross-cutting craft moves

Patterns that recur across all four families and are worth lifting wholesale:

1. **Open with a scene the reader can see, then earn the abstraction.** The unmarked luxury bus, the Sandberg auditorium, the toast café, the trade-association meeting — every essay opens at a specific time and place with a specific person.
2. **Name the system, don't only name the symptom.** Morozov names "the digital debate" so he can refuse it. Yang names "shareholder value" so she can trace it. The naming is the move.
3. **Make the invisible economics visible.** The Google bus is a transit story until Solnit shows the eviction map under it. The Shard is architecture until Shaxson shows the laundry. This is the engine's home turf — translate financial / structural claims into things the reader can see.
4. **Quoted insider voice does real work.** When a chat-room transcript or an FOMC transcript appears, it is the load-bearing evidence, not decoration. If the quote can be paraphrased, paraphrase.
5. **Long sentences earn their length only when they enact a thought.** Solnit's longest sentences imitate the sweep of a city changing; Faludi's enact the relentlessness of corporate sloganeering. Don't write a long sentence unless the thought is long.
6. **The reporter's I- voice is a tool, not a default.** It works in the diary (Solnit), the personal essay (Brand, Arnade), the lunch (Kellaway), the gonzo entry (Roose's Wall Street secret society). It does not work in the investigative piece. Match register to evidence regime.
7. **End on consequence, not summary.** Easterbrook on the NFL: *"Football is the king of sports. Should the favorite sport of the greatest nation really be one whose economic structure is based on inequality and greed?"* The closing question sticks because the case has been made.

## Anti-patterns the anthology avoids

- **The trend-piece tell:** *"In an era when …"* / *"Today's fast-paced …"*. None of these pieces opens with a generic temporal claim.
- **The expert-quote stack:** five experts in three paragraphs. The anthology's investigative pieces use experts sparingly; the long essays often use none.
- **The thesis-by-paragraph-five.** Every piece states or dramatises its claim by the third paragraph. Anthology editors threw out the ones that didn't.
- **The wrap-up that summarises what was just said.** The endings *enact* a consequence, ask a question, or land an image; they do not paraphrase the body.
- **The named subject without a named voice.** Profiles in the anthology give the reader a sentence the subject actually said — or document why no such sentence exists.

## When to use which family

| Brief looks like | Family to study |
|---|---|
| "We need a piece arguing that X is wrong about Y" | **Long essay / criticism** — Morozov, Faludi |
| "We have documents that prove X did Y" | **Investigative reporting** — Vaughan/Finch/Ivry, Mayer |
| "Profile this person / company / product" | **Narrative profile** — Sacks, Vance, Kellaway |
| "Explain how X works to a smart non-specialist" | **Explainer / feature** — O'Brien, Yang, Moss |

Match the form to the evidence and the question; do not pick the form by length.
