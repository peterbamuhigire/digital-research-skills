# Scenario playbooks (Ramsey four-axis frame)

Encodes Jon Ramsey, *Business Writing Scenarios* (Bedford/St Martin's, 2017) into the engine. Use whenever the deliverable is a one-off communication artefact — an email, memo, letter, or short document — that has to land an unwelcome message, request something delicate, defend a decision, or repair a relationship.

## The four-axis frame

Before drafting any scenario-based artefact, write down four things:

1. **The background.** What has just happened? What is the history? What does the reader already know, suspect, fear, or assume?
2. **The purpose.** What should the reader think, feel, and do after reading? One sentence.
3. **The audience.** Who is the named reader? What is their relationship to you, their authority, their likely emotional state, and their cultural register?
4. **The communication strategy.** What tone, what structure, what length, what channel, and what call to action will serve the purpose with this audience given the background?

This is the same frame the rest of `business-writing` uses (Maxwell Step 1) — Ramsey's contribution is making it the **mandatory front-matter** for any scenario where the message is hard. Skipping the frame is the cause of every gaffe in the book.

## The seven communication purposes (Ramsey ch. 1)

Every business message serves at least one of these purposes. Naming the purpose dictates the structure.

| Purpose | Lead with | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Explain / clarify / inform** | The fact, the change, or the answer | Background that buries the news |
| **Defend** | The decision and the principle behind it | Long apologetic preambles |
| **Negotiate** | What you can offer; what you need | Ultimatums; emotional anchoring |
| **Appease / mollify / apologise** | Acknowledgment of the harm; specific corrective | Defensiveness, "I'm sorry if you feel" |
| **Persuade / request / market** | The reader's interest, framed first | "I" sentences; abstract benefits |
| **Construct policy / procedure** | Effective date, scope, who is affected | Buried effective dates; passive voice on enforcement |
| **Propose new direction** | The recommendation; then the case for it | Building up to a recommendation in the last paragraph |

When the deliverable serves two purposes (e.g., explain *and* mollify; defend *and* persuade), choose the dominant one and give the secondary one a single paragraph in service of the first.

## Conveying negative news (Ramsey ch. 5–6)

The hardest scenario family. Eleven named pitfalls — every one is a real failure mode the engine has seen in client work.

| Pitfall | Symptom | Cure |
|---|---|---|
| Wanting your audience to like you | Friendship language muddles the bad news | Express empathy, not friendship: *"This change will be a challenge to all of us."* |
| More dramatic than empathetic | "Awful problem for all of us" | *"An unfortunate and, we trust, temporary necessity."* |
| Minimising the impact | Furloughs reframed as "more time with family" | Tell it straight, with sympathetic acknowledgement |
| Obfuscating | "Usual market cycles" instead of the real cause | State the cause; readers will smell evasion |
| Seeming aloof | "Better you than me" tone | Demonstrate that you are affected too |
| Burning bridges | Combative reply to angry email | Stay above the fray; protect future communication |
| Sweetening bitter news | "Have a nice day" closing | Close on respect, not cheer |
| Clouding your purpose | Reader cannot tell if this is a decision or a consultation | Name it: *"This memo announces a decision"* or *"This memo seeks input"* |
| Too much information | Five pages of pension calculations | Two examples by salary band |
| Too little information | "We are reducing tech support" with no context | The decision, the reason, the future provision |
| One communication is enough | Single email about a sensitive change | Plan a series: pre-announcement, announcement, Q&A, follow-up |
| Unrealistic expectations | "Brighter times ahead" hints when they are not | Say what you know; do not promise what you cannot deliver |

Pair this list with the HBR "ease into bad news" letter pattern in `roman-raphaelson-principles.md` — that pattern is the **letter form**; this list is the **scenario discipline** that surrounds it.

## Responding to a discourteous communication

Pattern from Ramsey ch. 5. When a senior colleague or client sends an angry, vague, or unreasonable message, do not reply in kind. The engine's default reply is built from four moves:

1. **Acknowledge the dissatisfaction without conceding the substance.** *"I am sorry the figures we provided are not as convincing as they might be."*
2. **Restate what you have already done, briefly.** *"The team has already conducted a month of research."*
3. **Ask a precise clarifying question that forces the angry party to specify their dissatisfaction.** *"It would help us greatly if you could provide a few more specifics about the costs you believe we could meet."*
4. **Offer a realistic next step with a concrete date.** *"With your further guidance we could submit by this Wednesday a more focused report."*

Never apologise for the substance unless the substance was wrong. Apologise for the friction; defend the work.

## Distinguishing collegial from professional voice

Two replies to the same request — *"Can you send me the financial information we discussed?"*

```
NOT:  Yo Jack — That thing we talked about yesterday won't be ready until later.
      It'll be a top priority for sure, but we need to get some other stuff
      together before we can produce it. Stay tuned. ~ Jill

BUT:  Dear Jack — As it turns out, the further analysis of our 1999 tax filings
      that we discussed yesterday will take a few days longer than I had thought.
      The records for 2000 and earlier are in our warehouse and will need to be
      retrieved. I will make every effort to have the analysis in your hands no
      later than Friday, 30 September. Thanks for your patience. ~ Jill
```

Voice rules for the professional version:
- Specific reference to the prior conversation (no *"that thing"*).
- A reason for the delay (the warehouse trip).
- A specific commitment date.
- Closing thanks for the patience already extended.

The casual version is fine in chat with a friend; in a workplace where Jill's competence is the actual subject, it is dangerous.

## A library of scenarios the engine handles

The frame above applies to all of these. Each requires the four axes resolved before drafting; each maps to a purpose and inherits the corresponding pattern.

| Scenario | Purpose | Pattern reference |
|---|---|---|
| Explaining a workplace disruption | Inform + mollify | Bad-news pattern (HBR ch. 19) |
| Politely declining a colleague's request | Negotiate + preserve relationship | "Ease into bad news" letter form |
| Conveying bad news about benefits | Inform | Twelve negative-news pitfalls (above) |
| Conveying bad news about a discontinued perk | Inform + mollify | Show the work behind the perk; explain why it cannot continue |
| Requesting a promotion | Persuade | Lead with the reader's interest (the work delivered, not the rewards sought) |
| Requesting information about office supplies | Inform | Subject line carries the verb; specific list and deadline |
| Revising a poor communication about office space | Inform | Lead with effective date; explain rationale once, briefly |
| Revising a poor communication about child-care policies | Construct policy | Effective date, scope, eligibility, enforcement, where to ask |
| Revising a poor communication about flexible work schedules | Inform + consult | Make explicit whether decision or consultation |
| Revising a poor communication about parking | Inform | Acknowledge inconvenience; do not signal personal exemption |
| Apologising for an offensive employee email | Defend + correct | Identify the specific behaviour; cite the policy; state consequence |
| Responding to a former colleague seeking confidential information | Defend | Friendly refusal with the principle, not the suspicion |
| Replying to a discourteous boss email | Negotiate | The four-move pattern above |
| Inquiring about office-space needs | Inform + request | One specific question per email; deadline |
| Seeking volunteers for a non-profit | Persuade | Reader's interest first; named ask; specific commitment |

## A worked scenario

**Scenario.** The CEO who used to buy each employee a Thanksgiving turkey can no longer continue. (Ramsey ch. 5.)

**Background.** The perk has run for five years. Many employees see it as an entitlement. The actual labour and personal cost (visiting twelve supermarkets, buying 250 turkeys) was never visible.

**Purpose.** Tell employees the gift will not happen this year, without breeding resentment.

**Audience.** All staff. Mixed length of service. Some will not have known the gift was personally funded.

**Communication strategy.** Explain the history briefly; show the work; thank the employees; do not promise the gift will return.

**Off-putting first draft (what not to do):**

> *"I am sorry to report that I can no longer afford to purchase a turkey for every employee's family. I know you have enjoyed this tradition since 2011, but times have changed. I do wish you all the best in your Thanksgiving celebrations."*

The history is missing. The reason is vague ("times have changed"). The closing is a non sequitur. Employees feel arbitrarily docked.

**Revised:**

> *"Many of you will recall that, in 2011, I supplied each of my 250 staff colleagues with a turkey for Thanksgiving. The purchase was made with my own funds and as a gesture of thanks to the many dedicated co-workers who had led us to prosperous times after several years of severe financial constraints.*
>
> *Your pleasure in receiving the unexpected gift inspired me to provide the same token of appreciation for Thanksgiving 2012, 2013, 2014, and 2015. As much as I would like to continue this tradition, I find that I no longer have the personal or financial resources to visit the twelve supermarkets in our area that have in the past supplied me with the turkeys needed. I hope you will understand that I continue to appreciate your wonderful dedication to our company.*
>
> *Please accept my thanks during this season of celebrations as we contemplate our good fortune on so many levels. I hope the happiness you experience this Thanksgiving with friends and family will not be diminished by my inability to contribute to the feast."*

The revised version: shows the gift was personal; explains the labour involved; thanks the staff; does not promise the gift will return; avoids "have a nice day" sweetening.

## Anti-patterns specific to scenarios

- **Drafting before resolving the four axes.** The single biggest cause of scenario gaffes.
- **Treating angry input as a script.** Replying in kind escalates; replying with the four-move pattern de-escalates.
- **Confusing announcement with consultation.** State the mode in the first sentence. *This memo announces …* or *This memo invites your input on …*
- **Single communication on a multi-stage change.** Sensitive changes need a sequence: pre-announcement to leaders, announcement to all, open Q&A, follow-up FAQ.
- **Using friendship voice as a shortcut.** "Yo Jack" is fine with friends, dangerous with colleagues, fatal with senior stakeholders.
- **Trying to be liked while delivering bad news.** Empathy is not friendship.
