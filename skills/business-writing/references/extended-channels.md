# Extended channels

Additional channel guides not covered in the original `roman-raphaelson-principles.md` and `web-copywriting.md`. Drawn from Henwood, *Business Writing for Innovators and Change-Makers* (2020); Lindsell-Roberts, *Business Writing with AI for Dummies* (2024); and Garner, *HBR Guide to Better Business Writing* (2013).

For longer-form forms (full reports, proposals, white papers in the strict B2B-publication sense, full grant applications), defer to `report-and-proposal-craft`.

## Pitch decks (Henwood ch. 8)

**What it is.** A short slide presentation, usually 10–20 slides, used to attract investor interest. The job of the deck is **not** to close investment — it is to win an in-person meeting.

**Format choice — first decision:**
- **Standalone deck (Slidedoc®).** No live narrator. Allows 11–12 pt text. Visuals plus short paragraphs of supporting text on each slide.
- **Live-presentation deck.** Larger fonts (20 pt boardroom, 22+ pt auditorium). Visuals carry the slide; the speaker carries the argument.
- **Both.** Use full-sentence slide headlines so messages land without narration; extend the slide count rather than crowding each slide.

**Three critical questions every deck must answer:**

1. **What is your business?** Market need, value proposition, business model, team, traction, customers.
2. **Why will you succeed?** Competitive analysis (the most-skipped section), market trends, defensibility, response to anticipated competition.
3. **What will the ROI be?** Financial projections with stated assumptions, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, spending plan.

**Four-part structure (Henwood synthesis of DocSend's 10 essential topics):**

| Section | Slides | Topics |
|---|---|---|
| 1. The Opportunity | 5 | Company purpose · Problem · Solution · Why now? · Market size |
| 2. The Company | 2 | Product · Team |
| 3. The Model | 2 | Business model · Competition |
| 4. The ROI | 1 | Financials |

Anchor sources: DocSend's 2019 study of 200 successful seed decks found an average of 19 slides and 3 minutes 44 seconds of investor attention per deck.

**Five investor turn-offs in the financials section (Henwood):**

1. Downplaying the risk.
2. Underestimating the timeline (Davidson's rule of thumb: assume 2× your initial estimate).
3. Assuming the numbers speak for themselves.
4. Leaving the customer out of the picture (CAC, LTV, willingness-to-buy evidence).
5. Omitting a spending plan.

**Visual discipline.** Each slide centres on one compelling visual that (a) is crystal clear and (b) directly supports the slide's headline. Irrelevant visuals are the most common design failure.

**Process.** Use the **accordion method** (`maxwell-7-steps.md` § Pipher / Henwood): expand to capture every detail, contract to fit the slide budget, expand again to restore load-bearing detail, contract once more.

**Get feedback before pitching.** Trusted readers with investor experience or who can role-play one. Most companies that successfully raise contact 20–30 investors first.

**Checklist:**

- [ ] Format chosen (standalone vs live)
- [ ] Three critical questions answered
- [ ] All ten essential topics covered, in the four-part structure
- [ ] Story of Our Shared Success threads through the deck
- [ ] Risks named with mitigation
- [ ] Financials interpreted in text, not left to graphs alone
- [ ] Detailed financials in appendix
- [ ] One compelling visual per slide; no irrelevant visuals
- [ ] Generous white space
- [ ] Tested with at least one trusted reader

## One-pagers (Henwood ch. 11)

**What it is.** A single-page (occasionally two-page) promotional sheet describing a company, product, service, or capability. Different from a flyer (single event) and from a brochure (longer sales document). Used at trade shows, after exploratory meetings, as conversation focus, or as an investor pitch summary.

**The job.** Move the reader **one step** along the customer journey — not to close a sale.

**Design — four anchors:**

1. **Page layout.** At least one inch of margin all around. Aim for balance, not symmetry. Plan in thirds rather than quarters.
2. **Colour.** Same palette as your other branding. Maximum five colours.
3. **Shapes.** Squares and rectangles are easy to balance. Circles for holistic / organic brands. Triangles for sharp focus. Don't mix more than two systems.
4. **Fonts.** Minimum 12 pt (14 pt if mainly digital). Maximum two font families.

**Writing — minimalist style:**

- Short word over long word (*get*, *buy* over *obtain*, *purchase*).
- Cut every avoidable word: *there is/are* throat-clearing, optional *that*, low-value adjectives and adverbs (*very, quite, dramatically, significantly, phenomenally*).
- Turn paragraphs into lists when content deserves the white-space emphasis.
- Conversational over technically correct (e.g., *like* instead of *such as* is fine on a one-pager).
- Second reader pass — fresh eyes will find more cuts.

**Four messaging tips:**

1. **Focus on the next step, not the sale.** Your CTA is "phone us", "visit the page", "email for a consult", not "buy now".
2. **One clear CTA.** Plain, conversational, not flashing-neon.
3. **Headline that evokes emotion** (fear, curiosity, desire). ≤ 10 words.
4. **Apply the Think / Feel / Do test.** What should the reader be thinking, feeling, and doing immediately after reading? If you produce long, rambling answers, the one-pager has not focused enough.

**Testimonials.** A one-pager has no room for hard data, so social proof carries the validation. For each testimonial:

- Full name, title, organisation (testimonials without these are not worth much).
- Specific outcome (*"increased profits by 23 % in the first year"*, not *"a great experience"*).
- Coach the testimonial-giver — most appreciate guidelines.

**Multi-track design.** Heath & Heath's *Decisive*: develop two or three designs in parallel rather than iterating one design six times. A 2010 study (Dow et al., *ACM TOCHI* 17.4) found parallel-track designs outperformed serial designs on click-through, time on site, and designer self-efficacy.

**Checklist:**

- [ ] At least one-inch margins
- [ ] ≥ 12 pt body font
- [ ] ≤ 5 colours, aligned with brand
- [ ] Headline that evokes emotion
- [ ] One clear CTA
- [ ] Minimalist, conversational copy
- [ ] At least one specific testimonial
- [ ] Think / Feel / Do test passed
- [ ] Two or three design variants compared

## Training material (Henwood ch. 12)

**What it is.** Workshops, instruction manuals, online courses, video tutorials — any content whose purpose is to move a learner from ignorance to competence.

**The neurological premise.** New learning happens by connecting to existing knowledge. The trainer's primary job is to find that connection point — not to display expertise.

**Five principles:**

1. **Meet the audience where they are, not where you wish they were.** Most learners are rushed; few care about theory; many lack assumed-baseline knowledge; most need help connecting concepts to practice; many bring fear of trying new things.
2. **Map a clear learning path.** Overall description, specific outcomes (*"By the end of this training, you will be able to …"*), staged journey, small sections, descriptive headings, repeated summarisation.
3. **Make it memorable.** Generate emotional resonance; invent mnemonics; chunk into groups of five or fewer (Cowan's 4 ± 1 has displaced Miller's 7 ± 2 as the better approximation); incorporate visuals (the pictorial superiority effect).
4. **Provide examples and practice.** SMEs assume learners can connect dots; they cannot. Realistic examples and practice exercises with feedback bridge concept to practice.
5. **Create tools for the real world.** Worksheets, checklists, pre-formatted spreadsheets, short videos. The reader's goal is not to know — it is to do.

**Voice rules.**
- Empathetic, encouraging tone — many adult learners bring negative learning histories.
- Aim for Grade 8 reading level (Wintink's rule for non-academic audiences).
- Strip "icing on the cake" — the theory the SME loves but the learner does not need.

**Failure mode.** Treating training as explanation. Training is **motivation plus explanation plus practice plus tools** — explanation alone teaches no one.

**Checklist:**

- [ ] Audience analysis written before drafting
- [ ] Practical content > theory
- [ ] Empathetic, encouraging tone
- [ ] Section overviews, descriptive headings, regular summaries
- [ ] Mnemonics for the 3–5 most important concepts
- [ ] Chunks of ≤ 5 items
- [ ] Visuals on every key concept
- [ ] Examples and practice for every skill taught
- [ ] At least one usable real-world tool delivered with the training

## Business plans (Henwood ch. 9)

**What it is.** A document for a lender or angel investor demonstrating that a business will succeed and repay the investment. **Not** an internal operating manual — that is a different document.

**The lender's three questions:**

1. What is the opportunity?
2. Why is it significant?
3. How will the business pay the bank back?

**Form.** No universal template. Length varies by lender and stage. A five-page plan with thoughtful analysis can outperform a fifty-page document with poor analysis. **Meet the lender early** to learn what they look for; tailor accordingly.

**Story vs argument — critical distinction.**

| Mode | Structure | Where it fits in a business plan |
|---|---|---|
| Story | Linear: situation → escalation → crisis → resolution | Founder origin; how the company got here; vision of future success |
| Argument | Hierarchical: one main claim → sub-claims → evidence | Financial case; competitive defensibility; repayment path |

A business plan is **fundamentally an argument** that the business will succeed and repay. Story has a supporting role; if story carries the document, the lender has nothing to evaluate.

The single core claim: *this business will succeed and repay the lender.* Every section, every chart, every paragraph either supports that claim, develops a sub-claim that supports it, or supplies evidence under a sub-claim. Anything outside this hierarchy is decoration.

**Five-page structure (lender-orientated):**

1. Executive summary — the answer to all three questions in 250–500 words.
2. Opportunity — market need, evidence of demand, customer segment.
3. Plan — what the business does and how it makes money.
4. Repayment — financial projections, base assumptions, sensitivity, risk mitigations (especially client-concentration risk).
5. Team and execution — why this team, why now.

**Common failure.** A page of irrelevant detail (e.g., contract paperwork for a single major client) that highlights a risk rather than mitigating it. Always think about how each detail looks to the lender's risk lens.

**Checklist:**

- [ ] Met with the lender before drafting
- [ ] Three lender questions answered explicitly
- [ ] Argument hierarchy mapped before drafting
- [ ] Story used for context, not for the case
- [ ] Financials with stated assumptions and a sensitivity analysis
- [ ] Risks named with mitigation
- [ ] Length matched to the lender, not to a template

For larger or more formal plans, defer to `report-and-proposal-craft`.

## Grant proposals (Lindsell-Roberts ch. 17 · Henwood ch. 7)

**What it is.** A document seeking funding from a foundation, government agency, or institutional donor. Length and form prescribed by the funder; content shaped by the funder's priorities.

**Read the call before drafting.** A grant proposal is a response to a specific request; not a general solicitation. Funder priorities, criteria, and word limits override every general guidance below.

**Standard structure (where the funder does not prescribe one):**

1. Cover sheet / abstract — who, what, how much, by when.
2. Statement of need — sized, evidenced, recent.
3. Aims and objectives.
4. Methodology / approach — what you will do and how.
5. Workplan / timeline.
6. Team and capability.
7. Outputs, outcomes, indicators.
8. Sustainability beyond the grant.
9. Budget and budget narrative.
10. Monitoring, evaluation, and reporting plan.
11. Risks and mitigations.
12. Annexes — letters of support, CVs, registration documents.

**Voice rules.**
- Confident but not over-claiming. Funders penalise both timidity and hubris.
- Statement of need cites recent data, with sources.
- Methodology must be reproducible from the proposal alone.
- Budget must reconcile to the methodology — every line item must be explainable from the workplan.

**AI-aware production.** Lindsell-Roberts ch. 17 details prompts that produce candidate sections rapidly. The engine treats AI-generated proposal text as **draft only** until the writer has verified every statistic, every cited paper, and every named partner.

**Failure modes.**
- Copy-pasting boilerplate from a previous proposal where the call differs.
- Statement of need with no recent local data.
- Budget that exceeds funder ceiling, or that does not match the methodology.
- Missing sustainability section — almost every funder asks for it.

For research-funding proposals (academic ethics + supervisory + funder), defer also to `research-output-formats/references/academic-formats.md` § Research-proposal.

## Performance appraisals (Garner / HBR ch. 21)

**What it is.** A written evaluation of an employee's work over a defined period. Usually annual; carries professional and sometimes legal weight.

**The discipline.** Specific behaviour → calibrated phrase → particular consequence. Each phrase is anchored to a named action. Performative kindness and performative criticism are equally useless.

**Five-band calibration scale (per dimension):** Superb / Good / Acceptable / Needs Improvement / Poor.

**Seven dimensions covered:**

| Dimension | Sample phrase pairs (Superb → Poor) |
|---|---|
| Attitude | shows unwavering commitment → lacks commitment |
| Efficiency | never wastes time or effort → often wastes time and effort |
| Human relations | demonstrates keen sensitivity → rarely pays attention to others' reactions |
| Judgment | makes excellent decisions → exhibits poor judgment |
| Knowledge | has comprehensive expertise → lacks needed knowledge |
| Reliability | always delivers on commitments → cannot be counted on |
| Communication skills | communicates clearly and persuasively → struggles to communicate clearly |

**Worked sentence (HBR ch. 21):**

> *"When we had several layoffs last June, Lauren remained utterly calm and collected while demonstrating keen sensitivity to those who lost their jobs. She [specific action]."*

The calibrated phrase ("remained utterly calm and collected", "demonstrating keen sensitivity") sits between the **incident** and the **specific action**. Without the incident, the phrase is hot air. Without the specific action, the phrase is undefended.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person, formal but warm.
- "Needs improvement" and "Poor" descriptions stay specific, never personal.
- Future-oriented close: what would success look like next cycle?

**Anti-patterns.**
- Vague praise without behaviour. *"Great team player"* (compared to whom, doing what?).
- Vague criticism without behaviour. *"Could improve attitude"* (which behaviour, on which occasion?).
- Calibration drift — every phrase rated "Good" because Good is comfortable.
- Surprise content — the appraisal raises issues the employee has never been told about.
