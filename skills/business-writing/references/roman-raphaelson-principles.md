# Writing That Works (Roman & Raphaelson)

Encodes Kenneth Roman & Joel Raphaelson's *Writing That Works, 3e: How to Communicate Effectively in Business* as the engine's prose-quality baseline. Apply alongside `business-writing-7-steps` (process) and `reports-proposals-craft` (structure).

## Master rule: Don't mumble

> *"It is generally desirable to communicate your thoughts in a forthright manner."* — *Don't mumble.* Toning your point down and tiptoeing tempts the reader to tune out.

Every sentence the engine produces must carry its point at full volume. Hedge soup is a Roman/Raphaelson cardinal sin.

## The 18 Principles

1. **Make the organization of your writing clear.** Tell the reader where you are going. Outline first; signal major points with section headings; end with a summary (not a conclusion — summary introduces no new ideas).
2. **Use short paragraphs, short sentences, and short words.** Wall Street Journal first paragraphs ≤3 sentences. Prefer the short word: *now* over *currently*, *start* over *initiate*, *show* over *indicate*, *finish* over *finalize*, *use* over *utilize*, *put* over *position*.
3. **Make your writing active — and personal.** Active voice over passive whenever possible. Active verbs add energy; passive hides who acts.
4. **Avoid vague adjectives and adverbs.** "Significant," "substantial," "very," "really" — strike them. Specifics replace them: *18 % growth*, not *significant growth*.
5. **Use down-to-earth language.** Anglo-Saxon over Latinate. Reader should not need a dictionary.
6. **Be specific.** Numbers, names, dates, places. "Saved £42k in Q2" beats "achieved efficiencies."
7. **Choose the right word.** *Imply* / *infer*. *Comprise* / *compose*. *Affect* / *effect*. Look it up; getting it wrong loses the deep reader.
8. **Make it perfect.** Typos, factual errors, mis-spelled names — kill credibility. Proof everything.
9. **Come to the point.** Lead with the news. Bury nothing.
10. **Write simply and naturally — the way you talk.** Read aloud; if it doesn't sound like speech, rewrite.
11. **Strike out words you don't need.** "In order to" → "to." "At this point in time" → "now." "Due to the fact that" → "because."
12. **Use current standard English.** Not slang; not 1960s formal. Read what your reader reads.
13. **Don't write like a lawyer or a bureaucrat.** No "heretofore," "pursuant to," "the aforementioned."
14. **Keep in mind what your reader doesn't know.** Define terms once; explain context that you live in but they don't.
15. **Punctuate carefully.** Comma splices, missing serial commas, and dropped quotation marks all signal carelessness.
16. **Understate rather than overstate.** "We grew" beats "we revolutionized growth."
17. **Write so that you cannot be misunderstood.** Ambiguity is the writer's problem, not the reader's.
18. **Use plain English even on technical subjects.** No subject is too complex for clear language; complexity is an excuse for vague writing.

## Per-channel rules

### Email (Chapter 4)
1. Make the subject heading clear and compelling — it must signal the action or the topic.
2. Cut to the point — the ask in the first line.
3. Avoid e-mail tag — answer all open questions in one reply.
4. Set the right tone of voice — assume the message will be forwarded.
5. Default ≤200 words; one ask per email; CC sparingly; never reply-all by reflex.

### Memos and letters (Chapter 5)
1. Put a title on every memo.
2. Address memos only to the person who must take action.
3. Make your structure obvious — short numbered sections.
4. Letters that get results: open with the reader's interest, not yours; close with the specific action requested by a date.

### Presentations and speeches (Chapter 6)
- Open with a hook the audience cannot ignore.
- Tell them what you'll tell them; tell them; tell them what you told them.
- One idea per slide; few slides; few words per slide.
- Close with one specific request.

### Plans and reports (Chapter 7)
- Lead with conclusions, not history.
- Numbered recommendations with owners and dates.
- Executive summary stand-alone.

### Recommendations and proposals (Chapter 8)
- State the recommendation in the first sentence of the document.
- Frame benefits in the reader's terms — what they gain, save, avoid.
- Anticipate objections and answer them inside the proposal.

### Sales and fund-raising letters (Chapter 9)
- Open with the prospect's problem or aspiration, not your organisation.
- Specific stories beat statistics; statistics beat adjectives.
- Single ask; specific amount or action; deadline.
- P.S. line is the most-read line — use it for the ask.

### Resumes and cover letters (Chapter 11)
- Cover letter: customised; one page; specific to the role.
- Resume: achievements with numbers; reverse chronology; one page if possible, two if necessary.
- No photos; no fluff objectives.

## Editing yourself (Chapter 12)

Roman/Raphaelson's editing pipeline:

1. **Write first; format later.** Do not interrupt drafting to format.
2. **Practice the Rule of More Than One.** Revise more than once; the first revision is the structural pass; subsequent passes refine.
3. **Give thought to your file names** — versioning matters.
4. **Read aloud** — if your tongue stumbles, the reader will too.
5. **Cut by 25 % on the first revision.** Almost any first draft tightens.
6. **Strip every unnecessary modifier.**
7. **Make every sentence carry weight** — if a sentence can go without loss, it goes.

## Anti-patterns (named in the book)

- **Mumbling** — soft openings, hedged claims, evasive verbs.
- **Latinate fog** — *facilitate, utilize, leverage, optimize, expedite*.
- **Lawyer/bureaucrat voice** — *heretofore, pursuant, said party*.
- **Passive evasion** — "it was decided that…" hiding the actor.
- **Adjective inflation** — *significant, substantial, world-class, leading*.
- **Walls of text** — paragraphs over 5 sentences without break.
- **Buried lead** — recommendation on page 4.
- **One-size email** — the same long email used for an ask, an FYI, and a heads-up.
- **Resume objective fluff** — "seeking a challenging position…"
- **PS as afterthought** instead of the strongest closing.
- **Too many sentences in one paragraph; too many ideas in one sentence.**

## Consolidated checklist

- [ ] First sentence carries the news.
- [ ] No Latinate substitutes where Anglo-Saxon works.
- [ ] No vague adjectives ("significant," "substantial," "very," "really").
- [ ] Average sentence ≤20 words; max 30; varied length.
- [ ] Every paragraph: one idea, ≤5 sentences.
- [ ] Active voice unless deliberate.
- [ ] Numbers, names, dates instead of adjectives.
- [ ] Read aloud — no stumbles, no jargon.
- [ ] First-revision cut ≥25 %.
- [ ] No bureaucrat words (*heretofore, pursuant, the aforementioned*).
- [ ] Channel rule applied (email, memo, letter, plan, presentation, proposal, resume).
- [ ] Names spelled correctly; numbers reconcile; one factual proof pass.

The Warren Buffett Test: take any paragraph and rewrite it the way Buffett would in a Berkshire Hathaway letter. If your rewrite is materially shorter and clearer, ship the rewrite. ## Garner / HBR — tone

From *HBR Guide*, ch. 17 ("Watch your tone"):

- **Avoid hyperformality.** Write more or less as you would speak. *How may I help you?* not *How may I be of assistance?* *David Green*, not *the above-mentioned patient*. *We recommend*, not *it is recommended by the undersigned*.
- **Be collegial.** Imagine the message paraded in a contentious lawsuit; you want the jury to think you behaved admirably. Aggressive stance only as a last resort, on advice of counsel.
- **Match register to the relationship.** "How would I say this if she were right here with me?" Not chummy with strangers; not distant with close colleagues.
- **Drop sarcasm.** Sarcasm doesn't shame readers into compliance — it irritates and alienates. There is no professional context where sarcasm helps.
- **Never make the reader admit they are in the wrong.** Strike *you labor under a delusion*, *fail to understand*, *erroneously assert*, *distort*. They breed ill will and rarely change a mind.

## Garner / HBR — chronology

From ch. 12. When you give a factual account, **plot a chronology of relevant events first, then string it into prose**. Common failure mode: the writer dives into the middle of the story and the reader has to back-fill. The fix is mechanical:

```
Last week — Sarah asked me to gauge X's interest.
Today    — I spoke with Jim Martinez.
Tuesday  — Jim and I meet at his office.
Two weeks — If interested, NDA → sample app → demo.
```

Once the chronology is on paper, the email or memo is just an act of stringing.

## Garner / HBR — letters

From ch. 19. Three patterns the engine will use repeatedly.

**Open with you, not I.** *What a wonderful host you were last week*, not *I just thought I'd drop you a note*. The reader's interest holds the reader's attention.

**Ease into bad news.** When the answer is "no", sandwich it: positive opening → reason → decision → forward-looking close. Never start with the rejection. Never overexplain.

```
NOT: We regret to inform you that we cannot supply the 500 copies at the 60 % discount you requested. No one — not even our authors — receives such a hefty discount. If you would care to resubmit at the more modest figure of 30 %, we will gladly consider...

BUT: How rewarding to hear that you intend to use Negotiate It Now! as part of your business summit. You've chosen the best book on the subject. Although you've requested a 60 % discount, the most we can offer is 30 % — that's the largest discount available to anyone, and we're happy to extend it to you with a purchase of 500 copies.
```

**Don't write in anger.** A combative letter loses customers; rejection rarely needs the prosecution's voice. *Please* and *thank you* are mandatory in complaint replies.

**Fund-raising letters** must (1) appeal directly from one person to another, (2) frame an opportunity for the recipient to satisfy personal needs by supporting a worthwhile aim, and (3) prompt one specific, decisive action. The eight donor motivations to weave in: belief in impact, belief in your kind of organisation, recognition, association with respected figures, sense of belonging, relief of fear or guilt, sense of duty, tax benefits.

## Garner / HBR — memos and short reports

From ch. 20.

**Subject lines do work, or they don't.** *"Approval of Siegelson Acquisition"* beats *"Siegelson"*. *"Why We Should Reject Frost's Settlement Offer"* beats *"Settlement"*. The subject promises either a status update or a recommendation — never a riddle.

**Summary block at the top.** For each main issue (no more than three):

```
Issue:           [the problem in one sentence anyone can understand]
Proposed Solution: [your recommendation]
Reason:          [why it works, or why it beats the alternatives]
```

This three-line summary is the executive's read; the body unpacks it for fact-checkers and future readers, who are also legitimate audiences.

**Recommendations spell out:** what needs to be done, who should do it, when and where, why, and how.

## Garner / HBR — performance appraisals

From ch. 21. Appraisals follow a fixed scaffold: **specific behaviour → calibrated phrase → particular consequence**. Garner's Superb / Good / Acceptable / Needs Improvement / Poor scale gives you the calibrated phrase. The discipline is to anchor each phrase to a named behaviour:

> *"When we had several layoffs last June, Lauren remained utterly calm and collected while demonstrating keen sensitivity to those who lost their jobs. She [specific action]."*

Calibrated phrase + specific action protects the appraisal against both performative kindness ("she's wonderful") and performative criticism ("she struggles"). Seven aspects of work covered: attitude, efficiency, human relations, judgment, knowledge, reliability, communication skills.
