# Commercial formats

Covers: **white paper, market research report, market analysis, product description, case study, feasibility study**.

Audience is a buyer, investor, executive sponsor, or evaluator. Voice is authoritative-but-accessible. Citations are present but unobtrusive — tables, charts, and pull-quotes carry argumentative weight.

These formats exist to **support a commercial or strategic decision**.

---

## White paper

A long-form (6–25 pages, sometimes longer) authoritative document making an evidenced argument about a problem and a class of solutions, usually published by a vendor, association, or think-tank.

**Purpose.** Establish thought leadership; educate a buyer; support a sales cycle without being a brochure.

**Structure.**
1. Title and executive summary.
2. Problem framing — why this matters, with sized data.
3. Background / current state — landscape, gaps, prior approaches.
4. Argument — the position the paper takes, evidenced.
5. Implications / recommendations.
6. About the publisher and methodology note.
7. References / endnotes.

**Sub-types.**
- **Backgrounder** — explains a complex topic; lightest sales overlay.
- **Numbered list** — "Five ways to …"; entry point for newer readers.
- **Problem/solution** — most common B2B form; ends pointing at the publisher's offering.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person, no first-person plural sales language.
- Vendor pitch belongs at the end (or a sidebar), never in the body.
- Charts and tables carry argument; prose explains them.

**Common failure modes.**
- Reads like a brochure — instant credibility loss.
- Argument reverse-engineered from the product feature list.
- Statistics without sources — every chart needs a citation under it.

**Academic variant.** None natively; closest equivalent is a **working paper** at an academic institute — same length, more citations, no commercial intent.

**Non-academic variant.** The default vendor / industry-association white paper.

**References.**
- Stelzner, *Writing White Papers*, WhitePaperSource Publishing, 2007.
- Graham, *White Papers For Dummies*, Wiley, 2013.
- Kantor, *Crafting White Paper 2.0*, Lulu, 2009.
- Forrester / IDC / Gartner published white-paper templates (industry reference).
- IEEE / ACM technical white-paper guidelines (for technical white papers).

## Market research report

A sized-and-segmented industry survey — TAM/SAM/SOM, segmentation, growth drivers, competitive landscape, forecasts. Length 30–200 pages. Sold or distributed to buyers, investors, strategy teams.

**Purpose.** Provide the **decision-supporting data substrate** for market-entry, investment, or product decisions.

**Structure.**
1. Cover, table of contents, list of exhibits.
2. Executive summary.
3. Methodology — sources, definitions, time horizon, currency conversion, segmentation logic.
4. Market sizing — TAM, SAM, SOM, historical and forecast.
5. Segmentation — by product, geography, customer type, channel.
6. Drivers, restraints, opportunities, challenges.
7. Competitive landscape — vendor profiles, market share, strategy moves.
8. Trends and outlook.
9. Appendices — data tables, abbreviations, source list.

**Voice rules.**
- Neutral, third person, evidence-led.
- Every chart and forecast cites methodology and source.
- Forecasts are explicit about base year, model, and assumption sensitivity.

**Common failure modes.**
- Forecast magic — numbers without method.
- Segmentation that does not sum to total market.
- Vendor profiles written from corporate marketing rather than primary signals.

**Academic variant.** **Industry economics paper** — peer-reviewed, narrower question, full citations. Different beast; treat as a `paper` (see `academic-formats.md`).

**Non-academic variant.** The default. Gartner, IDC, Forrester, IBISWorld, Euromonitor, BMI, Statista, McKinsey-Global-Institute reports all sit here.

**References.**
- Smith & Albaum, *Fundamentals of Marketing Research*, SAGE, 2005.
- Hague, Hague & Morgan, *Market Research in Practice*, 4th ed., Kogan Page, 2022.
- Kotler & Keller, *Marketing Management*, 16th ed., Pearson, 2022. (Chapter on market analysis.)
- ESOMAR, *Global Market Research* annual report and methodology guidelines.
- Aaker, Kumar, Leone & Day, *Marketing Research*, 13th ed., Wiley, 2022.

## Market analysis

Shorter than a market research report (5–25 pages). Answers a **specific** strategic question: "Should we enter market X?" "Who are our top three competitors and how are they moving?"

**Structure.**
1. The question.
2. Snapshot — relevant segment sized and described.
3. Competitive picture — focused, not encyclopaedic.
4. Customer / demand picture.
5. Strategic implications — what this means for the asking party.
6. Recommendation.
7. Sources.

**Voice rules.**
- Decision-grade. Clear recommendation.
- Citations present but compressed — most live as endnotes or a source list.

**Common failure modes.**
- Drifts into a market-research report and loses the question.
- Recommendation that does not follow from the analysis.

**Academic variant.** None — this is inherently applied. Closest scholarly form is a **case-based industry paper**.

**Non-academic variant.** The default consulting / corp-dev / VC analysis.

**References.**
- Porter, *Competitive Strategy*, Free Press, 1980 (and *Competitive Advantage*, 1985).
- Grant, *Contemporary Strategy Analysis*, 11th ed., Wiley, 2021.
- Rumelt, *Good Strategy, Bad Strategy*, Crown Business, 2011.
- Magaziner & Patinkin, *The Silent War*, Random House, 1989. (Classic market-analysis case-craft.)
- McKinsey Quarterly methodology essays (open access selection).

## Product description

Marketing copy for a single product or SKU. Length 50–500 words. Lives on a website, packaging, catalogue, or shelf-talker.

**Structure.**
1. Hook / headline — value proposition in one line.
2. Body — features framed as benefits; the "what it does for you" line.
3. Specifications — bullets, table, or sidebar.
4. Social proof / certifications (where relevant).
5. Call to action.

**Voice rules.**
- Aimed at the buyer's decision context.
- Active voice; concrete nouns; verifiable claims only.
- Regulated categories (medical, food, financial, cosmetic) carry mandatory disclaimers and fact-checked claims.

**Common failure modes.**
- Listing features without translating to benefits.
- Unverifiable superlatives ("best in class") that trip advertising-standards rules.
- SEO keyword stuffing that breaks readability.

**Academic variant.** None. The closest scholarly form is a **product / artefact description** in a design or HCI paper, which sits inside an academic paper.

**Non-academic variant.** The default — e-commerce, B2B catalogue, retail packaging.

**References.**
- Sugarman, *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*, Wiley, 2007.
- Ogilvy, *Ogilvy on Advertising*, Vintage, 1985.
- Maslen, *Persuasive Copywriting*, 2nd ed., Kogan Page, 2019.
- Caples & Hahn, *Tested Advertising Methods*, 5th ed., Prentice Hall, 1998.
- Jurisdiction-specific consumer-protection / advertising-standards codes (UCC in Uganda, KEBS in Kenya, ASA in the UK, FTC in the US) — load before writing regulated copy.

## Case study

A focused narrative of a single subject (organisation, project, intervention, patient, locality) used to illustrate a pattern, evaluate an outcome, or transfer learning.

**Sub-types.**
- **Marketing case study** — customer success story, 1–4 pages, ends in measurable outcomes and a quote.
- **Business / Harvard-style teaching case** — 8–25 pages, presents a decision context without giving the answer; used in teaching.
- **Research case study** — academic single- or multi-case study using Yin / Stake methodology, embedded in a paper or thesis.
- **Clinical / legal case study** — structured narrative of a specific patient or matter.

**Structure (marketing variant).**
1. Customer profile.
2. Challenge.
3. Solution.
4. Outcomes — quantified.
5. Quote and call to action.

**Structure (research variant).**
1. Case selection rationale.
2. Context.
3. Data sources and triangulation.
4. Within-case analysis.
5. Cross-case analysis (multi-case).
6. Implications.

**Voice rules.**
- Marketing variant: warm, specific, quantified.
- Research variant: third person, methodologically explicit, generalisation claims tightly bounded.
- Both: real names only with consent; otherwise anonymise consistently.

**Common failure modes.**
- Marketing case studies with unverifiable outcome numbers.
- Research case studies that over-generalise from a single case.
- Confusing teaching cases with research cases (they have opposite goals).

**Academic variant.** Yin- or Stake-style **research case study** — embedded in a paper, thesis, or stand-alone monograph.

**Non-academic variant.** Marketing case study, teaching case, and clinical/legal case studies.

**References.**
- Yin, *Case Study Research and Applications: Design and Methods*, 6th ed., SAGE, 2018.
- Stake, *The Art of Case Study Research*, SAGE, 1995.
- Eisenhardt, "Building Theories from Case Study Research", *Academy of Management Review* 14(4), 1989.
- Ellet, *The Case Study Handbook*, Harvard Business Review Press, 2007. (For teaching cases.)
- Content Marketing Institute, *Case Study Roadmap* (open guidance — for marketing variant).

## Feasibility study

Structured assessment of whether a proposed project, investment, or intervention is viable. Length 20–150 pages depending on stakes.

**Structure.**
1. Executive summary.
2. Project description and objectives.
3. Market feasibility — demand, willingness to pay, competition.
4. Technical feasibility — design, engineering, technology, capacity.
5. Operational feasibility — staffing, processes, partners.
6. Legal and regulatory feasibility.
7. Financial feasibility — capex, opex, NPV, IRR, payback, sensitivity.
8. Environmental and social feasibility (ESIA where required).
9. Risk register.
10. Recommendation — go / no-go / go-conditional.
11. Appendices — financial model, technical drawings, source list.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person, neutral, evidence-led.
- The recommendation is explicit and conditional on stated assumptions.
- Sensitivity analysis is mandatory — single-point forecasts in a feasibility study are a tell.

**Common failure modes.**
- Confirmation-biased toward "go" because the sponsor wants it.
- Financial model that does not match the technical and operational chapters.
- Missing regulatory chapter — kills the project at the wrong stage.

**Academic variant.** Rare; closest is the **engineering / planning thesis chapter** that performs a feasibility assessment as part of a wider research project.

**Non-academic variant.** The default. Used in infrastructure, energy, real-estate, public-sector investment, and donor-funded development projects.

**References.**
- Behrens & Hawranek (UNIDO), *Manual for the Preparation of Industrial Feasibility Studies*, rev. ed., UNIDO, 1991. (The canonical reference; still widely used in development finance.)
- Thompson, *Feasibility Studies: Tools and Techniques*, Routledge, 2014.
- World Bank, *Project Appraisal Document* templates and *Reference Guide to Cost-Benefit Analysis*.
- IFC / Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency, environmental and social performance standards (for the ESIA chapter).
- Justis & Kreigsmann, "The Feasibility Study as a Tool for Venture Analysis", *Journal of Small Business Management* 17(1), 1979. (Origin of the modern entrepreneurship feasibility study.)
