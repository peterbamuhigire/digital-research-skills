# Academic formats

Covers: **essay, paper, thesis, dissertation, research proposal, literature review**.

These formats share an audience that accepts **citation, structured argument, hedged claims, and methodological transparency** as proof. Voice is third-person, formal, and cautious. Every claim is either cited, marked as inference, or marked as the author's own argument.

Default citation style: as prescribed by the institution / journal — most commonly APA 7, Chicago author-date, or OSCOLA (legal). Never invent a style; ask or default to the discipline's norm.

---

## Essay

A focused argument-driven prose piece, usually 800–5,000 words, with no formal section headings (or minimal headings). The reader is an examiner or a generally educated audience.

**Purpose.** Argue a thesis. Demonstrate reasoning, source-handling, and command of the topic.

**Structure.**
1. Introduction — context, scope, thesis statement, road-map of the argument.
2. Body — paragraphs each making one sub-claim, evidenced and analysed. Use signposts ("First", "By contrast", "However").
3. Conclusion — restates the thesis in light of the argument; does not introduce new evidence.
4. Reference list / bibliography.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person, no "I" (unless reflective essay variant explicitly invites it).
- Hedged: "suggests", "indicates", "appears to" — claims are calibrated to evidence.
- No bullet lists, no tables (unless the discipline allows them).

**Common failure modes.**
- Descriptive instead of argumentative — narrating the topic rather than defending a thesis.
- Quote-stuffing — paragraphs stitched from quotations with no analysis between them.
- Conclusion that introduces a new claim.

**Non-academic variant.** Long-form magazine essay (Harper's, Atlantic, n+1 register): named author voice, narrative scaffolding, fewer in-text citations, footnotes optional, looser structure. Same evidence discipline.

**References.**
- Bailey, *Academic Writing: A Handbook for International Students*, 5th ed., Routledge, 2018.
- Hewings & Hewings, *Grammar and Context*, Routledge.
- Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style*, 4th ed.
- University of Manchester *Academic Phrasebank* (online, free).

## Paper

A journal-shaped academic article making an original empirical or theoretical contribution. Length and structure are dictated by the target journal — usually 4,000–10,000 words.

**Purpose.** Add to scholarly knowledge. Pass peer review.

**Structure (IMRaD — empirical paper).**
1. Title, author(s), affiliations.
2. Abstract — 150–300 words, structured (background, aim, method, results, conclusion).
3. Keywords.
4. Introduction — gap and contribution.
5. Literature review (if not folded into introduction).
6. Methods — reproducible.
7. Results — without interpretation.
8. Discussion — interpretation, limitations, implications.
9. Conclusion.
10. References.
11. Supplementary materials / data availability statement.

**Theoretical / humanities papers.** Replace IMRaD with thematic sections; introduction → argument sections → conclusion. Method is the analytic frame.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person; hedged claims; explicit limitations.
- Distinguish between **what your data show** and **what you argue from them**.
- Use the discipline's preferred citation style consistently.

**Common failure modes.**
- Over-claiming — discussion section asserts more than the data support.
- Missing limitations section — peer reviewers will reject.
- Citation padding — listing sources you have not actually engaged with.

**Non-academic variant.** Industry "research note" or working paper: shorter, less hedged, fewer citations, but still IMRaD-shaped. Voice closer to a long-form analyst report.

**References.**
- Booth, Colomb, Williams, Bizup & FitzGerald, *The Craft of Research*, 4th ed., University of Chicago Press, 2016.
- Wallwork, *English for Writing Research Papers*, 2nd ed., Springer, 2016.
- Day & Gastel, *How to Write and Publish a Scientific Paper*, 8th ed., Cambridge UP, 2017.
- Belcher, *Writing Your Journal Article in Twelve Weeks*, 2nd ed., University of Chicago Press, 2019.

## Thesis

A master's-level multi-chapter research output, usually 15,000–40,000 words, supervised, examined.

**Structure (typical, varies by institution).**
1. Title page, declaration, acknowledgements, abstract, table of contents.
2. Chapter 1 — introduction (problem, aims, objectives, research questions, scope, structure).
3. Chapter 2 — literature review.
4. Chapter 3 — methodology.
5. Chapters 4–5 — findings.
6. Chapter 6 — discussion.
7. Chapter 7 — conclusion and recommendations.
8. References.
9. Appendices.

**Voice rules.** Same as paper, plus an explicit reflexivity section if methodology demands it (qualitative, action-research, ethnography).

**Common failure modes.**
- Chapter scope creep — literature review becomes a textbook.
- Methods chapter written as a list rather than a justified design.
- Findings chapter that interprets instead of reporting.

**Non-academic variant.** Rare. Equivalent is the **multi-part professional capstone** — same depth, less rigid structure, accepted by professional bodies (e.g., chartered-institute final projects).

**References.**
- Murray, *How to Write a Thesis*, 4th ed., Open University Press, 2017.
- Dunleavy, *Authoring a PhD*, Palgrave Macmillan, 2003. (Master's-applicable.)
- Bui, *How to Write a Master's Thesis*, 3rd ed., SAGE, 2019.
- Glatthorn & Joyner, *Writing the Winning Thesis or Dissertation*, 4th ed., Corwin, 2017.
- Local: Uganda / Kenya university handbook overrides anything above. Always load the home institution's thesis manual.

## Dissertation

Doctoral-level multi-chapter research output, 60,000–100,000 words, examined viva voce, claims an **original contribution to knowledge**.

Structure mirrors the thesis but expanded: longer literature review, deeper methodology justification, more findings chapters, an explicit "contribution to knowledge" section in the conclusion.

**Voice rules.**
- Same as thesis, with stronger originality and contribution claims.
- Defends positions against alternatives.
- Discusses implications for theory, methodology, and practice separately.

**Common failure modes.**
- Failure to articulate the contribution — examiners reject.
- Methodology chapter that describes methods without justifying their choice over alternatives.
- Conclusion that does not return to the research questions in Chapter 1.

**Non-academic variant.** None — this is the canonical doctoral form. Industry "PhD-by-publication" routes still produce a dissertation-shaped binder.

**References.**
- Dunleavy, *Authoring a PhD*, Palgrave Macmillan, 2003.
- Phillips & Pugh, *How to Get a PhD*, 6th ed., Open University Press, 2015.
- Kamler & Thomson, *Helping Doctoral Students Write*, 2nd ed., Routledge, 2014.
- Murray, *How to Write a Thesis*, 4th ed., Open University Press, 2017.
- Local handbook (Makerere, UCU, KNUST, UDSM, etc.) overrides general guidance on word count, format, and submission.

## Research proposal

Pre-research document seeking funding, ethics approval, or supervisor sign-off. Length 1,500–10,000 words depending on funder/institution.

**Structure.**
1. Title and applicant.
2. Background and problem statement.
3. Literature review (focused, gap-oriented).
4. Aims, objectives, research questions / hypotheses.
5. Methodology — design, sampling, instruments, analysis plan.
6. Ethics statement.
7. Work plan / Gantt chart.
8. Budget (for funder applications).
9. Expected contribution / impact.
10. References.

**Voice rules.**
- Future tense for the research itself ("the study will examine"); past tense for prior work.
- Confident but not over-claiming — funders penalise both timidity and hubris.
- Methodology must be **reproducible from the proposal alone**.

**Common failure modes.**
- Vague research questions ("explore the impact of …").
- Ethics treated as a tick-box rather than a design issue.
- Budget that does not match the methodology.

**Non-academic variant.** **Consulting / grant proposal** — replaces "contribution to knowledge" with "deliverables" and "value for money". Use `report-and-proposal-craft` for that variant.

**References.**
- Locke, Spirduso & Silverman, *Proposals That Work*, 6th ed., SAGE, 2013.
- Punch, *Developing Effective Research Proposals*, 3rd ed., SAGE, 2016.
- Denscombe, *Research Proposals: A Practical Guide*, 2nd ed., Open University Press, 2019.
- Funder-specific guides (NIH, Wellcome Trust, IDRC, AHRC) — always read the call before drafting.

## Literature review

Survey of existing scholarship on a defined question. Stands alone (a publication or chapter in its own right) or sits inside a thesis / paper / proposal.

**Types.**
- Narrative review — argument-led survey.
- Systematic review — protocol-driven, PRISMA-compliant, replicable.
- Scoping review — maps the field without pooling effects.
- Meta-analysis — quantitative pooling of effect sizes.

**Structure (narrative).**
1. Introduction — scope, question, criteria.
2. Themes / sections — organised by theme, theory, or chronology.
3. Synthesis — what is known, contested, missing.
4. Conclusion — implications for further research.
5. References.

**Structure (systematic).** PRISMA flow diagram, search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, quality appraisal, data extraction, synthesis, limitations.

**Voice rules.**
- The reviewer is a synthesiser, not a list-maker.
- Each cited source must do work — illustrate a claim, support a synthesis, or mark a gap.
- Avoid the "annotated bibliography" failure mode (paragraph per source with no cross-cutting argument).

**Common failure modes.**
- No defined question — review wanders.
- Source-by-source paragraphs instead of theme-by-theme synthesis.
- Missing inclusion/exclusion criteria in systematic reviews.

**Non-academic variant.** **Landscape scan / state-of-the-field memo** — same synthesis discipline, less rigid PRISMA scaffolding, faster turnaround. Used by think tanks and consultancies.

**References.**
- Hart, *Doing a Literature Review*, 2nd ed., SAGE, 2018.
- Booth, Sutton & Papaioannou, *Systematic Approaches to a Successful Literature Review*, 3rd ed., SAGE, 2021.
- Ridley, *The Literature Review: A Step-by-Step Guide for Students*, 2nd ed., SAGE, 2012.
- PRISMA 2020 statement (Page et al., *BMJ* 372:n71, 2021) — for systematic reviews.
