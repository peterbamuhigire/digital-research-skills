# Advocacy and public formats

Covers: **pamphlet, op-ed / commentary, policy brief**.

Audience is non-specialist or decision-making, not scholarly. Voice is named, plain, and persuasive. Citation is light but not optional — every fact still has a verifiable source, just not necessarily in the body text. Typography and layout do real work.

These formats exist to **change minds or trigger decisions**, not to add to knowledge.

---

## Pamphlet

Short, illustrated, mass-distributed persuasive document — 1 to 16 pages, often a folded leaflet, sometimes a tract-length booklet (up to ~24 pages).

**Purpose.** Inform and persuade a public audience to adopt a view, take an action, or attend an event.

**Structure (typical 6-panel tri-fold).**
1. Front panel — hook, image, organisation.
2. Inside panels — three or four short sections each headed by a question or claim.
3. Back panel — call to action, contact details, sources line.
4. A "for more" / "sources" block citing 3–8 anchor sources, often as a short bibliography or QR code to a source page.

**Voice rules.**
- Second person ("you") or first-person plural ("we") often acceptable.
- Short sentences; one idea per paragraph.
- Reading age usually targeted at 12–14 (Flesch-Kincaid).
- Image-text ratio is part of the design, not a bolt-on.

**Common failure modes.**
- Confusing pamphlet with brochure (brochure is a sales document; pamphlet argues).
- Burying the call to action.
- Stat-stuffing without a "sources" block — destroys credibility.

**Academic variant.** Rare but real: a **scholarly pamphlet** in the eighteenth-century sense — a polemical short essay published as a standalone booklet (think contemporary Verso Pamphlets or LRB pamphlet series). Voice is named, evidenced, but persuasive rather than peer-reviewed.

**Non-academic variant.** The default. Public-health, civic-education, advocacy-NGO, electoral, or marketing pamphlets.

**References.**
- Williams & Newton, *Visual Communication: Integrating Media, Art, and Science*, Routledge, 2007.
- *Plain Language: A Handbook for Writers in the U.S. Federal Government* (plainlanguage.gov).
- McCandless, *Knowledge is Beautiful*, William Collins, 2014. (Information-design grammar applicable to leaflets.)
- IFRC / WHO communications-toolkit pamphlet design guides (open access).
- Raymond, *Pamphlets and Pamphleteering in Early Modern Britain*, Cambridge UP, 2003. (Historical grounding for the "scholarly pamphlet" variant.)

## Op-ed / commentary

A 600–1,200 word commentary published in a newspaper, magazine, or platform under a named byline. Argues a single sharp position grounded in the author's expertise or experience.

**Purpose.** Influence public debate. Establish or reinforce the author's voice on the topic.

**Structure.**
1. Hook — news peg or vivid scene, 1–2 sentences.
2. Thesis — one clear sentence stating the position.
3. Argument — three or four short sections, each anchored on one piece of evidence.
4. Counter — acknowledge and rebut the strongest counter-argument.
5. Pivot — what should change as a result.
6. Sting — memorable closing line.

**Voice rules.**
- First person allowed and often expected ("As a researcher who has spent ten years in …").
- Plain, declarative sentences. Active voice.
- One argument per piece. Op-eds that try to make three arguments make none.
- Hyperlinks, not footnotes, for sources online; print versions cite inline.

**Common failure modes.**
- Academic essay smuggled into op-ed clothes — too hedged, no thesis up front.
- No news peg — editors reject.
- No counter-argument — looks one-sided to the median reader.

**Academic variant.** **Scholarly commentary** in journals like *Nature*, *BMJ*, *Foreign Affairs* — same argumentative shape but with full citations and longer length (1,500–3,000 words), still named-author voice. Distinct from a peer-reviewed paper.

**Non-academic variant.** The default newspaper / platform op-ed. Substack and LinkedIn long-form posts are an op-ed sub-variant.

**References.**
- Op-Ed Project, *How to Write an Op-Ed* (theopedproject.org — open guide).
- Stoner & Perkins, *Making Sense of Messages*, 2nd ed., Routledge, 2015. (Argumentation for public audiences.)
- Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style*, 4th ed.
- Hartley, *Communicating Ideas: The Politics of Scholarly Publishing*, Routledge, 2008. (For scholarly commentary variant.)
- *The New York Times Op-Ed Guide for Contributors* (live online guide).

## Policy brief

A 2–6 page (sometimes up to 8) document presenting a recommendation to a decision-maker on a specific policy choice.

**Purpose.** Move a named decision-maker (minister, parliamentary committee, head of agency) toward a specific course of action.

**Structure.**
1. Title — names the policy question.
2. Executive summary / key messages — three to five bullet points, must work standalone.
3. Problem statement — why the issue matters now, with the strongest two or three statistics.
4. Policy options — usually three: status quo, intermediate, recommended.
5. Recommendation — explicit, with implementation pathway.
6. Risks / trade-offs.
7. Sources / further reading.

**Voice rules.**
- Third person, formal, but plain.
- No academic hedging in the recommendation — decision-makers need a clear "do this".
- Evidence is summoned in service of the recommendation, not for its own sake.
- Visual design matters: pull-quotes, sidebars, infographics are standard.

**Common failure modes.**
- Burying the recommendation past page two.
- Listing options without ranking them.
- Using academic citation density — readers skip the brief.
- No implementation pathway — the recommendation is rhetorical.

**Academic variant.** **Evidence brief** produced by university-affiliated policy centres (IDS, ODI, Brookings working briefs). Same shape, denser citation, longer (up to ~12 pages), peer-reviewed in some series.

**Non-academic variant.** The default ministerial / NGO / think-tank policy brief. Length 2–6 pages.

**References.**
- Young & Quinn, *Writing Effective Public Policy Papers*, Open Society Institute, 2002 (open access).
- DeMarco & Tufts, *The Mechanics of Writing a Policy Brief*, *Nursing Outlook* 62(3), 2014.
- ODI, *Writing Policy Papers* (Research and Policy in Development Programme — open guidance series).
- IDRC, *How to Write a Policy Brief* (toolkit, open access).
- Tsai, *Communicating Research for Policy Change* (open access course materials).
