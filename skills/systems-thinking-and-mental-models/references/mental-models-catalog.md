# Mental-models catalog

**Date written:** 2026-04-27
**Drawn from:** *Thinking in Systems and Mental Models* (anonymous primer), Part 2 (Chapters 1–8 in particular: What Are Mental Models?, 40 Mental Models, Breaking Down Barriers, Focusing on Process Not Outcome, Mistakes of Thinking, Individual and Collective Mental Models).

This catalog gives the analyst a checklist of frames to apply *before* committing to an analysis. The discipline: instead of asking "what do I think?", ask "which of these frames should I have used?" — then re-examine the problem.

A mental model is **not** a personality test or a self-help slogan. It is a structured way of looking at a problem that has earned its keep across many domains.

## How to use the catalog

1. State the problem in one sentence.
2. Walk the catalog. For each model, ask: *would looking at this problem through this lens change my answer?*
3. Where the answer is yes, work the model fully — not as a slogan, but as a discipline.
4. Where two models give different answers, treat the conflict as the most important finding.
5. Record which models were applied and what each produced. The audit trail matters as much as the conclusion.

## Core thinking models

### First principles

Reduce the problem to its irreducible truths and reason up from those, rather than from analogy or convention.
*Apply when: the consensus answer feels stuck; everyone is reasoning from the same analogy.*

### Inversion

Solve the problem by considering the negative — what would *guarantee failure*? Then avoid those things.
*Apply when: paths to success are unclear but failure modes are visible.*

### Second-order effects

For every proposed action, ask: "and then what?" — at least twice. The first-order effect is intended; the second- and third-order effects are usually not.
*Apply when: an intervention is being designed.*

### Opportunity cost

Every commitment is also a refusal to do everything else. The true cost of doing X is the best thing not done.
*Apply when: resources or attention are scarce; "free" or "low-cost" claims are being made.*

### Base rates

Before estimating the probability of *this* case, ask what the rate is in the *reference class* of similar cases. Specifics rarely beat the base rate by much.
*Apply when: a forecast or judgement is being made; there is a temptation to treat the case as unique.*

### Regression to the mean

Extreme outcomes tend to be followed by less extreme ones — not because of any intervention, but because the extreme contained luck. Beware crediting interventions whose timing coincided with a regression.
*Apply when: an intervention "worked" or "failed" right after an extreme event.*

### Signal vs noise

Not every variation is meaningful. Distinguish the underlying signal from the noise around it. Small samples are mostly noise.
*Apply when: a recent change is being treated as a trend; a single quarter's data is being extrapolated.*

### Margin of safety

Build buffers against the unknown. A plan that only works if everything goes right is not a plan, it's a hope.
*Apply when: estimating timelines, costs, capacity, or risk tolerance.*

## Decision-quality models

### Process vs outcome

A good decision can produce a bad outcome (variance), and a bad decision can produce a good outcome (luck). Judge the decision on the *process and information available at the time*, not on the result.
*Apply when: reviewing past decisions; designing decision-review processes.*

### Premortem

Before committing, imagine the project has failed catastrophically. Walk back from that failure to identify what went wrong. Patch the obvious causes before launch.
*Apply when: a major commitment is about to be made.*

### Confidence calibration

Distinguish the strength of belief from the evidence. State probabilities. Track them. Update when wrong.
*Apply when: any forecast or estimative judgement is published.*

### Reversibility

How reversible is this decision? Reversible decisions can be made faster and with less analysis; irreversible ones deserve disproportionate scrutiny.
*Apply when: triaging decisions for depth of analysis.*

## Reasoning-discipline models

### Steelmanning (vs strawmanning)

Argue against the *strongest* version of the opposing position, not a caricature. If you can't, you don't yet understand the position.
*Apply when: dismissing a counter-argument feels easy.*

### Hanlon's razor

Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by carelessness, incompetence, or system design.
*Apply when: assigning intent to actors.*

### Occam's razor

Among competing explanations, prefer the one with the fewest unsupported assumptions. (Note: the simplest explanation is often right; it is not always right.)
*Apply when: a complex theory is being preferred over a simple one without evidence.*

### Map vs territory

The model is not the system. A useful map omits things; the omissions become problems when the territory shifts.
*Apply when: an analysis based on a model is being treated as the system itself.*

### Confirmation bias check

Did I look for evidence that would *change my mind*, or only for evidence that supports my position?
*Apply when: a position has crystallised quickly.*

### Survivorship bias

Looking at the survivors and ignoring the failures produces systematic over-confidence in patterns. Where are the cases that didn't make it?
*Apply when: drawing lessons from successful cases.*

## Strategic / structural models

### Circle of competence

There is a domain in which one's judgement is reliable; outside it, judgement is closer to a guess. Know the boundary.
*Apply when: tempted to opine outside the analyst's actual expertise.*

### Asymmetric upside / downside

Most decisions are not symmetric. A small loss vs a large gain is different from the reverse. Decisions with capped downside and uncapped upside are the prize.
*Apply when: comparing options with different risk profiles.*

### Lindy effect

The longer something has lasted, the longer it can be expected to keep lasting (for non-perishable cultural / institutional things). Old institutions outlast new ones.
*Apply when: forecasting whether a recent disruption will stick.*

### Path dependence

Where the system is *now* is partly explained by where it has *been* — initial conditions and lock-in shape current options.
*Apply when: explaining why a "obvious" reform hasn't happened.*

### Goodhart's Law

When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Behaviour optimises for the metric, often at the expense of what the metric was meant to track.
*Apply when: a KPI or indicator is driving behaviour.*

## Knowledge and learning models

### Strong opinions, loosely held

Hold positions strongly enough to act, but loosely enough to update when contradicted by evidence.
*Apply when: avoiding either over-tentative analysis or unfalsifiable certainty.*

### Beginner's mind

Approach a familiar problem as if seeing it for the first time. Domain expertise can occlude obvious questions.
*Apply when: returning to a problem the analyst has worked on before.*

### Spaced repetition / elaborative interrogation

Knowledge is consolidated by spaced retrieval and by being asked *why?* — not by re-reading.
*Apply when: building enduring expertise on a topic.*

## Individual vs collective mental models (primer, Chapter 7)

The primer makes a distinction worth keeping:

- **Individual mental models** — the analyst's own frames; explicit and editable
- **Collective mental models** — the frames embedded in an organisation, community, or discipline; often invisible and harder to edit

Engine-relevant point: collective mental models drive the structure level of the Iceberg (`causal-loops-and-leverage-points.md`). When systemic change isn't happening despite obvious need, the collective model is usually the culprit. Naming it is the first leverage move.

## Anti-patterns

- Listing mental models without applying any of them to the specific problem
- Treating a mental model as a verdict rather than a lens
- Picking the model that gives the answer the analyst already wanted
- Conflating "I know this model" with "I have used this model on this problem"
- Skipping the catalog walk when stakes are high
- Reaching for first principles when a base-rate check would do
- Using inversion to dismiss reasonable arguments instead of stress-testing the position
